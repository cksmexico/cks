Scenario: Management of poisoning or overdose

From birth onwards.

How should I assess a person who is suspected of being poisoned or taking an overdose?

  • Most people should be seen (in person) after a poisoning episode or overdose.
    • Advice may be offered over the telephone (without seeing the person) if the healthcare professional feels confident that the poisoning episode is not serious. For sources of information regarding the management of poisoning, see Information sources.

Emergency management of poisoning or overdose

  • If the person has collapsed and is unresponsiveensure the airway is open (in the absence of trauma) and check for normal breathing. If they are unresponsive and not breathing normally, start cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) immediately, and call for an ambulance.
  • Assessment of a person who is acutely poisoned involves assessing their level of consciousness, breathingand circulation; taking a history and performing an examination.
    • Assess the person's consciousness. The Glasgow coma scale is the method most commonly used.
    • Measure respiratory rate. If a pulse oximeter is available, measure oxygen saturations. Note this may not be accurate if there is poor peripheral perfusion, or carboxyhaemoglobin or methaemoglobin.
    • Measure the person's pulse, blood pressure and temperature.
  • Arrange emergency transfer to hospital following the immediate assessment of the person if:
    • The person is unconscious or has a reduced level of consciousness.
    • If there is a reduced respiratory rateor oxygen saturation is reduced.
    • There is hypotension.
    • If there is tachycardia or bradycardia or an irregular pulse.
    • Ifthere is hypothermia or hyperthermia
    • The person has had a seizure
    • If there are any other concerning clinical features.

Assessment of a person who has been poisoned or taken an overdose

  • Take a history toassess the exact circumstances of the poisoning or overdose. Information from friends, family members, or other health professionals, if available, can be invaluable. Determine:
    • Whywas the substance taken? Was it accidental, deliberate or a therapeutic error? Is there a risk of repetition? People who have self-harmed will need a further psychosocial assessment by another trained specialist (mental health worker, social worker) after the treatment of their physical problems.
    • Whowas involved? Ascertain the age, weight, and sex of the person, and ask about their past medical history, medication and factors that will affect the excretion or metabolism of the poison, such as renal or hepatic impairment.
    • Whatwas taken? Ask about the amount taken, other medicines, and alcohol use. Note that the person's account of what and how much has been taken should be treated with clinical suspicion. This can result in inconsistency between an unreliable history and laboratory blood and urine tests.
    • Whenwas it taken?Try and establishthe exact timing(s) of ingestion or contact.This is especially important in paracetamol poisoning. Wasit a single acute overdose, staggered or chronic?
    • What was theroute of administration? By mouth, inhalation, or injection?
  • Ask about any symptoms the person may be experiencing, depending upon the substance(s) involved. SeeSymptoms and signs of drugs that are commonly involved in poisoning or overdose.
  • Perform an examination, depending on the clinical situation. In addition to the assessment of consciousness, breathingand circulation, this may include:
    • General observations. Alcohol or solvents may be smelt on the breath. Atypical bruising may be a sign of violence. There may be needle track marks from injection of drugs of misuse.Skin blisters may be found in people who are, or have been, unconscious due to poisoning.
    • Neurological examination and examination of the pupils and eye movements.
    • Checking for signs that are consistent with the poison or drug that has been taken. See Symptoms and signs of drugs that are commonly involved in poisoning or overdose.
    • If the poison is not known, compare the person's symptoms and examination findings to those commonly associated with known poisons, see toxidromes in Symptoms and signs of drugs that are commonly involved in poisoning or overdose.
  • If the person has deliberately self-poisoned:
    • Assess the person's risk. This includes physical risks (see above); the risk of psychological harm and the risk of further self-harm or suicide; and any safeguarding concerns.
    • Assess the risk of psychological harm and the risk of further self-harm or suicide. Explore the person's feelings and mental state. Key psychological characteristics associated with risk include hopelessness, depression and continuing suicidal intent.
    • Assess for any safeguarding concerns in children, young people or vulnerable adults. This concerns those who are dependent for their care on the person presenting with self-harm, and people from these groups who present with an episode of self-harm that may have occurred in response to child maltreatment, domestic violence or exploitation. For further information, see the CKS topic on Child maltreatment.
    • Where possible, the person should be seen alone for this assessment, to maintain confidentiality and allow the person to discuss any problems between themselves and friends or family.
    • Communicate the outcome of these assessments to other healthcare professionals who are involved in the person's care.
    • For more information about initial risk assessment, see the CKS topic on Self-harm.
  • Investigations are not routinely necessary or appropriate in primary care for those people who do not need emergency referral to hospital.
  • Consultthe poisons information database, TOXBASE, for information on the specific poison(s) involved, and manage accordingly. See Information sources.
  • Telephone the NPIS for advice:
    • For severe or complex cases, including multiple ingestions.
    • For people with significant co-morbidity.
    • If there are any uncertainties regarding the person's management after consulting TOXBASEonline.
    • If the poison is unknown.
  • Telephone theUK Teratology Information Service for advice if the person is pregnant. See Information sources.

Basis for recommendation

  • The recommendation that most people should be seen in person following a poisoning episode or overdose is based on what CKS considers to be best practice. Clinicians are advised to access TOXBASE for information regarding the management of poisoning, and if there are any uncertainties, to telephone the NPIS for advice.
Emergency management of poisoning
  • The recommendation to start CPR and call for an ambulance if the person is unconscious and is not breathing properly is based on what CKS considers to be best practice. The recommendations regarding the emergency assessment of someone who has been poisoned or taken an overdose are extrapolated from a review article on the hospital assessment and diagnosis of poisoning [Vale, 2016b]and the BNF [BNF 73, 2017]. The recommendation regarding clinical situations which require emergency transfer to hospital are based on what CKS considers best practice and are extrapolated from the BNF [BNF 73, 2017]. The BNF recommends both hypothermia and hyperthermia require urgent transfer to hospital. For amphetamine overdose, a person whose temperature is >38C requires urgent active cooling measures [BMJ Best Practice, 2015]. Atemperature over 39.5C is a sign of potentially life-threatening amphetamine toxicity. Opioid overdose should be considered in any personwho presents with altered mental state andbradypnoea and miosis [BMJ, 2016c].
Assessment of a person who has been poisoned or taken an overdose
  • The recommendations regarding what to include in the history are based on expert opinion in a review articles [Anderson, 2013; Vale, 2016b],from the TOXBASE website, www.toxbase.org,and what CKS considers to be best practice.Note that the person's account of what and how much has been taken should be treated with clinical suspicion [Vale, 2016b]. This can result ininconsistencybetween an unreliable history andthe results of laboratory blood and urine tests. It is important to try and establish which medicines were actually involved. People often do not use generic pharmaceutical names. As self-poisoning is often impulsive, the person may take tablets from the first container or pack they find, and so the medicine may be another person's prescription. Many people will not know the exact number of tablets they have taken. In this situation, it can be helpful to ask about thenumber of strips or packets, handfuls or mouthfuls. In paracetamol overdose, a staggered overdose is defined as doses taken over more than one hour [BMJ, 2017].For episodes of poisoning in children, it may be difficult to obtain aclear history from the child or parent of what has happened. It is difficult to quantify the amount taken, as the number of tablets in the container before the episode may not be known.In cases where it is uncertain which poison has been taken, it is very important to ask the family about possible toxic substances that may be in the home [Anderson, 2013].
  • The recommendations regarding what to include in the examination are based on expert opinion in a review article [Vale, 2016b]and the [BNF 73, 2017]. In cases where the person is unaware of the substances taken or isunable or unwilling to give this information,the poisons involved can sometimes be inferred by comparing the person's symptoms and examination findings to features commonly associated with known poisons (toxidromes) [NPIS, 2012]. However, this is a guide. The symptoms and signs listed can be caused by other poisons. Aperson may not show all the features listed for one poison and may have unlisted symptoms or signs.
Initial risk assessment

The recommendations about performing an initial risk assessment for any person who has self-harmed are based on expert opinion in the National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence (NICE) guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. For further information, see the CKS topic on Self-harm.

  • It is recommended that people are assessed alone initially to maintain confidentiality and to allow discussion of issues that may relate to the relationship between the person and their family, carers, or significant others that may have contributed to their thoughts of self-harm.
  • Each time a person presents following an act of self-harm it should be treated as an independent episode, as the factors contributing to self-harm behaviour can be different on each occasion. Failure to assess each episode in its own right may result in appropriate help not being offered at a time when a person is at high risk of suicide.
  • People who have self-harmed should be offered treatment for any physical consequences, even if they are unwilling to accept psychosocial assessment for psychiatric treatment.
  • The recommendation to assess a person who has self-harmed for safeguarding issues is based on expert opinion in the National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence (NICE) guideline The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004], which advises that healthcare professionals should always consider during initial assessment and treatment whether there are safeguarding concerns for vulnerable adults and children that require local safeguarding procedures to be followed. The Royal College of Psychiatrists report on Managing self-harm in young people [Royal College of Psychiatrists, 2014a]also states that children who have recently taken an overdose may be living in households where the adults cannot provide for their basic needs; or may have been abused or exploited. This recommends prompt social services assessment.
Investigations
  • The recommendation that investigations are not routinely required in primary care is based on what CKS considers to be best practice. People requiring further investigations should be admitted to hospital for further assessment.
Sources of Information
  • The recommendations regarding the situations in which a healthcare practitioner should telephone the NPIS for advice are based on information on the TOXBASE website http://www.toxbase.organd the NPIS annual report [NPIS, 2016]. The recommendation to telephone the UKTIS for advice if the person is pregnant is based on information on the TOXBASE website http://www.toxbase.org.

What sources of information should I use to guide the management of a person who has been poisoned or taken an overdose?

Seek advice from, or contact:

  • TOXBASEin the first instance for general information (seewww.toxbase.org).
    • TOXBASE is a clinically-based toxicology database and the first-line source of information for healthcare professionals. It is regularly updated by the National Poisons Information Service (NPIS). It gives information about the diagnosis, treatment and management of patients exposed to drugs, household products, and industrial and agricultural chemicals.
    • It is available through its online database, TOXBASE and the TOXBASE app for iOS and Android mobile devices. These are free to all UK NHS healthcare units and healthcare professionals.
    • A back-up site is available at www.toxbasebackup.org if the main site cannot be accessed.
  • The Public Health England and NPIS information leaflet for healthcare professionals on low toxicity substances [Public Health England, 2015].
  • The National Poisons Information Service (NPIS) on 0344 892 0111, if there are any uncertainties regarding the person's management after accessing TOXBASE, and for:
    • Severe or complex cases, including multiple ingestions.
    • People with significant co-morbidity.
    • Cases where the poison is unknown.
  • The NPIS advice line gives information and advice to NHS staff to assist them in deciding whether a person needs admission or can be managed at home. It also provides advice onthe hospital management of people requiring admission.
    • The NPIS is a network of units commissioned by Public Health England for the UK health departments
    • The service is available 24 hours a day via the central UK number 0344 892 0111. Advice is provided by information scientists. Consultant clinical toxicologists provide advice regarding more serious or complex cases of poisoning.
    • The service allows unnecessary hospital attendances for people at low risk of harm to be reduced, and improved quality of care for those who require hospital treatment.
  • In Northern Ireland, the Regional Medicines and Poison Information Service. Thisprovides information during working hours. The NPIS takes out-of-hours enquiries.
  • UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS)in the event of poisoning in a pregnant woman. See http://www.toxbase.org, www.uktis.org, or for telephone advice 0344 892 0909.
    • Information for healthcare professionals can be accessed online through TOXBASE, which contains the review documents produced by UKTIS on maternal exposures to drugs and chemicals, online through the UKTIS website, and by telephone.
    • The UK Teratology Information Service is part of the NPIS and provides a nationalservice onthe safety of drugs and chemicals during pregnancy.
    • Patient information leaflets are available through it's best use of medicines in pregnancy website http://www.medicinesinpregnancy.org.
  • UK Medicines Information (UKMI) servicefor general medicine information including dosing and drug interactions (seewww.ukmi.nhs.uk).
    • The UK Medicines Information service has 14 regional medicine information centres that can deal with enquiries regarding drug administration, dosing, and drug interactions. See the websitewww.ukmi.nhs.ukfor a list of regional contact telephone numbers.
    • These centres can provide practical advice on when to restart drugs (if the person has taken additional doses accidentally), possible adverse effects, and can also direct healthcare professionals to further information.

When should I admit a person to hospital after an episode of poisoning or overdose?

  • Consult TOXBASEfor advice regarding the toxicity of a substance and whether the person requires admission. Telephonethe NPIS for advice ifthere areany uncertainties following this. See Information sourcesfor furtherdetails. Advice can also be obtained from the consultant on call in Accident and Emergency. Arrange urgent hospital admission if this is advised.
  • Refer all children and young people (aged under 18) who have deliberately self-poisoned urgently to accident and emergency.
  • Refer adults who have deliberately self-poisoned urgentlyto Accident and Emergency.
    • If available, send a sampleof the suspected drug(s) or poison into hospital with them.
    • Arrange for the person to be accompaniedby an appropriate person, particularly if there is a risk of further self-harm, reluctance of the person to attend, or the person is very distressed.
    • Arrange follow-up within 48hours of discharge from hospitalto ensure ongoing management of the person's psychosocial needs and risk of further self-harm or suicide, depending on clinical judgement.
  • Refer people who are symptomatic after taking or being exposed to a poison or drug urgently to hospital.
    • Minor gastrointestinal upset alone is generally not considered an absolute indication for admission to hospital in people who have accidentally taken a small amount of a substance considered to be of low toxicity. See the Public Health England and NPIS leaflet for healthcare professionals on Low toxicity substances[Public Health England, 2015]for further information.
  • Refer people who have taken poisonswith delayed action urgently to hospital, even if the person appears well and is asymptomatic.
  • Delayed-action medicines include aspirin, iron, paracetamol, tricyclic antidepressants, co-phenotrope (diphenoxylate with atropine, Lomotil) and all modified-release preparations.
  • Note salicylates may be present in some over the counter antacid preparations such as Pepto-Bismol. Always consult TOXBASEfor advice regarding the toxicity of a substance.
  • Refer all people who have taken or been exposed to a drug that is unknown urgently to hospital.
  • Refer people urgently to hospital if this is recommended by theBNF, an information source consulted or the consultant on call in Accident and Emergency.
  • Consider admitting children, pregnant women, people with pre-existing comorbidities, or people taking concomitant medication, even if the drug is considered to have a good safety profile.
  • In areas that are a significant distance away from an emergency department, and where there will likely be a delay in reaching hospital,discuss the person's management with the consultant on-call in Accident and Emergency.
    • Consider starting the person's assessment and treatment in primary care, for example taking blood samples for paracetamol level, as per TOXBASE guidance.
  • Consider child neglect if there are repeated presentations of accidental ingestion of poisons in a child. For more information, see the CKS topic on Child maltreatment - recognition and management.

Basis for recommendation

  • The recommendation to consult TOXBASE, telephone the NPIS or discuss the personwith the consultant on call in Accident and Emergency, when deciding whether to admit a person with poisoningis based on expert opinion in the NPIS annual report [NPIS, 2016], the TOXBASE website http://www.toxbase.org, the BNF [BNF 73, 2017]and theNational Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence (NICE) guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care[National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004].
  • The recommendation to refer all children and young people who have deliberately self-poisoned urgently to accident and emergency is based on expert opinion in the Royal College of Psychiatrist's report,Managing self-harm in young people[Royal College of Psychiatrists, 2014a]. This recommends a cautious approach, as it is difficult to work out the risk after takinga substance, and it is important to have a thorough evaluation of physical effects and mental health risks. Emergency physical assessment and treatment,initial mental health risk assessment and initial assessment of safeguardingshould be performed in the emergency department. NICE and the Royal College of Psychiatristsrecommend children and young people under the age of 16 should be admitted overnight following thisto allow comprehensive mental health assessment to be performed by appropriately trained professionals [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004; Royal College of Psychiatrists, 2014a]. The Royal College of Psychiatrists report recommends thatyoung people aged 16 or 17 do not always need to be admitted after being seen in Accident and Emergency, ifthere has been a comprehensive mental health assessment and a managementplan formulated, and discharge is considered safe. If there are any doubts or concerns, the young person should be admitted overnight.
  • The recommendation that adults who have self-poisoned should be referred urgently to Accident and Emergencyis based on expert opinion in the National Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence (NICE) guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. People who have deliberatelyself-poisoned should be referred urgently to the nearest accident and emergency department, as the person may not know or may purposefully underestimate the nature and quantity of substances taken, which can make an accurate risk assessment in primary care difficult. CKS recommends a cautious approach in the management of self-poisoning and recommends referral to accident and emergency for physical and psychological assessment. TOXBASErecommends all people who have taken a deliberate overdose, or been exposed to a product as the result of self-harm should be referred to hospital http://www.toxbase.org.
  • Older people should be presumed to have suicidal intent until proven otherwise. This is based on evidence from a narrative review by NICE. This review identified seven studies that found older people have high suicidal intent and high completed suicide rates compared with the general population [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2011].
  • Rapid transfer to hospital is advisable, as for manydrugs taken in overdose, treatment with activated charcoal as soon as possible (preferably within one hour of ingestion), can prevent or reduce absorption of the drug [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004].
  • People who have deliberately self-poisoned should have a full psychosocial assessment of their needs and risk by a specialist mental health practitioner while in hospital [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004; Hawton, 2016]. The risks of repetition of self-harmand of suicide following deliberate self-poisoning are high [Hawton, 2016].Psychosocial assessmentinvolves evaluatingthe social, psychological and motivational factors surroundingthe episode of self-harm, feelings of hopelessness, present suicidal intent, and a mental health and social needs assessment [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. The person's subsequent management should be based upon the assessment of needs and risk. People with a serious psychiatric disorder and/or at high risk of suicide are likely to need inpatient treatment [Hawton, 2016]. Temporary admission may be needed if the person is very distressed, or if it is difficult to perform a psychosocial assessment, for example due to intoxication, or if the home environment is unsafe [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004].
  • The recommendation to admit people who are symptomatic is based on expert opinion inthe BNF[BNF 73, 2017]whichstates people with features of poisoning should generally be admitted.
  • The recommendation to refer all people who have taken medicines with delayed action urgently to hospital, even if the person is asymptomatic is based on expert opinion in the BNF [BNF 73, 2017].Delayed-action medicines include aspirin, iron, paracetamol, tricyclic antidepressants, co-phenotrope (diphenoxylate with atropine, Lomotil) and all modified-release preparations.
  • It is very important to access TOXBASEfor advice regarding the toxicity of a substance.Some over the counter antacid preparations may contain salicylates, such as Pepto-Bismol [BMJ Best Practice, 2016]. Ingestion of topical liniments containing oil of wintergreen carries a high risk of salicylate poisoning. Toxbase advises all patients who have ingested any amount of this product should be referred to an Emergency Department http://www.toxbase.org.
  • The recommendation to admit all people who have taken or been exposed to an unknown poison is based on what CKS considers best clinical practice.This allows the person to be monitored and any symptoms or complications to be appropriately managed. The NPIS advises that cases where the poison is unknown should be discussed via the NPIS advice line http://www.toxbase.org.
  • The recommendation to consider admitting children, pregnant women, people with pre-existing comorbidities, or people taking concomitant medication, even if the drug is considered to have a good safety profile is based on what CKS considers best practice. These groups are more vulnerable to toxic effects of drugs, even those that have a good safety profile. Similarly, there is a risk of dangerous drug interactions in people who are taking concomitant medication.
  • The recommendation to discuss the person's management with the on-call consultant in Accident and Emergency in remote areas where there is likely to be a delay in arriving at hospital is based on heNational Institute for Health and Clinical Excellence (NICE) guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. In these cases, it is important to consider whether assessment or treatment should be started in primary care.
  • The recommendation to consider child neglect if there are repeated presentations of accidental ingestion of poisons in a child is based on expert opinion in a review article [BMJ, 2016a]. For more information, see the CKS topic on Child maltreatment - recognition and management.

How do I manage a person who does not need admission to hospital?

  • Refer people who have ingested or been exposed to a substance as a result of self-harm to hospital for assessment, even if the poison is considered to be of low toxicity. For full information, see When should I admit a person to hospital after an episode of poisoning or overdose?.
  • Hospital assessment is not usually indicated for accidental poisoning with substances considered to be of low toxicity.
    • For a list of substances that are considered to be of low toxicity when ingested in a small amount, see the Public Health England and NPIS leaflet, Low toxicity substances[Public Health England, 2015].
    • If a small amount of one of these substances has been taken, advise the person that theymay experience mild gastrointestinal upset. If this occurs, advise the person to drink a small glass of water, milk or juice.
    • Access the substance on TOXBASE, and use this as a guide to the person's management. Advise the person onwhen to seek medical advice, for example if the gastrointestinal upset does not improve, becomes worse, or new symptoms develop.
    • If a considerable amount has been ingested, or the person has symptoms other than mild gastrointestinal upset, seek advice from TOXBASE. Contactthe NPIS if there are uncertainties after accessing TOXBASE. Refer the person to hospital if appropriate.
  • Hospital assessment is not usually indicatedif a person has accidentally taken an additional tablet of their own medication. Consider:
    • Theindications, adverse effects and interactions of the medicine. Consult the BNF for information regarding these, and on maximum daily doses.
    • The person's past medical history (especially renal and liver impairment) and medication history.
    • The person's age and social circumstances.
  • Refer to the BNF or TOXBASEfor information regarding individual medicines and the person's subsequent management. Seek advice where appropriate, for example a discussionwith the medical consultant on call, or contact the UK Medicines Information (UKMI) service.
  • Restart the person on their medication when appropriate.
    • Restarting medication may be complicated depending on the drug and dosing regimen.CKS cannot provide advice for individual drugs taken, due to the number of variables that must be considered.
    • If there is any uncertainty regarding when to restart medication after an additional tablet has been taken, further advice should be sought, seeWhat sources of information should I use to guide the management of a person who has been poisoned or taken an overdose?.
    • Consider if supervision of medication or a dosette box is needed (for example, if poor memory or confusion was the cause of the overdose).

Basis for recommendation

  • The recommendations regarding the management of someone who has accidentally ingested a substance that is considered to be of low toxicity is based on the Public Health England and NPISleaflet,Low toxicity substances,[Public Health England, 2015], advice on the TOXBASEwebsite, https://www.toxbase.org, and what CKS considers to be best clinical practice.
  • The recommendation that admission to hospital is not usually indicatedif a person has accidentally taken an additional tablet of their own medication, and to refer to the BNF or TOXBASE, or seek advice from a medical consultant, the NPIS or the UK Medicines Information (UKMI) service, when deciding the person's subsequent management, is based on what CKS considers best medical practice.
  • The recommendations regarding the management of a person who has accidentally taken an additional dose of their medication pragmatic and based on what CKS considers to be best medical practice.CKS cannot provide advice for individual drugs taken, due to the number of variables that must be considered. CKS advises in all cases to perform a full assessment, refer to the BNF and seek further advice where appropriate.

How do I manage a person who refuses admission to hospital after a poisoning?

  • Refer people who have deliberately self-poisoned urgentlyto the nearest emergency department. See When to admitfor further information.
  • If a person refuses to go to hospital, determine the reason(s) why. Provide information about the potential consequences of not receiving hospital treatment and assessment.
  • If the person still refuses admission, and this is felt to be in their best interests,assess their capacityand consider whether a compulsory admission under The Mental Health Act, 1983is appropriate.
    • Assessing mental capacity helps to decide whether the person is able to make an informed decision about whether to accept or refuse admission and treatment.
    • If the person allows, gather additional information from family and carers.
    • If a person is judged to have diminished capacity and/or the presence of a significant mental health disorder, refer for urgent hospital admission or urgent mental health assessment, depending on clinical judgement. Healthcare professionals have a duty to act in the person's best interests. This may include taking the person to hospital for further assessment and treatment against their wishes. Document and justify the decision that the person does not have mental capacity.
    • If there is uncertainty about whether a person has capacity to make informed decisions, seek advice from a psychiatrist who has experience in assessing capacity. For more information, seeHow do I assess a person's capacity?
    • The Mental Health Act will allow treatment for the mental health problems but will not allow treatment of the physical disorder (poisoning) unless that can be considered to be either a symptom or consequence of the person's mental disorder.
    • If considering using the Mental Health Act, always seek help from the local community mental health team (CMHT) or crisis team, social services, and the on-call psychiatrist.Section136 may be used by police to take people who appear to be suffering from a mental disorder and arein 'immediate need of care or control'from a public place to a place of safetyand enable examination by a registered medical practitioner and interview by an Approved Mental Health Professional (AMHP). See The Mental Health Act (1983)for further information.
  • In emergency situations, consider the person's capacity, but be aware that the steps reasonable when assessing capacity will be different to the steps in non-urgent cases. In such situations, it may be that capacity is only considered briefly. It will almost always be in the person's best interests to give urgent treatment without delay. See How do I assess a person's capacity?
  • If the person is judged to have capacity, and does not have signs of a mental disorder, and is at a low risk of suicide, but is at risk from the effects of their overdose, by law they have the right to refuse hospital admission and/or to refuse lifesaving treatment. Take all stepsto persuade them to attend hospital or have treatment. If the person allows, involve family and carers. Seek advice from the NPIS, Emergency or Medical consultant, consultant psychiatrist or medical indemnity organisation. Follow the person up and reassess the clinical situation as soon as is clinically appropriate; provide information about the potential consequences of not receiving hospital treatment and assessment; and reassess capacity. Clearly document their wishes and that these are against medical advice.

How do I assess a person's capacity?

The Mental Capacity Act (2005)
  • Mental capacity refers to the ability of a person to make a decision(or take a particular course of action) at a time when it is needed. It may change over time, for example, with changing levels of consciousness, emotional distress, or due to the effects of alcohol and/or drugs. Capacity may also vary according to the decision that needs to be made. The Mental Capacity Act (2005) states that all people aged 16years and over are presumed to have capacity. It aims to balance a person's right to make decisions for themselves with their right to be protected from harm.
  • The Mental Capacity Act (2005) has five key principles:
    • Presumption of capacity -adults should always be presumed to have the capacity to make a decision unless the healthcare professional can prove otherwise.
    • Maximizing decision-making capacity -the person must be given all practical support before it can be decided that they lack capacity. Support may involve extra time for assessment, repeating the assessment if capacity fluctuates, or using an interpreter or sign language.
    • The freedom to make seemingly unwise decisions -if the person makes a seemingly unwise decision, this in itself is not proof of incapacity. Proof of incapacity depends on the process by which the decision is made, not the decision itself.
    • Best interests -any decision or action taken on behalf of the person must be in their best interests. If the decision can be delayed until the person regains capacity, then it should be. A decision taken on another's behalf should take account of their wishes, including those expressed in an advance decision, and their beliefs and values. The decision-making process should involve, when appropriate, family, carers, and significant others.
    • The least restrictive alternative -when a decision is made on the person's behalf, the healthcare professional must choose the alternative that interferes least with the person's rights and freedoms while still achieving the necessary goal.
  • Capacity may be impaired by a number of factors, such asmental health disorders,alcohol or drug intoxication,drug withdrawal,confusion,significant emotional distress oranger,or due to intellectual disability.
  • To assess capacity, confirm that the person has an impairment of, or a disturbance in the functioning of, the mind or brain,which means they are unable to:
    • Understand relevant information about the decision to be made.
    • Or, retain that information.
    • Or, use or weigh that information as part of the decision-making process.
    • Or, communicate their decision (by talking, non-verbal communication, or any other means).
  • If a person fails one or more parts of this test and has an impairment of, or disturbance in the functioning of,the mind or brain, they do not have the relevant capacity.
  • When assessing a person's capacity, it may be useful to consider three questions:
    • Does the person have an impairment of, or a disturbance in the functioning of, their mind or brain?
    • Is the person unable to make a specific decision at the time it needs to be made for one or more of the reasons given in the Act?
    • Is the persons inability to make the specific decision at the time it needs to be made because of the impairment of,or disturbance in the functioning of, their mind or brain?
  • People who have capacity are entitled to make decisions that clinicians may think unwise. This right is protected by law in the Mental Capacity Act
  • The Mental Capacity Act also covers emergency situations. When someone who lacks capacity needs emergency medical treatment to save their life or prevent them coming to harm, the steps that are reasonable when assessing capacity and best interests will be different to the steps in non-urgent cases. In emergencies, it will almost always be in the person's best interest to give urgent treatment without delay. It is necessary to show that capacity has been considered, even if this has only been done so briefly.
  • In emergency situations involving children or young people aged under 16, or when the intervention is for the protection of others, common law, or the 'doctrine of necessity',applies. The principles of common law state:
    • The healthcare professional must reasonably believe that action is necessary to prevent harm to the person, or others.
    • Actions must be proportionate to the likelihood of the person, or others, being harmed and the seriousness of this harm.
  • Healthcare professionals should understand:
    • The Mental Capacity Act(2005) applies in England and Wales.
    • The Adults with Incapacity (Scotland) Act(2000) applies in Scotland. For more information, see the British Medical Association's documentMedical treatments for adults with incapacity: guidance on ethical and medico-legal issues in Scotland [BMA, 2009a].
    • In Northern Ireland, common law applies.
    • When and howThe Mental Health Act (1983)can be used in the urgent assessment of people who have self-harmed to treat the physical consequences of self-harm if their behaviour has been caused by a mental health condition.
    • That they have a duty to protect confidentiality wherever possible, but medical information may be disclosed without consent if a person lacks capacity and it is in their best interests.
Capacity and confidentiality issues in young people aged 1617 years
  • For young people aged 1617years who lack capacity, parents can consent on their behalf if the decision to be made is felt to be within parental control. Healthcare professionals are, however, able to give treatment regardless of whether parental consent has been given, as long as the principles of the Mental Capacity Act (2005) are followed, and the decision is judged to be in the young person's best interests.
  • If a young person who self-harms and has capacity refuses to involve their family or carers in their treatment, or refuses consent to disclose issues relating to their safety to family or carers, healthcare professionals must weigh the young person's right to confidentiality (and risk to the therapeutic relationship if confidentiality is breached) against providing family and carers with sufficient information to protect and care for the young person. For further information, see the Department of Health guidanceSeeking consent: working with children [DH, 2001].

The Mental Health Act (1983, amended 2007)

  • The Mental Health Act (1983) allows compulsory admission of people who:
    • Have a mental disorder of a nature and degree that warrants treatment in hospital,and
    • Need to be admitted in the interests of their own health or safety, or for the protection of other people.
  • Compulsory admission is arranged using the appropriate section of the Mental Health Act:
    • Section2 allows compulsory admission for up to 28days for assessment.
    • Section3 allows compulsory admission for up to 6months for treatment.
  • Sections2 and3 require an application from an Approved Mental Health Professional (AMHP, formerly an Approved Social Worker), or, rarely, the person's nearest relative, and recommendations from two doctors; one of whom is section12-approved (usually a psychiatrist) and one who has previous acquaintance with the person (usually the person's GP if at all practicable). Ideally, the person should be examined jointly by the two doctors with the AMHP also present. Where this is not possible, each doctor may carry out a separate examination. If the AMHP is not present, it is essential that at least one of the doctors discusses the person with the AMHP.
  • Section4 is used in exceptional cases to permit compulsory admission for up to 72hoursif there is urgent necessity, and undesirable delay would occur while trying to arrange admission under section2.
    • It requires an application from an AMHP (or, rarely, the person's nearest relative) and just one medical recommendation, preferably from a doctor with a previous acquaintance (usually the person's GP).
  • Section136 may be used by police to take people from a public place to a place of safetyand enable examination by a registered medical practitioner and interview by an AMHP. The person's GP, where known, may be informed.
  • Details of guidance and forms for the most common sections of the Mental Health Act can be accessed from theDepartment of Healthwebsite http://www.dh.gov.uk, as well as details of amendments made in theMental Health Act 2007 http://www.legislation.gov.uk.

Basis for recommendation

Assessing mental capacity
  • The recommendation to assess mental capacity for all people aged 16years and over who have self-harmed who are refusing treatment perceived to be in their best interests is based on expert opinion in the NICE guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care[National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004], and an updated chapter about consent, capacity, and confidentiality in the NICE guideline,Self-harm: longer-term management[National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2011]. The key principles for assessing capacity are based on expert opinion in the British Medical Association's guidance,The Mental Capacity Act 2005: guidance for health professionals[BMA, 2009b].
  • Decisions about capacity in the context of self-harm are often particularly difficult, as acute distress, ambivalence, and changeability often accompany self-harm thoughts and behaviour [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2011].
  • The recommendation to refer the person for an urgent mental health assessment or hospital admission, depending on clinical judgement, if they are judged to have diminished capacity and/or the presence of a significant mental health disorder, is pragmatic advice based on what CKS considers to be good medical practice. CKSacknowledges that diminished capacity may have multiple causes including drugs, alcohol, and acute delirium, as well as mental health states such as psychosis, and the person's place of care will depend on the likely underlying cause at the time of presentation.
  • The recommendation to seek advice from a psychiatrist who has experience in assessing capacity if there is uncertainty about whether a person has capacity is based on what CKS considers to be best practice.
  • The recommendations regarding the use of the Mental Capacity Act in emergency situations is based on expert opinion in a rapid response to the review article, When and how to treat patients who refuse treatment, [Humphreys, 2014; BMJ Rapid Responses, 2014]and the Mental Capacity Act Code of Practice [Office of the Public Guardian, 2013].
  • The recommendation that common law is applicable in emergency situations involving children or young people aged under 16, or when the intervention is for the protection of others, is based on expert opinion in a review article [Humphreys, 2014; BMJ Rapid Responses, 2014].
  • The recommendation regarding the management of a person who is judged to have capacity, and does not have signs of a mental disorder, and is at a low risk of suicide, but is at risk from the effects of their overdose is based on what CKS considers best practice and is extrapolated fromthe Newcastle upon Tyne Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust guidelineManagement of Poisoning and Drug Overdose in adult patients admitted to the Royal Victoria Infirmary [The Newcastle upon Tyne Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust, 2014].The NICE guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care[National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004], advises that healthcare professionals should have access to legal advice in such situations.
Capacity and confidentiality issues in young people aged 1617 years
  • The information about capacity and confidentiality issues in young people who self-harm has been highlighted as this group may have a number of special needs due to their vulnerability and family circumstances. The information has been based on the NICE guideline, Self-harm: longer-term management [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2011]and the Department of Health guidance, Seeking consent: working with children [DH, 2001].

What will happen in secondary care?

Medical management

  • A further assessment will be carried out, which may include:
    • Administration of antidotes, such as N-acetylcysteine, naloxone and flumazenil, if appropriate.
    • Blood and urinetests, such as toxicological investigations, full blood count, clotting, electrolytes, glucose, liver function tests and arterial blood gases.
    • ECG and radiological investigations.
  • Plasma or serum monitoring. Poisons that require emergency measurement of their plasma or serum concentration in order to guide clinical management include:
    • Carboxyhaemoglobin.
    • Digoxin.
    • Ethanol.
    • Ethylene and diethylene glycol.
    • Iron.
    • Lithium.
    • Methanol.
    • Paracetamol.
    • Salicylate.
    • Theophylline.
    • Valproate.
  • Treatment will depend on the type of poison taken, and the severity of the episode.
  • Most people will simply be observed, to make sure there are no worsening adverse effects, and that complications do not develop.
    • The length of the observation will depend on the clinical circumstances.
  • Prevention of absorption:
    • Activated charcoalis used if the person presents within an hour of ingestion. It may be effective after an hour formodified release preparations or medicines with antimuscarinic properties. The charcoal binds to the poison and prevents further absorption into the blood. An important use is to prevent the absorption of medicines which are toxic in small amounts, such as antidepressants.
    • In children this is only used if a potentially toxic amount of a drug that adsorbs to charcoal has been taken, and the child has presented within 1 hour of ingestion, in the absence of contraindications.
  • Active elimination is suitable for a limited number of drugs:
    • Activated charcoal given in repeated doses also enhances the elimination of some drugs even after they have been absorbed such as carbamazepine, phenobarbital, quinine, theophylline and dapsone.
    • Alkalinisation of the urine for salicylate poisoning. This is only recommended for moderate-severe salicylate poisoning for which haemodialysis cannot be performed and for chlorophenoxy herbicide poisoning.
    • Haemodialysis for ethylene glycol, lithium, methanol, phenobarbital, salicylates, and sodium valproate.
  • Removal from the gastrointestinal tract:
    • Gastric lavage is rarely performed. It is indicated only if a life-threatening amount has been ingested within the previous hour, and the poison cannot be removed using other methods. It may be useful for drugs such as lithium and iron that are not absorbed by charcoal. Lavage should never be done if a petroleum distillate or a corrosive substance has been ingested.
    • Whole bowel irrigation may be a management option for children who have taken potentially toxic amounts of iron or of sustained release or enterically coated medicines, especially the child presents more than 2 hours after ingesting these.In adults, this should only be performed following discussion with the NPIS.
  • Antidotes(if available) may be given for certain poisons, which either prevent the poison from working or reverse the effects of the poison.
    • N-acetylcysteine paracetamol.
    • Digoxin-specific antibody fragments digoxin.
    • Flumazenil injection benzodiazepines. This reverses the central nervous system and respiratory depression. However, if the person has also taken a tricyclic antidepressant or has epilepsy, there must be caution,as flumazenil can cause seizures and arrhythmias.
    • Glucagon injection insulin, beta-blockers.
    • Naloxone injection morphine, heroin, opiates. Note administration of intravenous naloxone is used to rapidly diagnose opioid poisoning. There should be a significant improvement in the person's condition 1-2 minutes after this has been given.
    • Desferrioxamine mesilate iron salts.
    • Fomepizole ethylene glycol or methanol.

Psychiatric assessment and management of deliberate self-poisoning

  • People who have deliberately self-poisoned should have a psychosocial assessment of their needs and risks by a specialist mental health professional while they are in hospital. The person's subsequent management is based on this assessment. This is likely toinclude:
    • Events that preceded the episode of self-harm.
    • Reasons for self-poisoning.
    • Assessment of possible suicidal intent.
    • Current personal difficulties and life issues.
    • Whether they have a psychiatric disorder.
    • Personality traits.
    • Family history.
    • Psychiatric history and history of self-harm.
    • Whether they have been exposed to self-harm in others, for example family or friends or through social media.
    • Risk of further self-harm and suicide.
    • The person's social supports and coping resources.
    • Whether the person is willing to accept help, and the help that would most benefit the person.
  • People who have a serious psychiatric disorder and/or are at a high risk of suicide are generally admitted for the treatment of the psychiatric disorder.
  • Children and young people aged under 16 who have deliberately self-poisoned should be admitted overnight on a paediatric ward, and be assessed the next day by an appropriately experienced healthcare professional. Young people aged 16 and 17 do not always need to be admitted overnight.

Basis for recommendation

Medical management

This information is based on expert advice in the British National Formulary [BNF 73, 2017], expert opinion in review articles [Anderson, 2013; Vale, 2016b], and the NICE guideline,The short-term physical and psychological management and secondary prevention of self-harm in primary and secondary care.[National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004].

Psychiatric assessment of people who have deliberately self-poisoned
  • The recommendation that people who have deliberately self-poisoned should have a psychosocial assessment of their needs and risks by a specialist mental health professional while they are in hospital is based on NICE guidance [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. The areas that should be included in this assessment and recommendation that people who have a serious psychiatric disorder and/or are at a high risk of suicide should generally beadmitted for the treatment of the psychiatric disorder are based on expert opinion in a review article [Hawton, 2016].
  • The recommendation that children and young people who have deliberately self-poisoned should be admitted overnight (regardless of their physical state), and be assessed the next day by an appropriately experienced healthcare professional is based on NICE guidance [National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2004]. This should be to a paediatric, adolescent or medical ward or to a designated unit.The Royal College of Psychiatrists recommends thatyoung people aged 16 or 17 do not always need to be admitted after being seen in Accident and Emergency ifthere has been a comprehensive mental health assessment, a managementplan formulated, and discharge is considered safe [Royal College of Psychiatrists, 2014b]. If there are any doubts or concerns, the young person should be admitted overnight.
