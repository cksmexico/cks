Domestic violence and abuse: Scenario: Managing domestic violence and abuse

Last revised in July 2018

Covers the management of domestic violence and abuse in primary care. It includes recommendations on how to support children who are affected by domestic violence and abuse and how to support people who disclose that they are perpetrating domestic violence and abuse.

Scenario: Managing domestic violence and abuse

From age 16 years onwards.

How should I assess a person with indicators of possible domestic violence and abuse?

Healthcare services should have a domestic violence and abuse policy and a designated person responsible for the response to domestic violence and abuse. Healthcare staff should be trained on how to recognize the signs of possible domestic violence and abuse, how to enquire sensitively and safely, the importance of confidentiality, and the organization’s process for responding to disclosure.

If domestic violence and abuse is suspected:

  • Facilitate disclosure in private with no third parties present (the presence of a child, partner, or relative makes disclosure potentially dangerous).
    • Ensure that the person feels safe.
    • Be attentive and approachable, and discuss the concerns with the person in a sympathetic and non-judgemental manner.
    • If needed, always use a professional interpreter who has had domestic violence and abuse training, or an advocate from the local specialist domestic violence and abuse agency. Never use the person's child, relative, or friend as an interpreter.
  • Advise the person that the discussion is confidential and that the information provided will be shared only with their consent, subject to practice policy on adult safeguarding and child protection.
    • Explain that the duty to keep patient information confidential is not absolute and that in exceptional circumstances (for example, if there is a risk of death to an adult or a risk of significant harm to a child) information may be shared without consent.
  • Ask direct questions. 
    • Explain that you are concerned (or, if it is a routine enquiry, that you ask everyone), and ask direct questions, such as:
      • 'Has anyone ever hit you? Who was it? What happened? When? What help did you seek?'
      • 'Are you afraid of your husband (or partner)?'
      • 'Has your husband (or partner) or someone else at home ever threatened to hurt you or physically harm you in some way? If so, when has it happened?'
      • 'Does your husband (or partner) or someone at home bully you or insult you?'
      • 'Does your husband (or partner) try to control you, for example, not letting you have money or go out of the house?'
      • 'Has your husband (or partner) pressurized or forced you into sex or to have any sexual contact you did not want?'
      • 'Has your husband (or partner) threatened to kill you?'
    • Where you have contact with children, give your attention to every child and talk sensitively and directly to each one to create opportunities for a disclosure.
      • Do not interrogate the child. Ask simple questions, such as: 'Is there something you are sad or worried about?'
      • Be aware that some children may not want to talk at all, whilst others disclose indirectly (not sharing the details without being prompted) or in a roundabout way, for example: 'Sometimes my stepdad upsets my mum.'
      • Reassure the child, but only so far as is honest and reliable. For example, do not say 'Everything will be all right now.' Suggested phrases include: 'I believe you; I am glad you came to me; I am sorry this has happened; you are not to blame; we are going to do something together to get help' – this will ensure that the child will not be surprised that you share the information.
      • The Bristol Primary Care Trust has produced a practical guide for parents and practitioners on Talking to Children about domestic violence and abuse, courtesy of Against Domestic Violence and Abuse (ADVA) partnership. 
    • Do not advise the person on what action they should take, as ill-informed advice could have serious consequences for all those involved.
      • For example, do not advise the person to leave their partner without appropriate support in place, including police involvement, because they are at high risk of injury or murder when they leave a violent and abusive partner.
  • If the person is reluctant to disclose domestic violence and abuse: 
    • Advise that you are available to talk if and when they are ready.
    • Provide information on help and support groups. 
    • Be aware of reasons why people may be reluctant to disclose domestic violence and abuse, including: 
      • Fear of retribution from the perpetrator of the abuse.
      • Fear of causing a family breakdown or bringing dishonour to the family, or that their children may be removed from their care, or of an unsympathetic response, and/or of not being believed.
      • Shame or embarrassment.
      • Cultural stigma.
      • Not believing that anything can be done to help them. 
      • Believing that the experience is ‘too trivial’ to mention.
  • Keep a detailed and accurate record of what was discussed, even if the suspicions of domestic violence and abuse did not lead to a disclosure.
    • Explain the need to document the consultation. Explain that, as a duty of care, healthcare professionals have a responsibility to record a disclosure or the findings of an examination (including any injuries) and do not need the person's permission to do this.
    • Enter in the medical record any health complaints, symptoms, and signs, as you would for any other person, including a description of any injuries.
      • It may be helpful to note the cause or suspected cause of these injuries or other conditions, including the person who caused the injury.
      • Consider taking photographs of physical injuries as pictures will convey the severity of injuries more effectively than a verbal description. Drawings or body maps can also be used to show the injuries.
    • Ensure that the record can only be accessed by those directly involved in the person’s care.
      • This has become more difficult with the advent of online general practice records and will require use of the online visibility function that is now available for the electronic medical record. For greater confidentiality, some facilities use a code or special mark to indicate cases of (or suspected) domestic violence and abuse.
      • Do not write anything where it can be seen by those who do not need to know, for example, on an X-ray slip or in hand-held notes (such as maternity notes). 
    • It is also useful to include:
      • The presence of children in the household and their ages.
      • Presence of risk factors, such as pregnancy and childbirth.
      • Indication of action taken (for example direct referrals).
      • Indication of information provided on local sources of help.
  • If it is deemed necessary or desirable to share information with other agencies, be aware of the legal grounds for sharing information. See the section on Confidentiality and sharing information for more information.
  • For further sources of support and information for healthcare professionals dealing with domestic violence and abuse, see the section on Support and information for healthcare professionals.

Domestic violence and abuse policy

A joint guideline by the Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP), SafeLives, and IRIS (Identification and Referral to Improve Safety) provides guidance to general practices to help them respond effectively to people experiencing domestic violence and abuse. The guideline includes the following key principles to help the practice develop a domestic violence and abuse policy [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014]:

  • A senior person within the practice should be identified to clarify the practice’s response to domestic abuse by:
    • Finding out what existing domestic violence and abuse services are available.
    • Engaging with local domestic violence and abuse services (and the Domestic Violence Co-ordinator) to develop an effective working partnership.
    • Supporting commissioning of the IRIS programme by the clinical commissioning groups (CCGs) and/or public health. This programme includes training and a referral pathway to specialist domestic violence and abuse support.
    • If IRIS is not yet commissioned, ensure effective training for the practice team. The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) public health guideline on Domestic violence and abuse makes detailed recommendations on the level of training required for different groups of professionals on how to respond to domestic violence and abuse [NICE, 2014]. Training should cover [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014]:
      • The range of possible health-seeking markers of domestic violence and abuse, such as depression, anxiety, tiredness, chronic pain, or non-specific symptoms.
      • How to ‘ask the question’ sensitively and safely.
      • The implications of domestic violence and abuse for both child protection and adult safeguarding.
      • How to respond in cases of immediate and significant risk (that is, where it may not be safe for the person to go home).
      • How to document domestic violence and abuse and manage the person's notes safely.
      • The protocols of information sharing, consent, and confidentiality.
      • Local domestic violence and abuse response pathways for all levels of risk.
      • The practice’s process for responding to disclosure of domestic violence and abuse.
      • What to do when a perpetrator discloses or is also registered with the GP.
    • Establishing a simple care pathway for people disclosing domestic violence and abuse by identifying a local designated person who will be responsible for their initial assessment. The designated person can either be:
      • An external specialist domestic violence and abuse service practitioner (an advocate-educator in the IRIS programme) who undertakes the initial assessment on behalf of the practice and liaises with the GP, or
      • An internal practice nurse or other health professional who is trained to carry out this work.
    • Ensuring that the practice’s response to disclosure always adheres to its information sharing protocols.
  • The primary healthcare team’s role is to:
    • Recognize people whose symptoms mean they might be more likely to be experiencing domestic violence and abuse.
    • Enquire sensitively and provide a safe and empathetic first response.
    • Understand the practice’s process for responding to disclosure, and know what to do when there is an immediate risk of harm to the person and their children.
    • Know who the designated person is for their practice.
    • Understand the process for arranging the person's initial assessment with the designated person.
    • Document domestic violence and abuse within patient records safely and keep records for evidence purposes.
    • Share information appropriately.
      • Information will be shared only with the consent of the person, subject to practice policy on child protection and adult safeguarding.
      • In exceptional circumstances, information may be shared without the person’s consent.
      • Some cases considered at multi-agency risk assessment conference (MARAC) meetings are likely to constitute exceptional circumstances because MARACs discuss the most serious cases of alleged or suspected domestic violence and abuse.
  • The role of the designated person is to:
    • Conduct an initial assessment using a recognized risk assessment too, such as the SafeLives DASH (domestic abuse, stalking and 'honour'-based violence) Risk Checklist.
    • Advise the person about the services available according to the risk level. This may result in:
      • The person becoming part of the designated person’s own case load, if they are a specialist domestic violence and abuse practitioner themselves.
      • Referral to an appropriate local specialist domestic violence and abuse service, such as the local MARAC co-ordinator, if the person consents.
      • Signposting to domestic violence and abuse resources and provision of a basic safety plan, if the person is unwilling to engage with services at this time.
    • Ensure that child protection and adult safeguarding procedures are initiated where required, especially where there is an immediate risk of harm to the person and/or their children.
  • MARACs are regular meetings where information on the highest risk domestic violence and abuse cases (that is, those at risk of homicide or serious harm) is shared between local agencies, including representatives of local police, probation, health, child protection, housing practitioners, independent domestic violence advisors (IDVAs), and other specialists from the statutory and voluntary sectors [DH, 2013; NICE, 2014].
    • After sharing all relevant information they have about the person experiencing domestic violence and abuse, the representatives discuss options for increasing the person's safety and turn these into a co-ordinated action plan.
    • The primary focus of the MARAC is to safeguard adults experiencing domestic violence and abuse. The MARAC will also make links with others to safeguard children and manage the behaviour of the perpetrator.
    • At the heart of a MARAC is the working assumption that no single agency or individual can see the complete picture of the life of the person, but all may have insights that are crucial to their safety.
  • IDVAs help keep people who experience domestic violence and abuse and their children safe from harm from violent partners or family. They serve as the person's primary contact and normally work with the person from the point of crisis, to assess their level of risk. They [Home Office, 2013]:
    • Discuss the range of suitable options.
    • Develop plans for immediate safety, including practical steps for the person to protect themselves and their children.
    • Develop plans for longer-term safety.
    • Represent their clients at the MARAC.  
    • Help apply sanctions and remedies available through the criminal and civil courts, including housing options.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based largely on the Department of Health guideline Responding to domestic abuse: A resource for health professionals [DH, 2017], the British Medical Association (BMA) guideline Domestic abuse [BMA, 2014], and a joint guideline by the Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP), SafeLives, and IRIS (Identification and Referral to Improve Safety) [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014], and draw on recommendations in the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) public health guideline Domestic violence and abuse: multi-agency working [NICE, 2014] and the NICE Quality standard on Domestic violence and abuse [NICE, 2016].

Facilitating disclosure
  • Experts state that despite the hidden nature of domestic violence and abuse, healthcare practitioners are in a key position to identify it and to initiate support and safety for people experiencing it [BMA, 2014; DH, 2017].
    • A study of women accessing domestic violence and abuse services (n = 2500) showed that before receiving specialist help, just under 50% of women had attended a GP appointment an average of 5.3 times, and 20% of women had attended A&E as a result of the abuse [CAADA, 2012].
    • It is estimated that 4–19.5% of women attending healthcare settings in England and Wales (particularly psychiatric, obstetrics and gynaecology, and emergency departments) may have experienced domestic violence and abuse in the past year [BMA, 2014]. 
  • The BMA recommends, based on good clinical practice, that when dealing with a person experiencing (or thought to be experiencing) domestic violence and abuse, the healthcare professional should [BMA, 2014]:
    • Treat the person with respect and dignity at all times. If the person is being abused, they are likely to feel ashamed, humiliated, and frightened, and even the slightest hint that a healthcare professional is sceptical about their story could stop them disclosing the information.
    • Respect confidentiality and privacy, and acknowledge the possible dangers associated with breaching these.
    • Think of the conversation as the start of a process rather than a one-off event. The person should not be pushed into disclosing abuse but should be given the time and space to have a conversation. If the person does not want to discuss it during the appointment, they should be assured that the healthcare professional is there to talk if and when they are ready.
Confidentiality
  • The BMA advises that every time a healthcare professional enquires about domestic violence and abuse they must emphasize that the discussion will be confidential, as such reassurance could make the difference as to whether or not a person chooses to disclose information. However, the person should be informed that there are limits to confidentiality and that in exceptional circumstances (such as if there is reason to suspect children are at risk) safeguarding and protection will always take precedence over confidentiality [BMA, 2014]. 
  • The BMA guidance on Vulnerable adults and confidentiality highlights that challenging situations can arise for health professionals where adults who are subject to abuse do not want confidential information disclosed, even where this would be the best way to ensure they are protected from harm. Decisions are particularly difficult where adults retain capacity under the terms of the Mental Capacity Act (2005) but health professionals may believe that they are not making a free and informed choice (for example as a result of pressure from or fear of the abuser). The BMA stresses the legal and ethical requirement to respect the choices of competent adults [BMA, 2017].
Asking questions about domestic violence and abuse
  • The Department of Health highlights that all health practitioners have a professional responsibility, if they identify signs of domestic violence and abuse or if things are not adding up, to sensitively ask the person alone and in private, whether old or young, about their experience of domestic or other abuse [DH, 2017].
  • The BMA acknowledges that enquiring about domestic violence and abuse is a challenging task (as the person experiencing it will often be feeling extremely daunted by the situation) but states that it would be a mistake for a healthcare professional to focus only on treating the person’s injuries or distress without asking about their causes, as this would provide only limited help to the person [BMA, 2014].
  • An expert reviewer of this Prodidy topic highlighted that clinicians often find it difficult to talk to children about domestic violence and abuse, as it can be upsetting to imagine what the child may be going through and frustrating if they feel they can’t do much about it. However, clinicians may need to overcome this difficulty for the following reasons:
    • Child safeguarding.
    • Children need to be heard and to know they can talk to the clinician.
    • It might be important to involve others, such as the health visitor or school nurse.
    • The child might disclose important information.
  • The recommended questions to ask are taken from the Department of Health guideline [DH, 2017] and the World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines on intimate partner violence or sexual violence [WHO, 2014].
Reasons for declining to disclose domestic violence and abuse
  • The reasons for declining to disclose domestic violence and abuse are taken from the Department of Health guideline [DH, 2017] and the BMA guideline [BMA, 2014].
How and what to record
  • These recommendations are based on the Department of Health guideline [DH, 2017] and the WHO guidelines on intimate partner violence or sexual violence [WHO, 2014].
Information and advice
  • The Department of Health and the BMA warn that it is not the job of a healthcare professional to advise a person experiencing domestic violence and abuse on what action they should take, as ill-informed advice could have serious consequences for all those involved. The professional is, however, expected to ask the person if they would like to be referred to specialist services for further treatment and/or support. If the person is unwilling or unable to engage with other services at that time, the healthcare professional should provide signposting and information on local services [BMA, 2014; DH, 2017].

How should I respond to a disclosure made by the person experiencing domestic violence and abuse?

Healthcare services should have a domestic violence and abuse policy and a designated person responsible for the response to domestic violence and abuse. Healthcare staff should be trained on how to recognize the signs of possible domestic violence and abuse, how to enquire sensitively and safely, the importance of confidentiality, and the organization’s process for responding to disclosure.

If domestic violence and abuse is disclosed by the person experiencing it:

  • Affirm that the person has made an important step by discussing the abuse.
    • Reassure the person that you believe them, that the abuse is not their fault, and that they have a right to safety. Some suggested phrases are:
      • 'Thank you for telling me.'
      • 'I believe you.'
      • 'This is not your fault.'
      • 'Your safety (and your children’s safety) is a priority.'
  • Reiterate that the discussion is confidential and that the information provided will be shared only with their consent, subject to practice policy on child protection and adult safeguarding.
    • Explain that the duty to keep the information confidential is not absolute and that in exceptional circumstances (for example, if there is a risk of death to an adult or a risk of significant harm to a child) information may be shared without consent.
  • Assess the person.
    • Undertake a comprehensive physical and mental health assessment.
      • Manage any presenting physical injuries and refer for more serious injuries.
      • Refer to appropriate health services as relevant, for example, maternity services, sexual assault referral centre (SARC) in the case of sexual abuse, and substance misuse services.
    • Work with the designated person for domestic violence and abuse to ascertain whether the person and/or any children are in immediate danger.
      • The SafeLives DASH (domestic abuse, stalking and 'honour'-based violence) Risk Checklist can help identify high risk cases of domestic abuse, stalking, and ‘honour’- based violence. However, it is not appropriate for most frontline clinicians as it requires additional specialist training to be used appropriately.
      • The SafeLives Risk Identification Checklist (RIC) – Young People’s version can be used to identify risk in cases of domestic abuse, stalking, and ‘honour’-based violence in young people’s relationships. It also requires additional training to be used appropriately.
      • The Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP) Safeguarding Children Toolkit for General Practice is designed to help GPs and the primary healthcare team to recognize when a child may be at risk of abuse, to know what to do if there are concerns, and to ensure that the team works with other disciplines and agencies to achieve the best possible outcomes for the child. 
    • If there is immediate danger: 
      • To an adult, contact the local police on 999 and initiate local adult safeguarding procedures, ideally with the person's consent.
      • To a child, contact local police on 999 and initiate local child protection procedures where relevant. 
    • If there is no immediate danger to the person (and any children), offer the person referral to, or an appointment with, the designated person for domestic violence and abuse, who will assess, advise, and refer the person appropriately.
      • Reassure the person that the referral is generally about support for the family and to keep children safe, not about taking children away from their families.
      • If the person declines the appointment, advise that the offer is there for whenever they are ready, and provide information on help and support groups. 
      • Ensure that children and young people affected by domestic violence and abuse are offered referral to age-appropriate specialist domestic abuse services.
      • Do not advise the person on what action they should take, as ill-informed advice could have serious consequences for all those involved.
      • Remember that risk is dynamic and can quickly change.
  • Keep a detailed and accurate record of what was discussed.
    • Explain the need to document domestic violence and abuse. Explain that, as a duty of care, healthcare professionals have a responsibility to record a disclosure or the findings of an examination (including any injuries) and do not need the person's permission to do this.
    • Enter in the medical record any health complaints, symptoms, and signs, as you would for any other person, including a description of any injuries.
      • It may be helpful to note the cause or suspected cause of these injuries or other conditions, including the person who caused the injury.
      • Consider taking photographs of physical injuries as pictures will convey the severity of injuries more effectively than a verbal description. Drawings or body maps can also be used to show the injuries.
    • Ensure that the record can only be accessed by those directly involved in the person’s care.
      • This has become more difficult with the advent of online general practice records and will require use of the online visibility function that is now available for the electronic medical record. For greater confidentiality, some facilities use a code or special mark to indicate cases of abuse or suspected abuse.
      • Do not write anything where it can be seen by those who do not need to know, for example on an X-ray slip or in hand-held notes (such as maternity notes). 
    • It is also useful to include:
      • The presence of children in the household and their ages.
      • Whether this is the first episode, or the history of how long regular abuse has been occurring.
      • Presence of risk factors, such as pregnancy and childbirth.
      • Results of completed risk assessment checklists. 
      • Indication of action taken (for example direct referrals).
      • Indication of information provided on local sources of help.
    • When recording a disclosure:
      • Use the person's words (with quotation marks) rather than your own.
      • Describe exactly what happened. For example, patient states 'my husband kicked me twice in the stomach' rather than 'patient assaulted'. 
    • The RCGP Safeguarding Adults at Risk of Harm Toolkit has a flowchart with information on how to record a disclosure of domestic violence and abuse in general practice medical records. 
  • If it is deemed necessary or desirable to share information with other agencies, be aware of the legal grounds for sharing information. See the section on Confidentiality and sharing information for more information.
  • Provide continuing support for all people who have once disclosed that they are experiencing domestic violence and abuse.
    • Monitor them for signs of further, or increased, domestic violence and abuse.
    • Monitor physical and/or mental health treatment.
    • Ensure that safeguarding measures are in place for vulnerable adults, and child protection plans are in place for children.
    • Support the person to seek help through local specialist services for domestic violence and abuse.
  • For further sources of support and information for healthcare professionals dealing with domestic violence and abuse, see the section on Support and information for healthcare professionals.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based largely on the Department of Health guideline Responding to domestic abuse: A resource for health professionals [DH, 2017], the British Medical Association (BMA) guideline Domestic abuse [BMA, 2014], a joint guideline by the Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP), SafeLives, and IRIS (Identification and Referral to Improve Safety) [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014], and the CAADA (Co-ordinated Action Against Domestic Abuse) guideline In plain sight: Effective help for children exposed to domestic abuse [CAADA, 2014], and draw on recommendations in the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) public health guideline Domestic violence and abuse: multi-agency working [NICE, 2014] and the NICE Quality standard on Domestic violence and abuse [NICE, 2016].

Affirmation and reassurance
  • An expert reviewer of this CKS topic highlighted that it can sometimes take years for people to disclose domestic violence and abuse for a number of reasons, including a feeling of shame, vulnerability, or fear. The person may also have very low self-esteem and may have been made to feel like they are ‘going mad’ or that no one would believe them. The immediate response from a healthcare professional is therefore very important.
  • The recommended phrases were suggested by the expert reviewer. 
Assessment
  • According to the Department of Health [DH, 2017]:
    • Assessment identifies the risks facing the abused person and can inform safety planning, inform referrals to specialist support services, and aid any police investigation.
    • Risk identification is a critical first step in ensuring that people are made safe. It provides healthcare professionals with:
      • A framework for processing the information obtained in order to make a professional judgement about levels of risk.
      • Reasons for referral to appropriate support and safeguarding.
      • A record that can be used to support future case management.
      • A common tool and a shared language and understanding of risk with the other agencies that will need to work with the person experiencing domestic violence and abuse.
  • The SafeLives DASH (Domestic Abuse, Stalking and Honour Based Violence) Risk Checklist is a reliable method for the initial risk assessment of adults [DH, 2017]. It is primarily intended for specialist domestic violence workers, such as Independent Domestic Violence Advisors [IDVAs] [SafeLives, 2016], and requires additional training to be used appropriately. 
  • The recommendation to use the SafeLives Risk Identification Checklist (RIC) – Young People’s version is based on the Department of Health guideline, which states that undertaking a risk assessment for each dependent child in the family, and listening and speaking to each child present carefully, provides a record of evidence and information on which to base a referral to social services [DH, 2017]. As with the SafeLives DASH Checklist, it requires additional training to be used appropriately.
Management
  • Expert opinion in the Department of Health guideline [DH, 2017], the BMA guideline [BMA, 2014], and the joint RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS guideline [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014] is that following a disclosure of domestic violence and abuse:
    • The person experiencing domestic violence and abuse must feel that they can trust the healthcare professional to help them stop the abuse.
    • The healthcare professional must reassure the person that they believe them, that the abuse is not their fault, and that they have a right to safety. Confidentiality must also be further emphasized at this stage.
    • The role of a healthcare professional is to provide support and information to help the person make a decision on what to do next, to encourage them to make this plan as safe as possible, and to help assess the risk to the person and any children in the household.
  • Experts advise that a multiagency input is essential when managing disclosure of domestic violence and abuse [BMA, 2014; NICE, 2014; DH, 2017].
    • A person being abused will only be able to disclose the things they know about. However, there are other services, for example, the police, probation, youth justice, substance misuse, mental health, and other health services, that may have additional information about the perpetrator. There are also professionals who know more about the impact of neglect and trauma on children than a mother could be expected to know [DH, 2017].
  • An expert reviewer of this CKS topic pointed out that parents might be fearful of disclosing abuse for fear that their child/ children could be taken away, and advises that it is worth emphasizing that a referral is generally about support for the family to keep children safe and not about taking the children away from their families.
Managing children and young people affected by domestic violence and abuse
  • NICE recommends that there should be clear referral pathways to local services that can support children and young people affected by domestic violence and abuse. These services should match the child's developmental stage, and the interventions should be timely and continue over a long enough period to achieve positive lasting effects [NICE, 2014].
  • Evidence identified by CAADA showed that specialist children’s services are vital in reducing the impact of domestic violence and abuse. They address not only the physical risks children face but also give critical early support to help children understand what has happened and that they are not responsible. The evidence also showed that [CAADA, 2014]:
    • These specialist children’s services give young people a voice and improve their safety, recovery, health, and wellbeing. Although many children will also require longer-term therapeutic help, these services are a vital first step in rebuilding their lives and sense of self.
    • These services are effective in reducing abusive behaviours amongst children, thereby representing a cost-effective early intervention to prevent harm in later life.
    • In addition to significant improvements in health, safety, wellbeing, and achievement, abusive behaviour in children exposed to domestic violence and abuse dropped from 25% to 7% following support from the services.
Record keeping
  • The BMA, RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, and Department of Health guidelines all highlight the importance of keeping accurate documentation of consultations with a person who has disclosed domestic violence and abuse [BMA, 2014; RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014; DH, 2017].
    • This record may be required as evidence in several scenarios, such as during the prosecution of a perpetrator, if the victim was obtaining protection through an injunction or court order, in cases where the person is at risk of deportation due to immigration laws, for housing provision applications, and to assess the possible risks to children [BMA, 2014]. 
    • Healthcare professionals do not need permission to record the disclosure of domestic abuse or their findings from an examination, and this must be explained to the person [BMA, 2014; DH, 2017].
  • An expert reviewer of this CKS topic pointed out that the challenge of recording domestic violence and abuse information in the electronic medical record of people experiencing or perpetrating abuse and their children is how to do this without increasing risk of harm to the person being abused and their children. The perpetrator may not know that their partner or family member has disclosed domestic violence and abuse to a GP or nurse, nor will they necessarily know if their case is being discussed at the multi-agency risk assessment conference (MARAC). When the perpetrator is not aware of a disclosure of domestic violence and abuse, an accidental discovery increases the risk to the person being abused and their children.
Information and advice
  • The Department of Health and the BMA warn that it is not the job of a healthcare professional to advise a person experiencing domestic violence and abuse on what action they should take, as ill-informed advice could have serious consequences for all those involved. The professional is, however, expected to ask the person if they would like to be referred to specialist services for further treatment and/or support. If the person is unwilling or unable to engage with other services at that time, the healthcare professional can provide signposting and information on local services [BMA, 2014; DH, 2017].
Follow up and support
  • The BMA highlights that due to the nature of primary care and especially with the work of GPs, a person experiencing domestic violence and abuse is likely to have repeat consultations with the same healthcare professional over time. Therefore, healthcare professionals should provide continuing support every time they see a person who has once disclosed that they are suffering from domestic violence and abuse, to enable them to monitor the person for signs of further, or increased, domestic violence and abuse [BMA, 2014].

How should I respond to a disclosure made by a perpetrator of domestic violence and abuse?

Healthcare services should have a domestic violence and abuse policy and a designated person responsible for the response to domestic violence and abuse. Healthcare staff should be trained on how to recognize the signs of possible domestic violence and abuse, how to enquire sensitively and safely, the importance of confidentiality, and the organization’s process for responding to disclosure.

The approach to managing a person who perpetrates domestic violence and abuse will depend on whether the person directly acknowledges their behaviour as a problem, seeks help for a related problem, or has been identified by others as abusive.

  • Before seeking or enabling a disclosure from a person suspected of being a perpetrator of domestic violence and abuse:
    • Consider your safety and that of the person being abused and any children.
    • Seek advice from (or refer the person to) the designated person for domestic violence and abuse.
  • Use motivational interviewing approaches to be persuasive but supportive. 
    • Express empathy through reflective listening.
    • Identify any discrepancy between the person’s goals or values and their current behaviour, and explore it further.
    • Avoid argument and direct confrontation.
    • Adjust to the person’s resistance rather than opposing it directly.
    • Support self-efficacy and optimism.
    • Some suggested phrases are:
      • 'Thank you for talking about this.'
      • 'These behaviours could be really affecting your partner/family.'
      • 'Help is available.'
  • If the person presents with a problem, such as excessive drinking, carer issues, stress, or depression, but does not refer to their abusive behaviour, some useful questions to ask include:
    • 'How is this drinking/stress at work/depression affecting how you are with your family and spouse?'
    • 'When you feel like that, what do you do?'
    • 'When you feel like that, how do you behave?'
    • 'Do you find yourself shouting or smashing things?'
    • 'Do you ever feel violent towards a particular person?'
    • 'It sounds like you want to make some changes for your benefit and for your partner/family. What choices do you have? What can you do about it? What help would you like to make these changes?'
  • If the person admits that domestic violence and abuse is an issue and they are not the victim, useful questions to ask include:
    • 'How does your behaviour make you feel?'
    • 'How does your behaviour affect people close to you?'
    • 'How do alcohol/drugs affect your behaviour?'
    • 'What do you think will help you change your behaviour?'
  • If the person responds openly to these prompting questions, more direct questions relating to heightened risk factors may be appropriate. For example:
    • 'Do you feel unhappy about your partner seeing friends or family? Do you ever try to stop them?'
    • 'Have you assaulted your partner in front of the children?'
    • 'Have you ever assaulted or threatened your partner with a knife or other weapon?'
    • 'Did your behaviour change towards your partner during pregnancy?'
  • Be aware of local referral pathways, and offer the person referrals to specialist services (based on the information gathered from them).
    • Manage the risk to the person being abused and any children.
    • Address the perpetrator’s behaviour and other health needs.
    • Refer young people aged 16–18 years who are harming their partner, siblings, parents, or other adult family members for support through child protection procedures. Be aware that children who harm others are likely to have considerable needs themselves.
    • Provide information on help and support groups. 
  • Keep a detailed record of the disclosure.
    • Record the information in the perpetrator’s case notes. 
      • The Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP) Safeguarding Adults at Risk of Harm Toolkit has a flowchart with information on how to record a disclosure of domestic violence and abuse in general practice medical records, including a disclosure made by a perpetrator. 
    • Advise the person that the discussion is confidential and that the information provided will be shared only with their consent, subject to practice policy on child protection and adult safeguarding.
      • Explain that the duty to keep the information confidential is not absolute and that in exceptional circumstances (for example, if there is a risk of death to an adult or a risk of significant harm to a child) information may be shared without consent.
  • If it is deemed necessary or desirable to share information with other agencies, be aware of the legal grounds for sharing information. See the section on Confidentiality and sharing information for more information.
  • For further sources of support and information for healthcare professionals dealing with domestic violence and abuse, see the section on Support and information for healthcare professionals.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Department of Health guideline Responding to domestic abuse: A resource for health professionals [DH, 2017], and draw on recommendations in the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) public health guideline Domestic violence and abuse: multi-agency working [NICE, 2014] and the NICE Quality standard on Domestic violence and abuse [NICE, 2016].

Management
  • According to the Department of Health [DH, 2017]:
    • After anonymous helplines, GPs are the main source of support accessed by perpetrators of domestic violence and abuse. 
    • Perpetrators are users of the health service, so healthcare professionals are in a key position to identify them and assist in the interruption of their harmful behaviours.
      • An expert reviewer of this CKS topic highlighted that there is no evidence on how to do this effectively and safely.  Moreover, many areas do not have perpetrator programmes, thereby limiting the potential response of healthcare professionals.
    • The primary goal of all work with perpetrators of abuse is to ensure the safety of their victims and any children. 
    • How a health professional responds to any disclosure of domestic violence and abuse could be significant in encouraging responsibility and motivating a perpetrator to change.
  • The recommended phrases were suggested by an expert reviewer of this CKS topic. 
Offering referral to specialist services
  • NICE recommends that health and wellbeing boards and commissioners should commission and evaluate tailored interventions for people who perpetrate domestic violence and abuse, in accordance with national standards and based on the local needs assessment [NICE, 2014]. 
    • The primary aim of the interventions should be to increase the safety of the perpetrator's partner and any children, and ensure this is monitored and reported. 
    • Perpetrator services should be linked with services providing specialist support for those experiencing domestic violence and abuse, including children and young people. For example, link ongoing risk assessments of the perpetrator with safety planning and support provided by specialist services.
  • The Department of Health highlights that the referral pathway for perpetrators of domestic violence and abuse should include specialist services in addition to universal services that deal with health issues, such as mental health problems and substance misuse. In the opinion of the Department of Health, a referral pathway that does not include specialist services risks dealing with such health issues alone and may allow the perpetrator to avoid responsibility for their domestic violence and abuse behaviours with increased risk of harming. Although mental health problems and substance misuse may be factors in abusive behaviour, they are not the sole causes of perpetrators’ behaviour (for example some perpetrators may have issues relating to past traumatic experiences) [DH, 2017].
    • A range of specialist services may be available locally or regionally based on a number of different therapy approaches, including a structured domestic violence perpetrator programme (DVPP) offering a series of weekly sessions using education and challenge, and examining the person’s behaviours and beliefs about gender and relationships to change their behaviour.
    • In the UK, perpetrator services are run by prisons and the criminal justice system or as community-based programmes, usually by third sector organizations. DVPPs aim to increase the safety of the person experiencing domestic violence and abuse by supporting perpetrators to end their use of violent and abusive behaviours. 
Recording and sharing information
  • The Department of Health highlights the importance of keeping accurate records and states that it will enable continuity of care and may also help in any future legal proceedings, serious case reviews, safeguarding adult reviews, domestic homicide reviews, or other inquiries [DH, 2017].

What do I need to know about confidentiality and sharing information?

Healthcare services should have a domestic violence and abuse policy and a designated person responsible for the issue of domestic violence and abuse. Healthcare staff should be trained on how to recognize the signs of possible domestic violence and abuse, how to enquire sensitively and safely, the importance of confidentiality, and the organization’s process for responding to disclosure.

  • If it is deemed necessary or desirable to share information with other agencies:
    • Consult with the designated person for domestic violence and abuse.
    • Obtain consent from the person to share information.
  • If the person does not consent:
    • Be aware of the lawful grounds for sharing without consent, including:
      • Where there is a risk of harm to the person, any children involved, or somebody else if information is not passed on as a referral.
      • To inform a risk assessment (where the definition of ‘harm’ to a child includes impairment caused by seeing or hearing the abuse of another person).
      • When the courts request information about a specific case.
    • The decision to over-ride the duty of confidentiality must be justifiable and proportionate, based on the potential or actual harm to adults or children at risk. 
      • For an adult, a relatively high risk is necessary before considering over-riding the duty of confidentiality.
      • For children, careful consideration should be given to disclosure even about low risks.
  • If a decision is made to share information:
    • Record the decision and the reasons for that decision. 
    • Always follow local single and multiagency guidelines for sharing information. These should:
      • Comply with the Data Protection Act 1998 and the General Data Protection Regulations 2018, Caldicott principles, and safeguarding guidance for children or adults.
      • Define the range of information that can be shared and with whom (including information on a perpetrator’s criminal history).
    • If a decision is made to share information without consent or if consent is refused:
      • Be completely sure that the decision does not place someone at risk of greater violence.
      • Record your reasons to be able to justify your decision. Also, record confirmation that the information you passed on has been received and understood.
      • If safe and appropriate, discuss what will be disclosed and why you are doing so with the person. Note when and whether the person was informed about the disclosure, and reasons why if not informed (for example increased risk).
      • Consider seeking specialist advice, for example, from the Caldicott guardian or the Information Commissioner’s Office helpline (0303 123 1113). 
  • If a decision is made not to share information, record the decision and the reasons for that decision.
  • Further information and advice on information sharing is available from several sources, including:
    • The Information Commissioner’s Office — the data sharing checklist provides a step by step guide through the process of deciding whether to share personal data. 
    • The Department of Health — the guidance on Confidentiality: NHS Code of Practice sets out standards required for NHS organizations concerning patient confidentiality to ensure that patient information is handled fairly, lawfully, and as transparently as possible.
    • The General Medical Council (GMC) — the guidance on Confidentiality: good practice in handling patient information provides a framework to help decide/consider when and why to share information. 
    • The British Medical Association (BMA) — the guidance on Vulnerable adults and confidentiality sets out the current legal and ethical position in relation to disclosure of information relating to adults who retain capacity but may be subject to some form of duress. There is also a section on further BMA and GMC guidance on disclosure of confidential information and on mental capacity.
    • Safelives.org.uk — the guidance on The legal grounds for sharing information contains a checklist for sharing information without consent. 

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based largely on the Department of Health guideline Responding to domestic abuse: A resource for health professionals [DH, 2017], the British Medical Association (BMA) guideline Domestic abuse [BMA, 2014], and a joint guideline by the Royal College of General Practitioners (RCGP), SafeLives, and IRIS (Identification and Referral to Improve Safety) [RCGP/SafeLives/IRIS, 2014], and draw on recommendations in the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) public health guideline Domestic violence and abuse: multi-agency working [NICE, 2014] and the NICE Quality Standard on Domestic violence and abuse [NICE, 2016].

Confidentiality
  • The BMA advises that every time a healthcare professional enquires about domestic violence and abuse they must emphasize that the discussion will be confidential, as such reassurance could make the difference as to whether or not a person chooses to disclose information. However, the person should be informed that there are limits to confidentiality and that in exceptional circumstances (such as if there is reason to suspect children are at risk) safeguarding and protection will always take precedence over confidentiality [BMA, 2014].
  • The BMA guidance on Vulnerable adults and confidentiality highlights that challenging situations can arise for health professionals where adults who are subject to abuse do not want confidential information disclosed, even where this would be the best way to ensure they are protected from harm. Decisions are particularly difficult where adults retain capacity under the terms of the Mental Capacity Act (2005) but health professionals may believe that they are not making a free and informed choice (for example as a result of pressure from or fear of the abuser). The BMA stresses the legal and ethical requirement to respect the choices of competent adults [BMA, 2017].
Sharing information
  • The legalities of information sharing (and confidentiality) for a healthcare professional are complex [BMA, 2014].
  • The BMA advises that [BMA, 2014]:
    • Each trust, health authority, and clinical commissioning group should have information sharing policies to help inform healthcare professionals, and these should be followed.
    • People have the right to object to information they provide in confidence being disclosed to a third party in a form that identifies them, even if this is to someone who might provide essential healthcare.
      • Where the person is competent to make such a choice and the consequences of that choice have been fully explained, their wishes should be respected.
      • It is very important in the context of domestic violence and abuse that the person is involved in all stages of the decision-making process and that they retain as much control as possible over disclosures of information. They may feel threatened by the thought of others knowing about their situation and may be concerned that the disclosure of what has occurred may lead to further violence and abuse.
    • However, healthcare professionals must consider whether other members of the household are also at risk and whether continued or more severe abuse could result in permanent damage. 
      • The decision to disclose is based partly on a balancing of several moral imperatives, including the risk and likelihood of harm if no disclosure is made, the risk and likelihood of harm if a third party disclosure is made, and the need to maintain the trust of the person.
      • Because there is no broad consensus of how harm to people should be evaluated or from whose perspective it should be judged, the BMA advises that, where feasible, healthcare professionals should try to envisage the seriousness of the potential harm from the viewpoint of the person likely to suffer it.
      • While a refusal to disclose information by a competent adult can be overridden in order to protect a third party (such as a child or vulnerable adult who may be in the household), it becomes more difficult where an adult refuses to disclose information in order to protect themselves.
