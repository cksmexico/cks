Scenario: Management of acute kidney injury

From age 18 years onwards.

What should I know to manage a person with acute kidney injury?

  • For a person with acute kidney injury, assess:
    • Volume statusby checking:
      • Fluid intake and losses.
      • Peripheral perfusion (capillary refill time).
      • Heart rate/blood pressure (and any postural changes).
      • Jugular venous pressure.
      • Moistness of mucous membranes, skin turgor.
      • Changes in urination pattern.
      • For peripheral oedema and pulmonary crackles.
    • Renal function and serum potassium level (to exclude hyperkalaemia).
    • For possible underlying causes, including asking questions on:
      • Current symptoms, if the person is unwell (for example diarrhoea, vomiting).
      • Any recent symptoms that may suggest an underlying obstructive cause (for example lower urinary tract symptoms, bloating from a pelvic mass, renal colic).
      • History of cardiovascular disease increasing the risk of impaired renal perfusion.
      • Symptoms of an underlying inflammatory process (for example vasculiticrash, arthralgia, epistaxis, or haemoptysis).
      • Drug history any medicines that could cause or exacerbate AKI or accumulate and cause harm, including over the counter formulationsand herbal remedies.
      • Possibility of rhabdomyolysis, for example,skeletal muscle injury,muscle overexertion, crush injury, prolonged immobility.
    • For renal disease by performing urine dipstick testing for blood, protein, leucocytes, nitrites, and glucose.
      • AKI with negative urinalysis usually indicates a pre-renal cause, but drugs should also be considered.
      • Positive protein and blood indicators on urinalysis may suggestglomerular disease (particularly with more strongly positive results, for example, 2+ blood, 2+ protein).
      • Increased white cells are non-specific but may suggest infection (most common) or interstitial nephritis.
      • Be aware that dipstick analysis of urine from people with catheters should be interpreted with caution because of the possibility of false-positive results (for example dipstick haematuria as a result of simple trauma).
    • The stageof acute kidney injury (see Table 2). Stage the person's acute kidney injury according to the criterion which gives the highest stage.
      • In a primary care setting the creatinine level will usually be the most readily available result, but if urine output is also being monitored, base staging on whichever shows the highest (worst) stage if creatinine and urine output map to different stages.

Basis for recommendation

Assessing volume status, renal function, and serum potassium level
  • This information has been compiled from guidelines on acute kidney injury (AKI) fromKidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]and Think Kidneys [Think Kidneys, 2016a],supported by expert opinion inreview articles[Blakeman et al, 2013; Thornburg and Gray-Vickrey, 2016].
Identifying the cause
  • There is agreement between guidelines on AKI from the National Institute for Care Excellence (NICE) [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]and KDIGO [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]that it is important to try to identify an underlying cause of AKI. The KDIGO guideline states that although it may not be possible to determine the cause, and identifying a cause may not always suggest a specific treatment, it is important to look so that people with certain diseases which can respond to a specific treatment can be identified [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012].
  • The advice on what questions to ask to identify a cause is extrapolated from theThink Kidneys documents Communities at risk of developing acute kidney injury[Think Kidneys, 2015],Best practice guidance. Responding to AKI warning stage test results for adults in primary care [Think Kidneys, 2016a],and Could my patient have acute kidney injury? Quick reference guide [Think Kidneys, 2017].CKS also derived this recommendation from expert opinion on the causes of AKI discussed in review articles[Thornburg and Gray-Vickrey, 2016; Makris and Spanou, 2016].
Urinalysis
  • This recommendation is based on the consensus of the NICE guideline development group (GDG) as evidence was lacking. The aim of dipstick testing is to detect treatable conditions such as glomerulonephritis, acute pyelonephritis, and interstitial nephritis. The GDG also discussed the possibility of false positive results in people with catheters (for example microscopic haematuria from traumatic catheter insertion)[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
  • The information on interpretation of urine dipstick results reflects the opinion ofNICE [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013], Think Kidneys[Think Kidneys, 2016a],and expert opinion in review articles[Larmour and Maxwell, 2015; Ostermann, 2016].
Staging
  • It is important to stage an episode of AKI because as the stage increases, so does the severity and the risk of adverse outcomes and the need for renal replacement therapy [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012; Think Kidneys, 2016a],therefore staging can help to guide management. The advice to stage AKI according to the criterion which shows the most severe stage is based on the Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes clinical practice guideline on acute kidney injury[Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]and expert opinion in a review article[Prescott et al, 2012].
  • The NICE GDG were of the opinion that using a system to stage AKI was beneficial but the evidence was not sufficient for a recommendation on using a specific staging system to be made. For prognostic purposes, the GDG felt that it was unlikely that harm would result from using one definition over another [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]. CKS, therefore, was of the opinionit is reasonable to provide a simplified version, amalgamating criteria from the three main staging systems and hasbased its recommendation on staging on summaries from NICE [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]andThink Kidneys Best practice guidance. Responding to AKI warning stage test results for adults in primary care [Think Kidneys, 2016a].

How should I manage a person with acute kidney injury?

Immediate admission or referral to hospital is not needed for all people with acute kidney injury (AKI). The decision to refer and urgency of referralshould also take into account factors including age, comorbidities (number and severity), and the need for carer input. The following list is intended as a guide, taking individual circumstances into consideration:

  • Arrange urgent admission or same day referral (depending on clinical judgement) for people with:
    • Likely stage 3 acute kidney injury.
    • An underlying cause that requires urgent secondary care management such as when an obstructed, infected kidney is suspected.
    • No identifiable cause for acute kidney injury.
    • A risk of urinary tract obstruction (for example known prostate or bladder disease; abdominal or pelvic cancer; known previous hydronephrosis; recurrent urinary tract infections; or other conditions consistent with possible obstruction, for example, anuria, single functioning kidney, neurogenic bladder).
    • Sepsis.
    • Evidence of hypovolaemia and a need for intravenous fluid replacement and monitoring.
    • A deterioration in clinical condition or a need for observation or monitoring of a frequency which is impractical in primary care.
    • A complication of acute kidney injury requiring urgent secondary care management such as pulmonary oedema, uraemic encephalopathyor pericarditis, or severe hyperkalaemia.
  • Discuss the management of acute kidney injury with a nephrologistas soon as possible and within 24hours of detection, when one or more of the following is present:
    • Stage 4 or 5 chronic kidney disease. For more information, see the CKS topic onChronic kidney disease.
    • A possible diagnosis that may need specialist treatment, for example, tubulointerstitial nephritis, glomerulonephritis (indicated by haematuria/proteinuria), systemic vasculitis that may also be affecting the kidney, or myeloma.
    • Inadequate response to treatment.
    • Othercomplicationsassociated with acute kidney injury.
    • A renal transplant.
  • For people with Stage 1 acute kidney injury, discuss with a general physicianif there is uncertainty about the cause or management or the person is not responding to treatment.
    • Discussion with a nephrologist may be an alternative option, depending on local availability and policy.
  • For people with Stage 1acute kidney injury, who do not have an indication for admission, referral, or specialist input:
    • Manage the cause, if the expertise and resources are available in primary care.
    • Offer supportive measures such as advice on maintaining appropriate hydration.
    • Consider stopping potentially nephrotoxicmedications (for example angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor antagonists, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, and diuretics) or adjusting thedoses of medication in relation to renal function.Seek specialist advice if unsure.
      • For more information, see the Think Kidneys documentsAcute kidney injury - potentially problematic drugs and actions to take in primary careand Guidelines for medicines optimisation in patients with acute kidney injury.
      • Information on dose adjustment in renal impairment is available from the British National Formulary (BNF) or the manufacturers' Summary of Product Characteristics (available atwww.medicines.org.uk/emc).
    • Monitor creatinine regularly, using clinical judgement to determine frequency.
      • Be aware that even small increases in creatinine can be significant.
    • Reconsider the need to admit to hospital or discuss with a specialist if there is deterioration in the person's condition,oran inadequate response to treatment.

Basis for recommendation

Approach to managementof acute kidney injury (AKI) and need for clinical judgement
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion. The full guideline on acute kidney injury from the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) states 'the extent of investigation and management should be proportionate to the stage of AKI' [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]. A Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) clinical practice guideline on AKI also states: 'because the stage of acute kidney injury has clearly been shown to correlate with short-term and even longer-term outcomes it is advisable to tailor management to acute kidney injury stage' [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012].
  • The KDIGO guidelines offer management suggestions for each stage, but make it clear that these are not mandatory and that care decisions should be individualized [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]. This is supported by expert opinion in a subsequent review article, which lists factors affecting the decision to refer [Thomas and Pal, 2013]and in a Think Kidneysbest practice guidance for primary care [Think Kidneys, 2016a].CKS has therefore offered a guide to management with the intention that clinical judgement based on individual circumstances should also be taken into account when making decisions on the care of a person with AKI.
Admission to hospital and same-day referral
  • CKS found no guidelines explicitly stating a threshold for referral from primary to secondary care. However, the NICE guideline development group (GDG) felt that clinical judgement would be required by primary healthcare practitioners when deciding whether admission is necessary. The GDG were also of the opinion that earlier specialist intervention could lead to better care and lower mortality[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
  • The recommendation for people with likely stage 3 kidney injury is based on the Think Kidneys documentBest practice guidance: Responding to AKI warning stage test results for adults in primary care. This recommends that if there is a high pre-test probability of stage 3 AKI (for example in the context of acute illness), immediate admission should be considered [Think Kidneys, 2016a].
  • The NICE GDG recommends that if pyonephrosis (infected and obstructed kidney) is suspected, immediate ultrasound of the urinary tract (within 6 hours) should be performed. It also recommends urgent ultrasound (within 24 hours of assessment) for people who have no identified cause for their acute kidney injury or are at risk of urinary tract obstruction. The GDG were of the opinion that this timescale should avoid delay in identifying treatable obstruction and that when weighing up the costs of misdiagnosing obstruction (such as permanent renal damage or the need for renal replacement) it should be cost-effective to recommend ultrasound for people at high risk of obstruction [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]. This is consistent with recommendations from Think Kidneys[Think Kidneys, 2016c].
  • The recommendation to admit a person with sepsis or a need for intravenous fluid replacement and monitoring is based on aThink Kidneys best practice guidance for primary care[Think Kidneys, 2016a] and an approach to the management of AKI from the Improvement Academy and Centre for Reviews and Dissemination[Improvement Academy and Centre for Reviews and Dissemination, 2015].
  • CKS has extrapolated the statement about admitting people with a deterioration in clinical condition from expert opinion in the NICE guideline which advises close monitoring and observation in this group [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013], because this intensity of care may not be available in a primary care setting.
  • Admission of people with complications of AKI has been suggested by CKSbased on extrapolation from the NICE criteria for when renal replacement therapy should be considered[NICE, 2013b];Think Kidneys guidance for primary healthcare professionals[Think Kidneys, 2016a];and expert opinion in a review article[Levey and James, 2017].
Discussion with a nephrologist
  • No data were found to support a recommendation on when a nephrologist should be consulted; therefore the NICE GDG used their clinical experience to compile a list of clinical circumstances for which people with AKI should be referred to nephrology services. These include people with potentially treatable disease and/or an increased risk of progression of their acute kidney injury and adverse outcomes. The time frame suggested was based on the expert opinion of the GDG [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
  • In terms of which people shoud be discussed with a nephrologist,recommendations are largely supported by the Think Kidneys guidance for primary healthcare professionals[Think Kidneys, 2016a]and the Improvement Academy and Centre for Reviews and Dissemination 'Effectiveness matters' summary[Improvement Academy and Centre for Reviews and Dissemination, 2015], and expert opinion in review articles [Larmour and Maxwell, 2015; Levey and James, 2017].
Stage 2 AKI
  • This recommendation is pragmatic. CKSfound no specific mention in the NICE guideline of the primary care management of people with stage 2 AKI. However, the KDIGO guideline has an algorithm for the stage-based management of AKIwhich suggests that some people with stage 2 AKI may require an invasive diagnostic work up and possibly admission for intensive care and renal replacement therapy [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012].As these interventions are not available in primary care it seems prudent to discuss with a medical specialist if there is uncertainty about the cause or management.
  • The NICE GDG acknowledged that rapid referral to acute nephrology servicesof in-patients with stage 2 to stage 3 acute kidney injury not needing critical care may result in earlier detection of primary renal disease, timely treatment, and a reduction in the risk of progressive AKI, but noted a need for further research on rapid referral to nephrology services for these groups. CKS has extrapolated these comments for a primary care population and recommended discussion wih a nephrologist, if available[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]
Stage 1 AKI
  • The NICE GDG states that 'the majority of patients with acute kidney injury require management which can be delivered by a competent clinical team in primary or secondary care without the need for specialist input. This includes correction of volume status, correction of hypotension and avoidance of nephrotoxins followed by regular monitoring'. The GDG acknowledges that stage 1 AKI is very common during acute illness and small rises in creatinine often improve in line with recovery from the underlying condition and competent clinical management. Because of this, and because the large numbers of people with stage 1 AKI would overwhelm specialist services, it was the opinion of the GDG that routine referral to nephrology is not necessary for all people with stage 1 AKI (although there are exceptions)[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
Managementaccording to cause
  • This recommendation is pragmatic and based on a statement from KDIGO that 'patients should always be managed according to the cause of their disease' [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]. CKSaccepts that clinical judgement will be required in deciding whether management of a particular condition is within the scope of a practitioner's expertise, and acknowledges that acute kidney injury will frequently need to be managed in secondary care for confirmation of the underlying cause or management of complications.
Supportive measures
  • This recommendation is pragmatic and based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice.Guidance from Think Kidneys, aimed at primary healthcare practitioners,emphasises the importance of assessment of volume status (fluid intake and fluid losses) and considering the need for admissionto hospital for intravenous fluid replacement and monitoring [Think Kidneys, 2016a].
Consider stopping potentially nephrotoxic medication and adjusting doses of regular medication
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion in guidelines from KDIGO [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012]and the opinion of the NICE GDG [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]and takes into accountguidance on medicines management from Think Kidneys[Think Kidneys, 2016c; Think Kidneys, 2016d].
    • NICE identified no relevant clinical studies which compared stopping angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACE inhibitors) or angiotensin II receptor antagonists (ARBs) with continuing treatment in people with diarrhoea and vomiting or sepsis, and were unable to estimate the cost implications of stopping treatment as no economic evidence was found. However, for these people, the GDG consensus was that the risks were lower with temporarily stopping ACE inhibitors or ARBs compared with continuing. They concluded that there is a clear association with continuing the use of ACE inhibitors or ARBs and AKI, but the temporary suspension of these drugs was felt to be unlikely to greatly increase the risk of cardiovascular events[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
    • NICE did not specifically make recommendations or discuss the management of other nephrotoxic drugs in detail, but theThink Kidneys documents Acute kidney injury: potentially problematic drugs and actions to take in primary care andGuidelines for medicines optimisation in patients with acute kidney injury provide guidance for prescribers[Think Kidneys, 2016c; Think Kidneys, 2016d].CKShas pragmatically made the recommendation toseek specialist advice if unsure based on what it considers to be good clinical practice.
Regular monitoring
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion from the NICE GDG, who felt that primary care clinicians would need to use clinical judgement to determine the frequency of any monitoring. They were of the opinion that daily measurement of serum creatinine levels was common practice in acutely unwell people (although this appliedto a hospital population) and that harm is more likely fromnotrequesting measurement of renal function [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
  • The NICE GDG emphasized the importance of an awareness that modest rises in creatinine (even at the level of stage 1 AKI) are associated with increased mortality [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
Reconsidering admission or seeking specialist advice
  • This recommendation is extrapolated from the opinion of the NICE GDG who agreed that as acute kidney injury advances in severity, thereshould be an increasinglylower threshold for referral [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].

How should I follow up a person after an episode of acute kidney injury?

  • Following an episode of acute kidney injury (AKI), care should become focused on monitoring and preventionof further episodes. For more information on measures to prevent future episodes of AKI, see theScenario: Prevention of acute kidney injury.
  • If a person already has chronic kidney disease and has had oneor more episodes of AKI, refer to a nephrologist (if not already done), even if renal function returns to the person's baseline level.
  • In a person who has recovered from an episode of acute kidney injury:
    • Monitor serum creatinine. Frequency of monitoring should be based on the stability and degree of renal function at the time of discharge (if they have been in hospital).
      • Consider referral to a nephrologist when estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) is 30mL/min/1.73m2or less.
      • If there is residual renal impairment, manage according to local chronic kidney disease guidelines.For more information, see the CKStopic onChronic kidney disease.
  • Ensure that any mention of AKI in a person's hospital discharge letter is documented in their notes to alert healthcare professionals to the increased risk of further episodes of AKI and of developing chronic kidney disease.
  • Review the need for long-term medications stopped during an episode of AKI.
    • For more informationsee the Think Kidneys documentWhen or if to restart ACEI, ARB, diuretics and other antihypertensive drugsafter an episode of AKI.
  • Offer written information about AKI and its implications, for example, theleaflet on Understanding acute kidney injuryfrom Kidney Care UK.

Basis for recommendation

Focusing care on monitoring and prevention
  • This recommendation is consistent with expert opinion in a review article [Thomas and Pal, 2013]and the Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) guideline which acknowledges that in the community there are limited opportunities to intervene prior to exposure to acute kidney injury (AKI) and that most people are only seen after the event, when AKI is already apparent.However, the guideline discusses the need to identify people who are more likely to develop AKI and who will, therefore, need monitoring and supportive measures [Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes, 2012].
  • The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) lists a history of AKI as a risk factor for further episodes [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013], therefore CKSrecommends preventative measures for a person who is being followed up after an episode of AKI.
CKD and more than oneepisode of acute kidney injury
  • This recommendation is based on the expert opinion of the NICE guideline development group (GDG)[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]and is consistent with subsequent expert opinion [Levey and James, 2017]. People with acute kidney injury who already have chronic kidney disease do not have much renal reserve and are therefore at particular risk from acute kidney injury. The GDG felt that nephrologists have the expertise to manage this group of people by stabilizing renal function, and preventing further acute kidney injury episodes and advancement to end-stage renal disease [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
Monitoring serum creatinine and referral to a nephrologist
  • This recommendation is based on a consensus decision by the NICE GDG that monitoring of renal function once a person has recovered from an episode of acute kidney injury is very important. They state that a clear plan for early monitoring should be made by the hospital clinical team based on clinical judgement, reflecting the stability and degree of renal dysfunction, and that this should be communicated to primary care in the discharge letter [National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013]. The Think Kidneys documentBest practice guidance. Responding to AKI warning stage test results for adults in primary care alsoemphasises the need for a clear plan for follow up in primary care which incorporates review to ascertain the extent of renal recovery[Think Kidneys, 2016a].
  • CKS found no evidenceon the frequency of monitoring for people who have been managed in primary care (rather than in hospital), but assumes that clinical judgement will be used in this situation depending on the person's circumstances.
Management of residual renal impairment as chronic kidney disease
  • Think Kidneys, in a best practice guideline for primary care, discussed post-AKI care, including longer-term monitoring to assess for new-onset of CKD or the progression of pre-existing CKD[Think Kidneys, 2016a].This isconsistent with the opinion of the NICEGDG who noted that a person who has had an episode of AKI may be left with CKD which can often be managed in primary care. Even with mild residual renal impairment, follow up (including GFR andblood pressure measurement and urinalysis) is important in most cases[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
Documenting episodes of acute kidney injury
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion in a review article [Prescott et al, 2012].
Reviewing the need for medication stopped during the AKI episode
  • The Think Kidneys document Best practice guidance. Responding to AKI warning stage test results for adults in primary care specifically mentions post-AKI care as an important role for primary care, including medication review[Think Kidneys, 2016a].
Offering written information
  • This recommendation isin line with NICE advice to give information on treatment, monitoring, self-management and support to people who have had AKI on the basis it is likely to be beneficial and unlikely to cause harm. The guideline development group also discussed the need for longer-term information or support for people after an episode of AKI with comorbidities such as CKD[National Clinical Guideline Centre, 2013].
