Contraception - progestogen-only methods: Scenario: Progestogen-only injectables

Last revised in November 2019

Covers the use of the progestogen-only injectable, including the available products; when to start it; the efficacy; how to manage common problems (such as late injection); the advantages, disadvantages, risks, possible adverse effects and key drug interactions; and follow up requirements for this method of contraception.

Scenario: Progestogen-only injectables

From age 13 years to 60 years (Female).

Starting a progestogen-only injectable

How should I assess a woman before providing her with a progestogen-only injectable?

  • For information on assessing a woman considering starting a progestogen-only injectable, see the section on Progestogen-only injectable in the CKS topic on Contraception - assessment.

When should I start a progestogen-only injectable?

  • If the woman is not currently using a regular method of contraception:
    • Ideally, give the first injection up to, and including day 5 after the start of menstrual bleeding. 
      • No additional contraception is required.
    • If this is not possible, give the progestogen-only injectable at any other time in the menstrual cycle provided it is reasonably certain that the woman is not pregnant and there is no risk of conception.
      • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days.
  • If the woman is starting the injectable after oral emergency contraception:
    • Ideally offer a bridging method of contraception (such as combined hormonal contraception or progestogen-only pills) until pregnancy can be excluded. However if this is not suitable or acceptable:
      • For levonorgestrel, give the progestogen-only injectable and advise the woman to avoid sexual intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days.
      • For ulipristal acetate, give the injection 5 days after taking ulipristal acetate, and advise the woman to avoid sexual intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days.
    • Advise the woman to take a pregnancy test no sooner than 3 weeks after the most recent episode of unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI).
  • If the woman is changing from a combined hormonal contraceptive (CHC):
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable on day 1 of the hormone-free interval.
      • No additional contraception is required.
    • If the injection is given on week 1 of normal CHC use: 
      • Advise the woman to abstain from sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days. 
      • If UPSI has occurred after day 3 of the hormone-free interval, advise the woman to restart the CHC method for at least 7 days.
    • If the injection is given on weeks 2–3 of normal CHC use: 
      • No additional contraception is required if the CHC method has been used consistently and correctly for the previous 7 days.
  • If the woman is changing from the progestogen-only pill (POP) or levonorgestrel intrauterine system (LNG-IUS):
    • The progestogen-only injectable can be given at any time in the menstrual cycle.
    • Advise the woman to abstain from sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days, or continue the existing method for 7 days, regardless of when in the menstrual cycle the injection is given.
  • If the woman is changing from another injectable progestogen-only contraceptive:
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable on or before the day the next injection is due.
      • No additional contraception is required.
  • If the woman is changing from a progestogen-only implant:
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable up to 3 years after insertion of the implant.
      • No additional contraception is required.
    • If the injection is given more than 3 years after insertion of the implant: 
      • Advise the woman to abstain from sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days after the injection.
      • Consider the need for emergency contraception or a pregnancy test (no sooner than 3 weeks after the most recent UPSI) if pregnancy cannot be excluded. For more information, see the CKS topic on Contraception - emergency.
  • If the woman is changing from a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms), or a copper intrauterine device (Cu-IUD):
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable on day 1–5 of the menstrual cycle.
      • No additional contraception is required.
    • If the progestogen-only injectable is given at any other time in the menstrual cycle:
      • Advise the woman to abstain from sex for 7 days, or continue the existing method for at least 7 days.

What types of progestogen-only injectables are available?

  • Three progestogen-only injectables are currently available in the UK:
    • Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate 150 mg (Depo-Provera®) is the most commonly used progestogen-only injectable and is licensed for long-term use.
      • It is given every 12 weeks by deep intramuscular injection.
    • Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate 104 mg (Sayana Press®) is also licensed for long-term use.
      • It is given every 13 weeks by subcutaneous injection.
      • Women may self-administer Sayana Press® after the first injection once they have received training in injection technique and the administration schedule from a healthcare professional.
    • Norethisterone enantate, 200 mg (Noristerat®) is rarely used and is only licensed for short-term use (two injections).
      • It is given every 8 weeks apart by deep intramuscular injection.

How do progestogen-only injectables work?

  • Progestogen-only injectables (depot medroxyprogesterone acetate and norethisterone enantate) prevent pregnancy by the inhibition of ovulation and thickening of the cervical mucus, thereby presenting a barrier for sperm penetration.
  • In addition, changes to the endometrium make it an unfavourable environment for implantation.

Which progestogen-only injectable should I recommend?

  • If the woman requires contraception for only a few months (short-term use), consider either:
    • Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (Depo-Provera® or Sayana Press®), or
    • Norethisterone enantate (Noristerat®).
  • If the woman requires long-term contraception, consider:
    • Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (Depo-Provera® or Sayana Press®).
      • Women may self-administer Sayana Press® after the first injection once they have received training in injection technique and the administration schedule from a healthcare professional.

Amenorrhoea, postpartum, termination of pregnancy, or miscarriage

  • If the woman is amenorrhoeic:
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable at any time if it is reasonably certain the woman is not pregnant. 
      • Advise the woman to abstain from sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days.
  • If the woman is postpartum (whether breastfeeding or not):
    • Give the progestogen-only injectable before day 21 postpartum:
      • No additional contraception is required.
    • If the progestogen-only injectable is given on or after day 21 postpartum:
      • Advise the woman to avoid sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days, unless menstruation has started and the injection has been given on days 1–5.
  • If the woman has had a miscarriage or termination of pregnancy:
    • If gestation is less than 24 weeks, ideally give the progestogen-only injectable immediately after a miscarriage, surgical termination, or the first part of a medical termination.
      • If the progestogen-only injectable is given within 5 days of miscarriage or termination, no additional contraception is required.
      • If the progestogen-only injectable is given more than 5 days after miscarriage or termination, advise the woman to abstain from sex or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days.
    • If gestation is 24 weeks or more, treat as for a woman who is postpartum.

What information and advice should I give a woman who is considering using a progestogen-only injectable?

  • Discuss:
    • The mechanism of action of the progestogen-only injectable.
    • The advantages, disadvantages, and risks of the progestogen-only injectable.
    • The efficacy of the progestogen-only injectable.
    • What happens when the progestogen-only injectable is stopped.
      • Advise the woman that there could be a delay of up to 1 year in the return of normal fertility. Menstruation can take several months to return to normal.
  • Give advice on:
    • The possible adverse effects (such as menstrual irregularities) of the progestogen-only injectable. 
    • How to manage a late repeat of the injection.
    • The link between Human papilloma virus (HPV) and cervical cancer, as well as strategies to reduce that risk. For more information see the CKS topic on Cervical cancer and HPV.
    • The need for routine cervical cytology screening. 
    • The possibility of drug interactions.
  • Also provide written information on the progestogen-only injectable.
    • The Family Planning Association provides a useful leaflet with information for users of the progestogen-only injectable. 

What should I do if the repeat injection is late?

  • If it has up to 14 weeks since the last injection of depot medroxyprogesterone or 10 weeks for norethisterone enantate was given:
    • Give the repeat injection.
    • No additional contraception is needed.
  • If it has been more than 14 weeks since the the last injection of depot medroxyprogesterone or 10 weeks for norethisterone enantate was given and:
    • Unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI) has occurred within the last 5 days:
      • Offer emergency contraception. For more information, see the CKS topic on Contraception - emergency.
      • Bridging methods such as progestogen-only pills (POPs) or combined hormonal contraception (CHC) are preferred, but the repeat injection can be considered.
      • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for the next 7 days, and to take a pregnancy test no sooner than 3 weeks after the last episode of UPSI. F
    • UPSI has occurred more than 5 days ago and within the last 5 days: 
      • Offer oral emergency hormonal contraception.
      • Bridging methods such as POPs or CHC are preferred, but the repeat injection can be considered.
      • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for the next 7 days, and to take a pregnancy test no sooner than 3 weeks after the last episode of UPSI. 
    • UPSI has occurred more than 5 days ago, but within the last 3 weeks:
      • Do not offer emergency contraception.
      • Bridging methods such as POPs or CHC are preferred, but the repeat injection can be considered.
      • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for the next 7 days, and to take a pregnancy test no sooner than 3 weeks after the last episode of UPSI. 
    • UPSI has occurred more than 3 weeks ago:
      • Do not offer emergency contraception.
      • Perform a pregnancy test, and administer the progestogen-only injectable if this is negative.
      • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for the next 7 days.
  • If the timing of the previous depot medroxyprogesterone acetate injection is unknown
    • Repeat the injection, provided it is reasonably certain that the woman is not pregnant.
    • Advise the woman to avoid intercourse or use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) for 7 days after the injection.

How should I manage a woman who becomes pregnant whilst using the progestogen-only injectable?

  • If a woman is found to be pregnant whilst using the progestogen-only injectable, reassure her that there is no evidence of harm to the fetus or the mother.

What are the advantages and disadvantages of progestogen-only injectables?

This information relates primarily to depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) and is assumed to apply to norethisterone enantate (NET-EN).

Advantages

  • It is very effective.
  • Users do not have to think about contraception for as long as the injection lasts — 12 weeks for DMPA and 8 weeks for NET-EN.
  • Sex need not be interrupted to use contraception.
  • It can be used when breastfeeding.
  • There are no known interactions with any medications (including liver enzyme-inducing drugs).
  • It may reduce heavy painful periods and help with premenstrual symptoms.
  • It can be used in situations where combined hormonal contraceptives are not recommended. 
  • It can be used by women with a body mass index (BMI) greater than 35 kg/m2 (but may cause further weight gain).
  • It may reduce the risk of ovarian and endometrial cancer.

Disadvantages

  • It is not rapidly reversible. After stopping use of progestogen-only injectables:
    • There could be a delay of up to 1 year in the return of normal fertility.
    • Menstruation can take several months to return to normal.
  • It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
  • Adverse effects including unscheduled bleeding and weight changes may occur. 

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) clinical guideline, Progestogen-only injectables [FSRH, 2014b] and the National Institute for Heath and Care Excellence (NICE) guidance Long-acting reversible contraception [NICE, 2014].

What are the possible risks and adverse effects of progestogen-only injectables?

  • Menstrual irregularities — for more information, see the section on Unscheduled bleeding.
  • Weight gain — depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) is associated with weight gain — up to 2–3 kg in weight over the first year of use.
    • A higher initial body mass index (BMI) is predictive of weight gain in adolescents.
    • Women who experience a weight gain of more than 5% in the first 6 months are likely to experience continued weight gain.
    • If a woman using the progestogen-only injectable presents with a weight gain:
      • Advise her that weight gain is common with DMPA use.
      • Exclude pregnancy.
      • Offer lifestyle advice (diet and exercise).
    • If weight gain is severe, consider:
      • Referring to a dietitian.
      • Investigating for other causes of severe weight gain (such as eating disorders, depression, or thyroid disease).
  • Cancer risk
    • There is a possible increased risk of breast cancer. However, this risk is very small compared with the overall risk of developing breast cancer and gradually disappears within 10 years after stopping the progestogen-only injectable.
      • For more information about the risks, which are the same as for progestogen-only pills, see the section on Adverse effects in the Scenario: Progestogen-only pill.
    • There is a weak association between cervical cancer and use of DMPA for 5 years or longer. Any increased risk is reduced with time after stopping.
      • Ensure women requesting DMPA are undergoing routine cervical screening.
  • Effect on bone mineral density (BMD) — the use of DMPA is associated with a small loss of BMD with long-term use (more than 1 year). However, this is usually recovered after stopping the injection, and there is no evidence that DMPA increases the risk of fracture.
    • If a woman wishes to use DMPA long-term, review the woman every 2 years to assess the risks and benefits of the injection and to decide whether treatment can be continued.
    • Consider other methods of contraception for:
      • Women younger than 18 years of age (consider the DMPA injection only if all other methods of contraception are unsuitable or unacceptable).
      • Women with significant risk factors for osteoporosis (for example long-term corticosteroid use). For more information, see the CKS topic on Osteoporosis - prevention of fragility fractures.
  • Injection site reactions
    • These are more common with subcutaneous (Sayana Press®) than intramuscular DMPA (Depo Provera®), and includes pain, abscess, and scarring.
  • There is little evidence that the use of the progestogen-only injectable causes the following reported adverse effects:
    • Mood changes.
    • Loss of libido.
    • Acne.
    • Headache.
    • Hot flushes
    • Vaginitis.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) clinical guidelines, Progestogen-only injectables [FSRH, 2014b] and Problematic bleeding with hormonal contraception [FSRH, 2015c], and the National Institute for Heath and Care Excellence (NICE) guidance Long-acting reversible contraception [NICE, 2014] and Suspected cancer: recognition and referral [NICE, 2015].

Progestogen-only injectables and cervical cancer risk
  • A pooled analysis from two case-control studies suggested a possible increased risk of cervical cancer with use for 5 years or more (RR 1.2, 95% CI 1.0–1.6) [FSRH, 2014b].
  • A re-analysis of epidemiological data also found a small significant increased risk of invasive cervical cancer (RR 1.22, 95% CI 1.01–1.46) in women who had used depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) for more than 5 (mean 8.8) years [FSRH, 2014b].

How should I manage unscheduled bleeding in women using the progestogen-only injectable?

  •  Exclude or manage situations which could result in unscheduled bleeding, such as:
    • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) — as a minimum, test for Chlamydia trachomatis. The risk of STIs is increased if the woman is under 25 years, has a new sexual partner, or has had more than one sexual partner in the last year. For more information, see the CKS topic on Chlamydia - uncomplicated genital.
    • Pregnancy — perform a pregnancy test.
    • Gynaecological conditions such as cervical cancer — if this is suspected, refer women the woman using a suspected cancer pathway referral (for an appointment within 2 weeks). For more information, see the CKS topic on Gynaecological cancers - recognition and referral.
    • Endometrial cancer — refer women aged 55 and older using a suspected cancer pathway referral (for an appointment within 2 weeks) if they have menstrual irregularities. Refer women younger than 55 years if they have risk factors for endometrial cancer and persistent, problematic bleeding after the first 3 months of use, or if they present with a change in bleeding pattern after at least 3 months of use.
      For more information, see the CKS topic on Gynaecological cancers - recognition and referral.
    • Drug interactions.
  •  Consider performing a pelvic and speculum examination:
    • For persistent bleeding beyond the first 3 months of use — unscheduled bleeding is common in the first 3 months of starting a new hormonal contraceptive method.
    • For new symptoms or a change in bleeding after at least 3 months of use.
    • If the woman has not participated in the National Cervical Screening programme regularly. For more information, see the CKS topic on Cervical cancer and HPV.
    • If requested by the woman.
    • If there are other symptoms such as pelvic pain, dyspareunia, or post coital bleeding. 
  • If no other underlying cause of irregular bleeding is suspected, and speculum and pelvic examination is normal, the bleeding can be assumed to be caused by the progestogen-only injectable.
    • Reassure the woman that bleeding disturbances are common and that:
      • Around 1 in 10 women may be amenorrhoeic in the first 3 months of use.
      • Rates of amenorrhoea increase with duration of use.
      • Around 50% or more of women are amenorrhoeic at 12 months.
    • Continue with the injectable if the woman is happy to continue.
  • However, if bleeding is problematic, consider treating with:
    • A combined oral contraceptive (30–35 micrograms of ethinylestradiol with levonorgestrel or norethisterone) either cyclically or continuously for up to 3 months provided it is not contraindicated (off-label use). For more information, see the Scenario: Combined oral contraceptive pill in the CKS topic on Contraception - combined hormonal methods.
    • Mefenamic acid 500 mg two or three times a day (or tranexamic acid 1 g four times a day) for 5 days. For more information, see the section on Prescribing information in the CKS topic on Menorrhagia.
    • Changing to another contraceptive method. For more information, see the CKS topic on Contraception - assessment.
  • Be aware that: 
    • Some women experience prolonged amenorrhoea; this is most likely in older women and is usually acceptable to the woman.
    • Amenorrhea is more likely with depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) than norethisterone enantate (NET-EN).
    • Irregular bleeding may continue for some months after stopping the injection.
  • There is no evidence that reducing the injection interval for DMPA improves bleeding.

What advice should I give regarding potential drug interactions with the progestogen-only injectable?

  • The efficacy of the progestogen-only injectable is not reduced by liver enzyme-inducing drugs. 
  • Ulipristal acetate has the potential to reduce the efficacy of hormonal contraception.
    • Advise women to start the progestogen-only injectable 5 days after taking ulipristal acetate and to use a barrier method of contraception (such as condoms) during this time and for the next 7 days. For more information see the CKS topic on Contraception - emergency.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) clinical guidelines Progestogen-only injectables [FSRH, 2014b], and Drug interactions with hormonal contraception [FSRH, 2012], and a statement from the FSRH clinical effectiveness unit [FSRH, 2015b].

How effective are progestogen-only injectables at preventing pregnancy?

  • For comparison of the efficacy of progestogen-only injectables with other methods of contraception, see the CKS topic on Contraception - assessment.
    • When used perfectly (consistently and correctly), 0.2% of women will conceive within the first year of use due to method failure.
    • When used typically, 6% of women will conceive within the first year of use due to method failure or user failure.

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) clinical guidelines Progestogen-only injectables [FSRH, 2014b].

What follow up should I arrange for a woman using a progestogen-only injectable?

  • At the follow up visit, when the woman attends for a repeat injection:
    • Check her knowledge of what to do if she is late for injections.
    • Address any issues or adverse effects she might have, such as weight gain and irregular menstrual bleeding.
    • Advise the woman to return if she experiences signs of infection at the site of the injection (for example redness, swelling, pain, or rash). For more information, see the CKS topic on Cellulitis - acute.
  • Review the woman every 2 years to assess the risks and benefits of the injection and to decide whether treatment can be continued.

How long can the progestogen-only injection be used for?

Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (Depo-Provera® or Sayana Press®)

  • No maximum duration of use is suggested in the Summaries of Product Characteristics.
  • Review women using depot medroxyprogesterone acetate every 2 years to discuss the benefits and potential risks of continuing this method of contraception.
  • When the woman reaches 50 years of age, stop depot medroxyprogesterone acetate and switch to one of the following methods:
    • A non-hormonal method of contraception (the copper intrauterine device or barrier methods).
    • An alternative hormonal method of contraception (the progestogen-only pill, the progestogen-only implant, or the levonorgestrel intrauterine system).

Norethisterone enantate (Noristerat®)

  • Norethisterone enantate is only licensed for up to two injections, given 8 weeks apart.

How often should I repeat the progestogen-only injection?

  • For Depo-Provera® (depot medroxyprogesterone acetate):
    • Repeat the injection every 12 weeks.
    • However, if this is not possible, repeat injections can also be given up to 2 weeks early (every 10 weeks) or up to 2 weeks late (every 14 weeks).
  • For Sayana Press® (depot medroxyprogesterone acetate):
    • Repeat the injection every 13 weeks.
    • However, if this is not possible, repeat injections can also be given up to 1 week late (every 14 weeks).
  • For Noristerat® (norethisterone enantate):
    • Repeat the injection after 8 weeks. 
      • Be aware that norethisterone enantate is licensed for up to two injections only, 8 weeks apart.
    • However, if this is not possible, a repeat injection can also be given up to 2 weeks early (6 weeks apart) or up to 2 weeks late (10 weeks apart).

Basis for recommendation

These recommendations are based on the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) clinical guidelines Progestogen-only injectables [FSRH, 2014b], Contraception for women aged over 40 years [FSRH, 2010b] and the manufacturers' Summaries of Product Characteristics [ABPI, 2015b; ABPI, 2016d; ABPI, 2016e].
