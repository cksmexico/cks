Scenario: Management

From age 30 years onwards (Male).

How is prostate cancer managed? 

  • Treatment for prostate cancer will be initiated and managed in secondary care.
    • Follow up and monitoring, as well as early recognition and initial management of the complications of the condition and adverse effects of its treatment can be carried out in primary care under shared care arrangements. 
  • Management options in secondary care depend on the man's prognostic risk, which is determined after taking into account the: 
    • Clinical staging.
    • Results of the prostate-specific antigen (PSA) test.
    • Gleason score.
  • Other considerations include age and comorbidities, as well as the man's preferences. 
  • Treatments options for prostate cancer include: 
    • Watchful waiting or active surveillance.
    • Radical treatments, such as radical prostatectomy, external beam radiotherapy, or brachytherapy.
      • High intensity focused ultrasound or cryotherapy should not be used except in controlled clinical trials comparing their use with established interventions.
    • Adjunctive and palliative treatments, such as hormone therapy, strontium-89 therapy, radium-223 therapy, chemotherapy, or bisphosphonates.

What is the Gleason score?

  • When the prostate is biopsied, 10–12 cores will be taken from different parts of the gland.
  • The commonest and second most common tumour patterns are analysed and graded from 1 to 5.
  • The Gleason score is the sum of these two grades and can range from 2 to 10. 
  • The tumour grade is classified into either of three risk categories on the basis of the Gleason score:
    • Low: 6 or less. 
    • Intermediate: 7.
    • High: 8–10.

What are the treatment options for localized prostate cancer?

  • For men with: 
    • Low risk — active surveillance can be offered. Other treatments that can be considered include:
      • Watchful waiting, radical prostatectomy, or radical radiotherapy (external-beam radiotherapy [EBR], or brachytherapy).
    • Intermediate risk — radical prostatectomy, or radical radiotherapy with 6 months of androgen deprivation therapy (before, during or after radiotherapy) can be offered (rather than radical radiotherapy or androgen therapy alone).
      • Other treatments that can be considered include watchful waiting, active surveillance, or high-dose rate brachytherapy in combination with EBR.
    • High risk — radical prostatectomy, or radical radiotherapy with 6 months of androgen deprivation therapy (before, during or after radiotherapy) can be offered (rather than radical radiotherapy or androgen therapy alone) if there is a realistic prospect of long-term disease control. Androgen deprivation therapy may be continued for up to 3 years.
      • Other treatments that can be considered include watchful waiting or high-dose rate brachytherapy in combination with EBR.
      • Active surveillance is not appropriate.
    • High-intensity focused ultrasonography (HIFU) and cryotherapy should not be considered except as part of a clinical trial comparing use with established interventions.

Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016] and the European Association of Urology (EAU) guideline Prostate cancer [EAU, 2017].

Watchful waiting/active surveillance

What is watchful waiting, and when is it an appropriate treatment option for prostate cancer?

  • During watchful waiting the man is followed up regularly in primary care — he will be monitored for disease progression in order to avoid treatment unless symptoms appear.
    • This includes regular clinical assessments and repeat prostate-specific antigen (PSA) testing, but not repeated digital rectal examination or prostate biopsies. 
  • Watchful waiting is often suitable for older men, or men with significant comorbidities, or slowly progressing tumours who are likely to die of other causes and not suffer significant morbidity from their prostate cancer. It is an option for men in any prognostic risk group.
  • If symptoms develop (for example bone pain), or PSA levels rise rapidly, the man should be reviewed by the urological cancer service to consider starting palliative treatment. 
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014] and the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016].

What is active surveillance, and when is it an appropriate treatment option for prostate cancer?

  • Active surveillance involves frequent serial prostate-specific antigen (PSA) tests, digital rectal examinations, and prostate biopsies. See Table 4 for a surveillance protocol.
    • The difference from watchful waiting is that repeating the prostate biopsy at intervals depending on age and PSA levels allows the man's prognostic risk category to reassessed. The aim of active surveillance is to safely reduce the risk of over treatment, as treatment is offered only when the risk increases.
  • If there is evidence of disease progression, the man should be offered radical treatment. His decision should be informed by his preferences, comorbidities, and life expectancy.
  • Active surveillance is the preferred option for men with low prognostic risk who are suitable for radical treatment in the event of disease progression. It is an option for men at intermediate risk who do not wish to have immediate radical prostatectomy or radical radiotherapy. It is not recommended for men at high risk.

Radical treatments

What is radical prostatectomy, and when is it appropriate treatment for prostate cancer?

  • The aim of radical prostatectomy is to remove the entire prostate gland and eradicate the disease. It can be performed by open, laparoscopic, or robot-assisted surgery.
  • Risks associated with radical prostatectomy include urinary incontinence, erectile dysfunction, and incomplete resection of the tumour.
    • Complete tumour removal is not always achieved, and approximately 20% of men develop biochemical or clinical recurrence of prostate cancer.
  • Radical prostatectomy is an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localized prostate cancer — it can be considered in men at low prognostic risk, and is a preferred option for men at intermediate risk and those at high risk who have a realistic chance of gaining long-term control of the disease.
      • It is not common to offer it to men older than 70 years of age.
    • Biochemical relapse after radical radiotherapy — an option for men who have biochemical relapse with increasing prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels after radical radiotherapy. However, the risk of incontinence, impotence, and rectal damage is higher than when it is used as primary treatment.
    • Locally advanced prostate cancer — surgery alone does not normally eradicate this, and the rate of progression of the disease varies considerably. Men normally receive radiotherapy or hormone therapy.
      • Men with locally advanced prostate cancer should not be offered immediate post-operative radiotherapy or adjuvant hormonal therapy except as part of a clinical trial.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016] and the European Association of Urology (EAU) guideline Prostate cancer [EAU, 2017].

What is external-beam radiotherapy, and when is it an appropriate treatment for prostate cancer?

  • External-beam radiotherapy (EBRT) directs an external source of radiation at the tumour from outside the body.
    • Intensity modulated radiotherapy (IMRT) is the accepted best standard for EBRT.
  • Adverse effects of EBRT short-term include bowel and bladder problems. Long-term complications include erectile dysfunction and urinary problems. 
    • There is a small increased risk of colorectal cancer after EBRT.
  • EBRT is an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localized prostate cancer — it can be considered for men at low prognostic risk, and is a preferred option for men at intermediate risk and those at high risk (who have a realistic chance of gaining long-term control of the disease) in combination with 6 months of androgen deprivation therapy (before, during or after EBRT), rather than either therapy alone.
      • For men with high risk localised prostate cancer, androgen deprivation therapy may be continued for up to 3 years.  
      • EBRT may be combined with high-dose rate (HDR) brachytherapy for men with intermediate or high risk. 
    • Biochemical relapse after radical prostatectomy — radiotherapy to the prostate bed is recommended for men who experience biochemical relapse with increasing prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels after radical prostatectomy, provided that they do not have metastases.
    • Locally advanced disease — EBRT may be used in combination with HDR brachytherapy.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014] the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016] and the European Association of Urology (EAU) guideline Prostate cancer [EAU, 2017].

What is brachytherapy, and when is it appropriate treatment for men with prostate cancer?

  • Brachytherapy is a type of radiotherapy in which the radioactive source is implanted in the prostate.
    • Low-dose rate (LDR) brachytherapy involves permanent implantation with radioactive seeds. 
    • High-dose rate (HDR) brachytherapy involves insertion of fine catheters, through which a radioactive source is temporarily passed. 
      • The overall dose for HDR is less than LDR, so it is given in conjunction with external beam radiotherapy (EBRT). 
  • Adverse effects include alteration of urinary and bowel function, and erectile dysfunction.
  • Brachytherapy is an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localized prostate cancer — for men at low prognostic risk and for men at intermediate or high risk, HDR brachytherapy in combination with EBRT.
    • Locally advanced prostate cancer — HDR brachytherapy in combination with EBRT.
  • Brachytherapy should not be used alone for men with high-risk localised prostate cancer.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], Suspected cancer: recognition and referral [NICE, 2015a], High dose rate brachytherapy in combination with external-beam radiotherapy for localised prostate cancer [NICE, 2006], and the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016].

What is high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), and when is it used to treat prostate cancer?

  • High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) uses ultrasound waves to heat the prostate gland and destroy the tissue.
  • Adverse effects of HIFU include erectile dysfunction, urinary incontinence, and rectal damage. 
  • HIFU is not recommended except as part of a clinical trial in which it is compared with established interventions.
  • HIFU may be an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localized cancer of the prostate.
    • Biochemical relapse after radiotherapy for prostate cancer. 
    • Locally advanced disease.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014] and High-intensity focused ultrasound for prostate cancer [NICE, 2005a] and the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016].

What is cryotherapy, and when is it used to treat prostate cancer?

  • Cryotherapy is used to destroy the prostate tissue by freezing.
  • Adverse effects of cryotherapy include erectile dysfunction, urinary incontinence, and rectal damage.
  • Cryotherapy is not recommended except as part of a clinical trial in which it is compared with established interventions.
  • Cryotherapy may be an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localized cancer of the prostate.
    • Biochemical relapse after radical radiotherapy for prostate cancer.
    • Locally advanced prostate cancer.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], Cryotherapy for recurrent prostate cancer [NICE, 2005b], Cryotherapy as a primary treatment for prostate cancer [NICE, 2005c], and the Public Health England (PHE) guidance Prostate cancer risk management programme (PCRMP): benefits and risks of PSA testing [PHE, 2016].

Adjunctive and palliative treatments

What hormonal treatment are used for prostate cancer, and when are they indicated?

  • Hormonal treatments used for prostate cancer include:
    • Androgen withdrawal — surgery (bilateral orchidectomy) or medical, with luteinizing hormone-releasing hormone (LHRH) agonists (such as goserelin, leuprorelin, triptorelin), or antagonists (such as degarelix). 
    • Androgen blockade — with drugs that bind to and block the hormone receptors of cancer cells (for example, cyproterone acetate), thus preventing androgens from stimulating cancer growth.
  • Hormonal treatment may be given at different times.
    • Neoadjuvant therapy is given for several months before radical treatment with surgery or radiotherapy.
    • Concurrent therapy is given at the same time as radiotherapy.
    • Adjuvant therapy is given after radical treatment with surgery or radiotherapy.
  • Hormonal treatment may be given continuously, or intermittently. 
  • The adverse effects of hormonal therapies include erectile dysfunction, loss of libido, breast swelling, hot flushes, and osteoporosis.
  • Hormone therapy is an appropriate treatment option for:
    • Localised prostate cancer — it can be used before, during or after treatment with radical radiotherapy for men at intermediate, or high risk. 
    • Biochemical relapse after radical prostatectomy or radiotherapy — it should not be used routinely unless the man has symptomatic local disease progression, proven metastases, or his prostate-specific antigen (PSA) level has doubled in less than 3 months.
    • Locally advanced prostate cancer
      • Neoadjuvant treatment is started 2–3 months before men receive radical radiotherapy for locally advanced prostate cancer. In practice, adjuvant therapy is started at the onset of radiotherapy, and it is recommended that it is continued for 2–3 years (rather than short-term, 6 months). 
      • Degarelix is an LHRH antagonist, which is licensed for treatment of men with advanced hormone-dependent prostate cancer.
    • Metastatic prostate cancer
      • Bilateral orchidectomy and continuous LHRH agonist therapy are options, depending on the man's preferences. 
      • Anti-androgen monotherapy with bicalutamide may be the preferred option for men who hope to retain sexual function and are willing to accept the risk of reduced life expectancy and the possibility of gynaecomastia.
      • Abiraterone (an androgen synthesis inhibitor) in combination with prednisone or prednisolone is an option for treating metastatic hormone-relapsed prostate cancer in men who have no, or mild, symptoms after androgen deprivation therapy has failed, and before chemotherapy is indicated, and if their disease has progressed on or after one docetaxel-containing chemotherapy regimen.
      • Enzalutamide (an androgen receptor antagonist) is an option for treating metastatic hormone-relapsed prostate cancer in people who have no, or mild, symptoms after androgen deprivation therapy has failed, and before chemotherapy is indicated.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], NICE health technology appraisals Abiraterone for treating metastatic hormone-relapsed prostate cancer before chemotherapy is indicated [NICE, 2016b], Abiraterone for castration-resistant metastatic prostate cancer previously treated with a docetaxel-containing regimen [NICE, 2017], and Enzalutamide for treating metastatic hormone-relapsed prostate cancer before chemotherapy is indicated [NICE, 2016c], the European Association of Urology (EAU) guideline Prostate cancer [EAU, 2017] and the British National Formulary [BNF 73, 2017].

What is strontium-89 therapy, and when is it used for prostate cancer?

  • The radioactive isotope of strontium, Sr-89, is an option for men with hormone-relapsed prostate cancer and painful bone metastases.
  • Strontium-89 is given intravenously and may be used as adjunctive treatment together with other therapies (for example, androgen deprivation therapy).
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014].

What is Radium–223 therapy, and when is it used for prostate cancer?

  • Radium–223 dichloride is a radiopharmaceutical agent designed to deliver alpha radiation to bone metastases without affecting normal bone marrow.
  • It is an option for treating hormone-relapsed prostate cancer, and symptomatic bone metastases (with no known visceral metastases) in men who have already had docetaxel, or in whom docetaxel is contraindicated or unsuitable.
  • It is administered by intravenous injection every 4 weeks for 6 injections.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) technology appraisal Radium-223 dichloride for treating hormone-relapsed prostate cancer with bone metastases [NICE, 2016d].

What chemotherapy is used for prostate cancer, and when is it offered?

  • Chemotherapy is an option for men with hormone-relapsed metastatic disease. 
  • Docetaxel
    • Docetaxel in combination with prednisolone is licensed for hormone-resistant metastatic prostate cancer.
  • Cabazitaxel
    • Cabazitaxel in combination with prednisone or prednisolone is an option for treating metastatic hormone-relapsed prostate cancer in people whose disease has progressed during or after docetaxel.
  • Corticosteroids
    • A corticosteroid, such as dexamethasone, is an option as third-line therapy after androgen withdrawal and anti-androgen therapy for men with hormone-refractory prostate cancer.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014], the British Medical Journal (BMJ) best practice guide Prostate cancer [BMJ, 2017], the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) guideline Systemic therapy in men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer [ASCO, 2014] and the NICE technology appraisal Cabazitaxel for hormone-relapsed metastatic prostate cancer treated with docetaxel [NICE, 2016e].

When are bisphosphonates used in men with prostate cancer?

  • Offer bisphosphonates to men taking androgen deprivation therapy who have osteoporosis. 
    • Do not offer bisphosphonates routinely to prevent osteoporosis in men with prostate cancer taking androgen deprivation therapy.
  • Consider bisphosphonates for pain relief in men with hormone-refractory prostate cancer when other treatments (including analgesics and palliative radiotherapy) have failed.
  • Do not offer bisphosphonates to prevent bone metastases or to reduce their complications.
Basis for recommendation

This information is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Prostate cancer: diagnosis and management [NICE, 2014].

What information and support should I give men with prostate cancer? 

  • Discuss all relevant management options with men, and if they wish, their partners or carers, irrespective of whether they are available through local services. This should include:
    • The risks and benefits of treatment, and that there is limited evidence for some treatment options.
    • The short- and long-term adverse effects of treatment and when and how to report them. 
    • The follow up required, including the purpose, duration, frequency, and location (hospital or primary care).
  • Offer men with prostate cancer advice on how to access information and support from websites, local and national cancer information services, and from cancer support groups, including:
