Scenario: Diarrhoea - antibiotic associated

From age 18 years onwards.

How should I assess someone presenting with suspected antibiotic-associated diarrhoea?

  • Assess the severity of the symptoms and consider whether hospital admission is appropriate.
    • For more information on assessing symptoms (including the risk of dehydration) and when to admit, see the CKS topic on Diarrhoea - adult's assessment.
  • Exclude other potential causes of diarrhoea or contributing factors.
    • For more information, see the CKS topic on Diarrhoea - adult's assessment.
  • Check which antibiotics were prescribed and the duration of treatment.
    • Clostridium difficile infection is more common with certain antibiotics (for more information, see Risk factors).
  • Consider the possibility of C. difficile infection.
    • There are no clinical symptoms that are specific for C. difficile infection.
    • However, suspect the possibility of C. difficile infection (particularly if the person is elderly) if risk factors are present.
      • For information on assessing the severity of C. difficile infection, see Severity of C. difficile.
    • Check for any history of C. difficile infection — as the rate of recurrence is high.
    • Send a stool sample to test for C. difficile (for further information, see Testing).
    • Check whether other cases of C. difficile infection have been reported recently — for example, within the care home or in the hospital ward from which the individual has been recently discharged. C. difficile-associated disease can occur in outbreaks.

Severity of Clostridium difficile

Severity of Clostridium difficile infection can be defined as [Public Health England, 2013]:

  • Mild: not associated with an increased white cell count (WCC). It is typically associated with less than three episodes of loose stools (defined as loose enough to take the shape of the container used to sample it) per day.
  • Moderate: associated with an increased WCC (but less than 15 x 109/L) and typically associated with 3–5 loose stools per day.
  • Severe: associated with a WCC greater than 15 x 109/L, or an acutely increased serum creatinine concentration (that is, greater than 50% increase above baseline), or a temperature higher than 38.5°C, or evidence of severe colitis (abdominal or radiological signs). The number of stools may be a less reliable indicator of severity.
  • Life-threatening: signs and symptoms include hypotension, partial or complete ileus, toxic megacolon, or computerised tomography (CT) evidence of severe disease.

Basis for recommendation

Assess severity of symptoms and consider hospital admission
  • The recommendation to assess severity of symptoms is based on guidance issued by Public Health England on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection as this will influence treatment decisions [Public Health England, 2013]. A North American practice parameter on C. difficile infection also advises a thorough evaluation to determine severity of symptoms and identify sepsis [Steele et al, 2015]. In some people with C. difficile infection, dehydration, shock, hypotension, tachycardia, and peritoneal signs may be present [Ong et al, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018; Hassoun, 2018]. 
  • The recommendation on considering hospital admission is extrapolated from information in review articles discussing when specialist opinion, interventions, or surgery may be required for C. difficile infection based on the severity of symptoms [Kazanowski et al, 2014; Ong et al, 2017; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
Exclude other potential causes of diarrhoea or contributing factors
  • This recommendation reflects guidance issued by Public Health England and Health Protection Scotland covering the prevention, management, and treatment of C. difficile infection [Public Health England, 2013; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. Expert opinion in a review article also advises considering other infectious and non-infectious causes if a person has diarrhoea and negative tests for C. difficile [Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
Check which antibiotics were prescribed
  • This recommendation is consistent with information in a NICE evidence summary on the risk of C. difficile with broad-spectrum antibiotics [NICE, 2015b], Public Health England diagnostic guidance [PHE, 2018a], and North American practice parameters [Steele et al, 2015]. 
  • The most frequently implicated antibiotics include broad-spectrum penicillins, cephalosporins, clindamycin, and flouroquinolones, but most antibiotics have been associated with C. difficile infection [Leffler and Lamont, 2015; Steele et al, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017]. The risk of C. difficile infection is increased with greater number of antimicrobials used, higher doses, and longer duration of treatment [Winslow et al, 2014; Trubiano et al, 2016]. 
Consider the possibility of C. difficile infection
  • CKS has advised considering the possibility of C. difficile infection in a person with antibiotic-associated diarrhoea based on guidance issued by Public Health England on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection [Public Health England, 2013], a NICE evidence summary [NICE, 2015b], and Canadian evidence review [CADTH, 2018a]. 
    • Antibiotic-associated diarrhoea is associated with C. difficile infection because the changes in normal microflora allows colonization and infection with C. difficile [PHE, 2018a; CADTH, 2018a]. Antibiotic use is the major risk factor for C. difficile infection [Steele et al, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016], and the use of broad-spectrum antibiotics increases the incidence (C. difficile infection is the most common identifiable cause of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea, accounting for around 20–30% of cases) [NICE, 2015b; PHE, 2018a].  
    • Antibiotic-related diarrhoea with C. difficile infection commonly develops during, or shortly after antibiotic treatment [Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
Clinical symptoms of C. difficile infection
  • Symptoms of C. difficile infection can vary from mild diarrhoea to more severe symptoms indicative of pseudomembranous colitis, toxic megacolon, bowel perforation, and sepsis [Winslow et al, 2014; Ong et al, 2017; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. Although C. difficile infection almost always causes diarrhoea, abdominal distension, and pain, other symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and fever may not be present. Physical findings can be non-specific and depend on the severity and duration of illness [Steele et al, 2015; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
Suspect the possibility of C. difficile infection if risk factors are present
  • Older adults are most at risk from C. difficile-associated diarrhoea, particularly if they have a history of recent antibiotic use. Other risk factors also contribute to the chances of developing C. difficile infection [Burke, 2014; Kazanowski et al, 2014; Winslow et al, 2014; Leffler and Lamont, 2015; Steele et al, 2015; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018; Hassoun, 2018].  
Check for a history of C. difficile infection
  • A systematic review and meta-analysis of risk factors for C. difficile infection found that symptomatic recurrence after the primary episode has completely resolved is a frequent complication [Deshpande et al, 2015]. Recurrence risk increases with increasing age and a small proportion of people have repeated relapses [Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Hassoun, 2018].  
Stool testing for C. difficile toxin
  • C. difficile infection is difficult to diagnose on the basis of physical examination alone, and therefore the diagnosis should be based on clinical signs and symptoms and supported by laboratory tests, including stool studies [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Steele et al, 2015; Crobach et al, 2016; Trubiano et al, 2016].  
Check for reports of other cases
  • C. difficile has been associated with outbreaks in hospitals and care facilities, especially in people of advanced age [Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; PHE, 2018a]. The index of suspicion for C. difficile infection is increased by a history of recent sick contacts (for example in a nursing home or hospital) [Steele et al, 2015].  

What tests should I perform if a Clostridium difficile infection is suspected?

For adults with suspected Clostridium difficile infection:

  • Send a stool sample to test for C. difficile infection if the person is symptomatic with liquid/loose stools (with a consistency that takes the shape of the container — ideally 1/4 filled) that is not clearly attributable to another condition or therapy, and C. difficile infection is suspected, for example if the person:
    • Has been in contact with a person with C. difficile infection. 
    • Has recently had a course of antibiotics, proton-pump inhibitor treatment, or been in hospital. 
  • Do not wait to initiate sampling or testing as any delay may increase the severity of the disease and the risk of C. difficile transmission.
  • Ensure the following details are stated on the request form:
    • Clinical features (for example nature and duration of symptoms).
    • Recent antibiotic or proton pump inhibitor, or hospital admission.  
    • Contact with other affected individuals or outbreak. 
    • Underlying illness. 
    • State if the test was requested by the Health Protection Team, a Consultant in Communicable Disease Control, or a Consultant in Health Protection. 
  • Check the full blood count and serum creatinine in order to help assess the severity of C. difficile.
  • Consider re-testing if the first test is negative and there is a strong clinical suspicion of C. difficile infection — seek advice from a consultant medical microbiologist or infection control doctor. 
  • Do not re-test people with a positive C. difficile infection if they are still symptomatic within the same episode. 
  • Only re-test to confirm recurrent C. difficile infection if the symptoms resolve and then recur.
  • Do not test to confirm cure.
  • For more information on how to advise the person to take a stool sample, see the section on Collection of stool samples in the CKS topic on Diarrhoea - adult's assessment.

Basis for recommendation

Laboratory confirmation of Clostridium difficile infection
  • Laboratory testing of stool is required because Clostridium difficile infection has no distinguishing clinical features from other causes of acute diarrhoea [Trubiano et al, 2016]. Therefore diagnosis is usually made on the basis of clinical history, symptoms and signs, and stool testing [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Debast et al, 2014; Crobach et al, 2016].   
  • British Society of Gastroenterology and Department of Health guidelines state that when testing for C. difficile infection, a standardised two-stage approach is recommended. This identifies the organism with glutamate dehydrogenase enzyme immunoassay (EIA), nucleic acid amplification testing, or PCR; then toxin EIA is performed to identify active C. difficile toxin production. This combination of a sensitive followed by specific test produces high negative and positive predictive values when the test results agree [DH, 2012; Arasaradnam et al, 2018]. 
  • When interpreting the laboratory results [DH, 2012]: 
    • If GDH EIA (or NAAT) is positive, and toxin EIA is positive (positive predictive value [PPV] = 91.4%), then C. difficile is most likely to be present.
    • If GDH EIA is negative, and toxin EIA is negative (negative predictive value [NPV] = 98.9%) then C. difficile is very unlikely to be present.
    • If GDH EIA (or NAAT) is positive, and toxin EIA is negative, then C. difficile could be present (potential C. difficile excretors).
When to send a stool sample to test for C. difficile infection
  • The recommendation on when to send a stool sample to test for C. difficile infection is based on guidance issued by Public Health England on managing suspected infectious diarrhoea [PHE, 2015] and the Department of Health's Updated guidance on the diagnosis and reporting of Clostridium difficile [DH, 2012].  
    • Testing of asymptomatic people is not recommended; it is important to only send unformed stools for testing because colonisation with C. difficile is relatively common [Crobach et al, 2016; Trubiano et al, 2016; Health Protection Scotland, 2017; Ong et al, 2017; McDonald et al, 2018].  
    • Public Health England and the Department of Health recommend stool collection from symptomatic patients as soon as possible because the results may be used to reduce the risk of transmission of C. difficile [DH, 2012; Public Health England, 2013]. This is consistent with advice from Health Protection Scotland and the Irish Department of Health [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. 
Request form details
  • The recommendation to describe the clinical features is pragmatic and based on information in a Health Protection Scotland guideline [Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. Other features to document are extrapolated from PHE guidance on managing suspected infectious diarrhoea [PHE, 2015], and risk factors for C. difficile infection outlined by North American and Scottish experts [Health Protection Scotland, 2017; McDonald et al, 2018] and expert opinion in a review article [Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017].
Check full blood count and creatinine
  • A full blood count (may show leukocytosis) and creatinine level can be useful in assessing the severity of disease and identifying the presence of complications [Steele et al, 2015; Guh and Kutty, 2018; Hassoun, 2018]. 
Retesting
  • Re-test only if the first test is negative and there is a strong clinical suspicion of C. difficile infection — seek specialist advice
    • CKS recommends seeking advice from a microbiologist on whether retesting is indicated, based on opinion from experts:
      • Health Protection Scotland guidance on obtaining stool specimens notes that a negative test result does not necessarily exclude infection, particularly if clinical symptoms are highly indicative, and advises discussion with a consultant medical microbiologist or infection control doctor [Health Protection Scotland, 2017].
      • European diagnostic guidance for C. difficile and North American clinical practice guidelines for C. difficile infection also discuss the potential value of repeated testing after a negative sample if there is a high clinical suspicion [Crobach et al, 2016; McDonald et al, 2018]. 
  • Do not re-test people with a positive C. difficile infection if they are still symptomatic during the same episode
    • If there has been a positive sample, repeated testing during the same episode of diarrhoea is not recommended by Health Protection Scotland guidelines on prevention and treatment of C. difficile infection [Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. This is consistent with Irish Department of Health guidelines that advise against retesting when a person is on treatment for C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014], and North American guidelines that do not recommend repeat testing (within 7 days) during the same episode of diarrhoea [McDonald et al, 2018].
  • Only re-test to confirm recurrent C. difficile infection if the symptoms resolve and then recur
    • North American guidelines advise testing (including toxin detection) if recurrent C. difficile infection is suspected after diarrhoea has stopped with successful treatment [McDonald et al, 2018].  This is consistent with Health Protection Scotland and the Irish Department of Health who recommend repeat testing if recurrent diarrhoea occurs after a symptom-free interval in a person with recent C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. 
  • Test of cure
    • UK, European, North American, and Australasian experts agree that a test of cure after treatment is not recommended [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Crobach et al, 2016; Leffler and Lamont, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. 
      • Positive tests can occur for weeks or months after symptoms resolve despite successful treatment, with asymptomatic carriage of C.difficile estimated at around 25–30% one month post-treatment [Kazanowski et al, 2014; Leffler and Lamont, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016]. 

How should I manage antibiotic-associated diarrhoea if C. difficile infection is not suspected or while awaiting the result of C. difficile testing?

For people in whom Clostridium difficile infection is not suspected, or while awaiting a C. difficile test result:

  • Assess the severity of the condition and consider whether hospital admission is appropriate (for further information, see the section on Admission or referral in the CKS topic on Diarrhoea - adult's assessment). 
  • If hospital admission is not required:
    • Stop the antibiotic, if this is appropriate.
      • Seek specialist advice if it is not appropriate to stop the antibiotic and the diarrhoea is severe.
    • If highly suspicious that the person has C. difficile infection, assess the severity and consider seeking specialist advice on whether an empirical antibiotic should be offered.
    • Manage fluid loss and symptoms as for acute gastroenteritis (for further information, see the CKS topic on Gastroenteritis).
    • Avoid the use of antimotility drugs (such as loperamide) to treat diarrhoeal symptoms while awaiting C. difficile results.
    • Give advice on hygiene measures to minimize the spread of possible infection (for further information, see the CKS topic on Gastroenteritis).

Basis for recommendation

Consider whether hospital admission is appropriate
  • This recommendation is pragmatic and based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice.
Management if hospital admission is not required
Stopping the antibiotic
  • A Canadian evidence review mentions reducing the use of antibiotics as a measure for addressing mild cases of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea [CADTH, 2018a]. 
  • For people awaiting Clostridium difficile test results, the recommendation to consider stopping the antibiotic causing diarrhoea is also extrapolated from Public Health England (PHE) guidance on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection which recommends that, if possible, antibiotic treatment should be stopped for people with C. difficile infection to allow normal intestinal flora to be re-established [Public Health England, 2013]. This is in line with subsequent PHE publications [PHE, 2015; NICE and PHE, 2019], guidelines from Ireland [An Roinn Slainte, 2014], North America [Steele et al, 2015], and Australasia [Trubiano et al, 2016] and expert opinion in review articles [Winslow et al, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018].  
  • It is important not to affect recovery from the condition for which the initial antibiotic was prescribed [Guh and Kutty, 2018], therefore the decision to stop an antibiotic should be based on the person's clinical state [Steele et al, 2015], and people for whom antibiotic treatment is stopped should be observed closely for signs of clinical deterioration [Debast et al, 2014]. If treatment of the original condition is necessary, it may be possible to select an alternative antibiotic which is less likely to contribute to C. difficile infection [Winslow et al, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017].
Consider seeking specialist advice about empirical antibiotic treatment
  • Guidance on prevention and control of C. difficile infection issued by Health Protection Scotland advises that if C. difficile infection is suspected, confirmation of the diagnosis from laboratory results should not be awaited before initiating treatment [Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. This recommendation is also extrapolated from discussion in a systematic review that empirical therapy is only indicated if the results of diagnostic tests are awaited and the probability of infection is high [Kazanowski et al, 2014]. 
Manage fluid loss and symptoms as for acute gastroenteritis
  • European treatment guidance for C. difficile infection suggest adequate replacement of fluid and electrolytes as a treatment measure [Debast et al, 2014]. A practice parameter for the management of C. difficile infection notes the potential importance of fluid resuscitation as a factor in limiting severity, duration, and complications [Steele et al, 2015]. 
Avoid the use of antimotility drugs
  • CKS does not recommend the use of antimotility drugs in people with suspected C. difficile infection in line with European treatment guidance for C. difficile infection [Debast et al, 2014], Irish guidance on surveillance, diagnosis and management of C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014] and expert opinion in review articles [Ong et al, 2017; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. There is a lack of evidence to support their use and there are concerns about possible adverse effects (for example ileus, toxic megacolon) [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017]. If a person has C. difficile infection, antiperistaltic drugs may also prevent distribution of therapeutic antibiotic in the gut, and affect expulsion of toxin [Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 

Give advice on hygiene measures

  • CKS recommends advising on hygiene measures if a C. difficile test result is awaited because Public Health England [Public Health England, 2013] and international guidelines advocate the use of infection control measures, including handwashing and environmental cleaning, to prevent the spread of C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Debast et al, 2014; Steele et al, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016; McDonald et al, 2018].
    • Hygiene measures are important if a diagnosis is made because the spores of C. difficile are transmissible and can contaminate the environment, where they survive for a long period of time as they are resistant to heat, acid, and chemicals. The spores can be picked up by contact with a person or contaminated surface and then swallowed. Carers of a person with C. difficile infection are easily contaminated and hand washing with liquid soap and water is advised to remove spores and prevent their spread as alcohol hand rubs do not kill spores [Steele et al, 2015; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. 

How should I manage antibiotic-associated diarrhoea if C. difficile toxin test is negative?

  • If the Clostridium difficile toxin test result is negative:
    • Continue management as outlined in C. difficile not suspected or awaiting result.
    • Consider seeking specialist advice and retesting if there is a strong clinical suspicion of C. difficile infection or if in doubt.

Basis for recommendation

Continue management as outlined in C. difficile not suspected or awaiting result.

  • This recommendation is pragmatic and is based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice. 
Consider seeking specialist advice and retesting if there is a strong clinical suspicion of C. difficile infection or if in doubt
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion in a review article advising a specialist opinion if the diagnosis is uncertain [Guh and Kutty, 2018].  
  • North American clinical practice guidelines for C. difficile infection from the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) and Society for Healthcare Epidemiology of America (SHEA) advise considering repeat testing in symptomatic people with a negative test for whom there is a high clinical suspicion of C. difficile infection [McDonald et al, 2018]. This is consistent with advice in a diagnostic guidance document for C. difficile infection from the European Society of Clinical Microbiology and Infectious Diseases [Crobach et al, 2016] and Public Health England, who note that one-off negative results can occur [PHE, 2018a].  

How should I manage antibiotic-associated diarrhoea if C. difficile toxin tests are positive?

  • People with features of severe infection should be managed in secondary care.
  • For people who have mild-to-moderate C. difficile infection consult local policy or discuss further management with a microbiologist and consider prescribing an antibiotic: 
    • People with mild disease (less than 4 episodes of stool a day) may not require specific C. difficile antibiotic treatment.
    • As a guide, Public Health England recommends: 
      • For the initial episode of mild-to-moderate C. difficile infection: oral metronidazole 400 mg three times a day for 10–14 days.
      • Vancomycin and fidaxomicin are reserved for severe C. difficile infection or for subsequent recurrences.
  • Assess whether there is a risk of a C. difficile outbreak — for example, among other elderly residents in a care or nursing home.
  • Stop any antibiotics not being used for treating C. difficile infection, if this is appropriate. Seek specialist advice if this is not possible and the diarrhoea is severe.
  • Manage fluid loss as for acute gastroenteritis (for further information, see the CKS topic on Gastroenteritis).
  • Avoid the use of antimotility drugs (such as loperamide) to treat diarrhoeal symptoms. If possible, avoid other drugs with anti-peristaltic effects (such as opioids).
  • Give advice on hygiene measures to minimize the spread of C. difficile (see the CKS topic on Gastroenteritis).
    • Note that alcohol-based hand rubs are not effective in removing C.difficile spores.
  • Review the person daily and monitor for signs of increasing severity of disease, as they may deteriorate very rapidly.
  • Advise people that:
    • The diarrhoea should resolve in 1–2 weeks.
    • They remain infectious while they are still ill and have symptoms.
    • They should not return to work or school until they have been free from diarrhoea for 48 hours. If medication is prescribed, they should ensure that the full course is completed and there is no further diarrhoea or vomiting for 48 hours afterwards.
    • Once recovered, they should seek medical advice if they develop unexplained diarrhoea, as there is a high rate of recurrence with C. difficile infection.
  • Offer written information, such as NHS information on Clostridium difficile infection, available at www.nhs.uk.
  • If the person's condition has improved considerably or has resolved without treatment, consider the possibility of a false-positive test result.

Basis for recommendation

Manage people with features of severe infection in secondary care
  • CKS has made this recommendation on the basis of guidance from the Irish Department of Health, that suggests people with severe Clostridium difficile infection should be managed by a multidisciplinary team (including a clinical microbiologist and/or infection diseases physician and a surgeon) [An Roinn Slainte, 2014] and expert opinion in review articles [Kazanowski et al, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
    • Outpatient treatment is not feasible for people with dehydration, hypotension, signs of peritonitis, toxic megacolon, suspected sepsis, or changes in mental state. Admission to intensive care or resuscitation may be required [Ong et al, 2017; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. Some people may also need surgical intervention, for example those with peritonitis, or severe and protracted infection leading to toxic megacolon and intestinal perforation and necrosis [Kazanowski et al, 2014; Steele et al, 2015; Ong et al, 2017]. 
Antibiotics for treating mild to moderate Clostridium difficile infection
  • The recommendations regarding the choice of antibiotic to treat C. difficile infection is based on guidance published by Public Health England: Updated guidance on the management and treatment of Clostridium difficile infection [Public Health England, 2013], Managing suspected infectious diarrhoea [PHE, 2015] and Summary of antimicrobial prescribing guidance - managing common infections [NICE and PHE, 2019]. 
    • A Cochrane systematic review of antibiotic treatment for C. difficile-associated diarrhoea in adults identified 22 studies (n = 3215), with most including people with mild to moderate disease. One small randomized controlled trial (RCT) compared vancomycin with placebo for treating C. difficile infections, but it was of low quality, and the lack of other placebo-controlled studies means it is not possible to assess the benefit of antibiotic treatment for C. difficile infection over withdrawal of the initial antibiotic. There is moderate quality evidence to suggest better efficacy of vancomycin compared with metronidazole (symptomatic cure in 79% vs 72%; RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.84 to 0.97), and superiority of fidaxomicin over vancomycin (symptomatic cure in 71% vs 61%; RR 1.17, 95% CI 1.04 to 1.31), although the Cochrane authors noted the relatively small differences in effectiveness and the much lower cost of metronidazole compared with vancomycin and fidaxomicin [Nelson et al, 2017]. 
    • A subsequent systematic review and network meta-analysis of the comparative efficacy of treatments for C. difficile infection (n = 5361) found moderate-to low quality evidence that, for first infections, fidaxomicin is more effective than vancomycin for sustained cure (OR 0.67, 95% CI 0.55 to 0.82), with both fidaxomicin and vancomycin offering superior performance compared with metronidazole (OR for fidaxomicin 0.49, 95% CI 0.35 to 0.68; OR for vancomycin 0.73, 05% CI 0.56 to 0.95) [Beinortas et al, 2018]. This study was discussed in a NICE evidence review in which the authors noted that there were limited relevant data on disease severity, causing difficulty making recommendations related to choice of treatment according to severity of infection, duration of treatment, or management of recurrence. They questioned whether future national recommendations on antibiotic preference should take note of these findings given the relative effectiveness of the different antibiotics [NICE, 2018].
Assess risk of a C. difficile outbreak
  • This recommendation is extrapolated from expert opinion in a review article that outlines the need for awareness of the potential for outbreaks of C. difficile infection in vulnerable populations [Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017]. Public Health England note the association with outbreaks in hospitals and extended care facilities for the elderly [PHE, 2018a].  
Stop the antibiotic causing the diarrhoea (apart from any being used to treat the C. difficile infection)
  • The recommendation to consider stopping the antibiotic causing diarrhoea reflects Public Health England guidance on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection which recommends that, if possible, antibiotic treatment should be stopped for people with C. difficile infection to allow normal intestinal flora to be re-established [Public Health England, 2013]. This is in line with subsequent PHE publications [PHE, 2015; NICE and PHE, 2019], guidelines from Europe [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Debast et al, 2014], North America [Steele et al, 2015], and Australasia [Trubiano et al, 2016], and expert opinion in review articles [Winslow et al, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
  • It is important not to affect recovery from the condition for which the initial antibiotic was prescribed [Guh and Kutty, 2018], therefore the decision to stop an antibiotic should be based on the person's clinical state [Steele et al, 2015], and people for whom antibiotic treatment is stopped should be observed closely for signs of clinical deterioration [Debast et al, 2014]. If treatment of the original condition is necessary, it may be possible to select an alternative antibiotic which is less likely to contribute to C. difficile infection [Winslow et al, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017]. 
Manage fluid loss as for acute gastroenteritis
  • Public Health England advises supportive care including attention to hydration and electrolytes [Public Health England, 2013]. This recommendation is also supported by European guidelines [Debast et al, 2014] and expert opinion in review articles [Hassoun, 2018; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. A practice parameter for the management of C. difficile infection notes the potential importance of fluid resuscitation as a factor in limiting severity, duration, and complications [Steele et al, 2015]. 
Avoiding antimotility drugs and drugs with anti-peristaltic effects
  • CKS does not recommend the use of antimotility drugs in people with C. difficile infection in line with Public Health England guidance on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection [Public Health England, 2013], European treatment guidance for C. difficile infection [Debast et al, 2014], Irish guidance on surveillance, diagnosis and management of C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014] and expert opinion in review articles [Ong et al, 2017; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. There is a lack of evidence to support their use and there are concerns about possible adverse effects [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017]. If a person has C. difficile infection, antiperistaltic drugs may prevent distribution of therapeutic antibiotic in the gut, and affect expulsion of toxin [Guh and Kutty, 2018].
  • This is in line with recommendations from the manufacturer of loperamide, which state that loperamide is contraindicated for people in whom inhibition of peristalsis should be avoided due to the possible risk of significant sequelae including ileus, megacolon, and toxic megacolon (including people with pseudomembranous colitis associated with the use of broad-spectrum antibiotics) [ABPI, 2018a]. 
Give advice on hygiene measures

  • Public Health England guidance on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection [Public Health England, 2013] and other international guidelines advocate the use of infection control measures, including handwashing and environmental cleaning, to prevent the spread of C. difficile infection [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Debast et al, 2014; Steele et al, 2015; Trubiano et al, 2016; Health Protection Scotland, 2017; McDonald et al, 2018].  

    • Hygiene measures are important because the spores of C. difficile are transmissible and can contaminate the environment, where they survive for a long period of time as they are resistant to heat, acid, and chemicals. The spores can be picked up by contact with a person or contaminated surface and then swallowed. Carers of a person with C. difficile infection are easily contaminated and hand washing with liquid soap and water is advised to remove spores and prevent their spread as alcohol hand rubs do not kill spores [Steele et al, 2015; Health Protection Scotland, 2017]. 
Review the person daily and monitor for signs of increasing severity of disease
  • The recommendation to review daily is based on guidance from Public Health England on the management and treatment of C. difficile infection, which recommends a management plan tailored to the course and severity of the illness [Public Health England, 2013]. This advice is also pragmatic, and based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice, given that people with severe disease should be managed in secondary care [An Roinn Slainte, 2014; Kazanowski et al, 2014; Ong et al, 2017; Sams and Kennedy-Malone, 2017; Guh and Kutty, 2018]. 
  • European guidelines suggest close follow up for signs of clinical deterioration in people for whom contributing antibiotic treatment has been stopped and therapy for C. difficile infection has not yet commenced [Debast et al, 2014]. For people failing to respond to treatment for C.difficile infection, Irish guidelines advise consideration of surgery if there is deterioration in the person's condition along with systemic inflammation [An Roinn Slainte, 2014].  
Advise on the time course of the illness
  • The recommendations about the duration of illness and the risk of recurrence of C. difficile infection are based on PHE Updated guidance on the management and treatment of Clostridium difficile infection. The recurrence rate for C. difficile infection is high after the initial episode: around 20% for the first episode and 45–60% after the second episode in hospitalised patients [Public Health England, 2013].  
  • Information on infection risk and avoidance of school or work is extrapolated from PHE guidance on health protection in schools and other childcare facilities for diarrhoea and vomiting [PHE, 2018b] and patient information on leaving hospital for people who have had C. difficile during admission [DH, 2011]. 
Use of probiotics
  • Probiotics are thought to repopulate the gastrointestinal tract and limit the growth of C. difficile [Guh and Kutty, 2018]. However, they are not recommended by Public Health England or Health Protection Scotland for the treatment of C. difficile infection on the grounds of insufficient evidence [Public Health England, 2013; Health Protection Scotland, 2017] and a review of guidelines from international organisations found that most did not support the use of probiotics for prevention or treatment of C. difficile infection [CADTH, 2018b]. It is also difficult to make recommendations because of uncertainties regarding the choice of probiotic, dose, and duration of treatment [Steele et al, 2015]. 
    • European and North American guidelines also noted sparse and insufficient evidence for the treatment of C. difficile infection [Debast et al, 2014; Steele et al, 2015]. A subsequent evidence review found conflicting evidence for the role of probiotics in prevention and treatment [Wilkins and Sequoia, 2017]. A further review of the clinical effectiveness of probiotics for antibiotic-associated diarrhoea and C. difficile infection suggested that probiotics may reduce the risk of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea, but some results were not statistically significant. There were mixed findings on the ability of probiotics to prevent C.difficile associated diarrhoea, and the review found no studies reporting on the effectiveness of probiotics for treatment [CADTH, 2018a]. 
Consider the possibility of a false-positive test result
  • C. difficile may be an incidental finding in stools of people with diarrhoea due to another cause [Starr, 2005]. Indiscriminate testing increases the likelihood of a false positive result (examples of inappropriate testing in one study included people without diarrhoea and those who had recently used laxatives) [Winslow et al, 2014]. 
    • Colonisation with C. difficile is quite common and people who are colonised may be asymptomatic [Kazanowski et al, 2014; Winslow et al, 2014; Ong et al, 2017; McDonald et al, 2018].
