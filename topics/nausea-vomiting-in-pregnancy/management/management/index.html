Nausea/vomiting in pregnancy: Scenario: Management

Last revised in November 2020

Covers the assessment of a woman with nausea and vomiting in pregnancy, and discusses self-care advice, when to consider drug treatment, and when admission to hospital or referral is appropriate.

Scenario: Management

From age 13 years to 60 years (Female).

What assessment is required?

  • Enquire about:
    • The onset, duration, and frequency of nausea and vomiting.
    • Whether food and drinks are being tolerated.
    • Associated symptoms (for example weight loss, abdominal pain).
    • Any co-existing conditions (for example diabetes) which may be adversely affected by nausea and vomiting.
    • The effect on the woman's life (for example work, home situation and support, ability to care for her family).
    • The effect on the woman's mood, with further assessment if appropriate.
      • If there are concerns that the woman is showing signs of depression, see the CKS topic on Depression - antenatal and postnatal.
  • Consider using a validated questionnaire to assess the severity of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy (for example the Pregnancy-Unique Quantification of Emesis [PUQE] score). This is described in more detail in Appendix II of the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum (Green-top Guideline No. 69), available at www.rcog.org.uk. 
  • If a woman has nausea or vomiting of sufficient severity to affect fluid and food intake:
    • Monitor her weight.
    • Examine for signs of dehydration (for example dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, postural hypotension).
    • Look for signs of muscle wasting.
    • Test the urine for ketones. 
    • Consider assessing for signs of hypokalaemia or thyrotoxicosis.
    • Consider referring for ultrasonography to identify predisposing factors (for example multiple or molar pregnancy).
  • Measurement of serum human chorionic gonadotrophin is not recommended.
  • Further blood tests (for example full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, calcium and phosphate levels, and thyroid function tests) are not routinely recommended in primary care; if they are thought to be necessary, admission to hospital may be more appropriate.
  • If features suggest an alternative cause of nausea and vomiting, exclude alternative diagnoses (for more information, see the section on Differential diagnosis). 

Basis for recommendation

Assessment
  • These recommendations are based on a Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016]; an American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) Practice Bulletin: Nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [ACOG, 2015]; and expert opinion in review articles [Clark et al, 2014; Bustos et al, 2017].
Use of a validated questionnaire
  • This recommendation is based on the RCOG guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016] and the Society of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists of Canada clinical practice guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [SOGC, 2016]. The Pregnancy-Unique Quantification of Emesis (PUQE) score [Ebrahimi et al, 2009] can be used to assess the severity of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy and can also be used to ascertain how treatment is progressing [RCOG, 2016; SOGC, 2016]. 
Measurement of serum human chorionic gonadotrophin
  • Measuring serum human chorionic gonadotrophin is not recommended because it is not thought to be helpful in determining whether vomiting is caused by hyperemesis gravidarum [ACOG, 2015].
Further investigations
  • Expert opinion in a review article suggests that laboratory tests are not needed in women with normal examination findings and no evidence of dehydration [Herrell, 2014].
  • CKS suggests that if a woman is sufficiently unwell that further investigations (for example full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests and thyroid function tests) are being considered, it is more appropriate to do these as part of a secondary care assessment. Therefore, blood tests are not discussed in detail.
Excluding differential diagnoses
  • This recommendation is extrapolated from a review article [BMJ Best Practice, 2016] and is also pragmatic, based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice.

What advice should I offer?

  • Reassure the woman that nausea and vomiting are a normal part of pregnancy that usually resolve by 16 to 20 weeks of gestation, and pregnancy outcomes are generally better for women who have nausea and vomiting in early pregnancy.
  • Advise rest.
  • The following may also be tried:
    • Avoiding any foods or smells that trigger symptoms (for example spicy or fatty foods).
    • Eating plain biscuits or crackers in the morning before getting up.
    • Eating bland, small, frequent meals low in carbohydrate and fat but high in protein.
    • Cold meals may be more easily tolerated if nausea is smell-related.
    • Drinking little and often rather than large amounts, as this may help to prevent vomiting.
    • Ginger.
    • Acupressure.
  • Consider advising avoidance of iron-containing preparations if they make symptoms worse.
  • Advise all women with nausea and vomiting in pregnancy to seek urgent medical advice if they experience:
    • Very dark urine, or no urination for more than 8 hours.
    • Abdominal pain or fever.
    • Severe weakness or feeling faint.
    • Vomiting blood.
    • Repeated, unstoppable vomiting.
    • Inability to keep down food or fluids for 24 hours.
    • Severe headache, visual problems, severe pain below the ribs, sudden swelling of the face, hands, or feet (symptoms of pre-eclampsia). 
  • Offer additional support for women experiencing nausea and vomiting in pregnancy, such as:
    • Self-help information, for example that offered by the NHS on Nausea and morning sickness; or the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists on Pregnancy sickness (nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum).
    • Support groups such as Pregnancy sickness support (www.pregnancysicknesssupport.org.uk).
    • Best use of medicines in pregnancy (bumps) patient information from the UK Teratology Information Service (www.medicinesinpregnancy.org).

Basis for recommendation

Reassurance
  • This recommendation is based on the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [NICE, 2008] and expert opinion in review articles [Davis, 2004; Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011]. Most women can eat and drink sufficiently to avoid use of anti-emetics or intravenous fluids, or admission to hospital [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011].
Rest
  • This recommendation is based on the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016] and a clinical practice guideline from the Society of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists of Canada (SOGC): The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [SOGC, 2016], which recommend rest as required to alleviate symptoms.
Dietary changes
  • Dietary changes to help the symptoms of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy are widely recommended by experts [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011; Bustos et al, 2017; RCOG, 2016; SOGC, 2016; Tan et al, 2016].
    • One small study has shown that a diet high in protein improved gastric dysrhythmias and symptoms of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy more than high carbohydrate or high fat diets [Jednak et al, 1999].
Ginger 
  • Ginger is included as an option to treat nausea and vomiting in pregnancy by NICE in the guideline Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [NICE, 2008]. The RCOG also suggest ginger as an option for women with mild to moderate nausea and vomiting in pregnancy who prefer to avoid antiemetic treatment [RCOG, 2016].
  • Although ginger has been used for many years to treat nausea and vomiting in pregnancy, there is little strong or consistent evidence that it is effective [Matthews et al, 2015]. A systematic review found 16 RCTs of ginger as an intervention for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy, but was unable to pool data because of the heterogeneity between studies in terms of population, intervention, comparators and outcomes. After looking at individual studies, the authors concluded that the overall quality of evidence was low, and was more applicable to women with less severe symptoms of nausea and vomiting. Results were promising and indicated that ginger may be better than placebo with regard to symptom reduction, but due to the methodological flaws of the included studies, this was not conclusive and larger, better quality trials are needed [O'Donnell et al, 2016].
    • This is consistent with the findings of other systematic reviews [Festin, 2014; Thomson et al, 2014; Viljoen et al, 2014; Matthews et al, 2015]. 
  • The UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS) reviewed the safety data for ginger and identified two cohort studies and a number of small clinical trials including a small number of women. These found no evidence of an increased risk of adverse pregnancy outcomes including congenital malformations associated with ginger when taken during pregnancy, although a higher risk of fetal adverse effects cannot be entirely ruled out because of the limited data available [UKTIS, 2013].
P6 (wrist) acupressure
  • P6 (wrist) acupressure is included as an option to treat nausea and vomiting in pregnancy by the NICE guideline Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [NICE, 2008]. In the guideline The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum, the RCOG states that acupressure may improve the symptoms of nausea and vomiting and is safe in pregnancy [RCOG, 2016]. 
  • Evidence for P6 (wrist) acupressure is limited [Matthews et al, 2015]. A systematic review found eight RCTs of acupressure compared with placebo or sham treatment for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy of which seven used the P6 (wrist) acupressure point. The evidence available varied in quality, which limited the conclusions which could be drawn as it was not possible to pool data in a meta-analysis due to variability between populations, interventions, outcomes, and settings. Acupressure may have a beneficial effect if nausea and vomiting symptoms are mild, but larger and better quality studies are required [O'Donnell et al, 2016].
    • Other systematic reviews had similar findings [Festin, 2014; Matthews et al, 2015; Van den Heuvel et al, 2016].
  • In terms of safety, there are no theoretical concerns about the use of acupressure in pregnancy, but a UKTIS review of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy found no formal data for this intervention [UKTIS, 2015a].
Avoidance of iron-containing preparations
  • This recommendation is based on RCOG and SOGC guidelines [RCOG, 2016; SOGC, 2016], and expert opinion in review articles [Clark et al, 2014; Tan et al, 2016].
  • The SOGC considers that, unless the woman is iron deficient, there may not be an indication for routine iron supplementation in the first trimester (for example iron-containing prenatal vitamins) because there is no increase in iron requirements at this time. However, the guideline emphasises the importance of maintaining folic acid supplementation [SOGC, 2016].  
Seeking specialist advice
  • The recommendation about when women should be advised to seek specialist advice is based on an evidence-based review of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy [Davis, 2004], with the symptoms suggesting pre-eclampsia reflecting those described in the NICE guideline on Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [NICE, 2008].
Offering additional support
  • This recommendation is pragmatic and is in line with advice from NICE [NICE, 2008] that information on self-help and non-pharmacological treatments should be available for women affected by nausea and vomiting in pregnancy. The RCOG makes similar recommendations about patient support groups on the basis that women and their partners may find this helpful  [RCOG, 2016].

Which drug treatments can be considered?

  • Consider drug treatment with an anti-emetic if initial treatments such as dietary advice or rest have failed and the woman has persistent symptoms.
  • If an anti-emetic is required in pregnancy, prescribe an antihistamine (oral cyclizine or oral promethazine), or a phenothiazine (oral prochlorperazine), and reassess after 24 hours. 
  • If the response to the treatment is good:
    • Continue treatment with the chosen anti-emetic.
    • Review the woman once a week thereafter.
  • If the response to treatment is inadequate, the woman is not dehydrated, and there is no ketonuria: 
    • Switch to another anti-emetic from a different class.
      • Oral metoclopramide or oral ondansetron are second-line options, but should not be prescribed for longer than 5 days.
      • Note: exposure to ondansetron during the first trimester of pregnancy is associated with a small increased risk of the baby having a cleft lip and/or cleft palate. The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) advises that if a licensed product is not available or not suitable or not sufficient alone to control severe nausea and vomiting in pregnancy, and there is a special clinical need to use ondansetron, then the decision to prescribe ondansetron decision should be made in consultation with the patient after she has been fully informed of the potential benefits and risks of the different treatment options. Prescribers should refer to clinical guidance if treatment with ondansetron is considered for severe nausea and vomiting in pregnancy [GOV, 2020].
    • Reassess after 24 hours.
    • Seek specialist advice if the response to a second anti-emetic is poor. For more information, see the section on referral and seeking specialist advice.
    • Review the woman once a week thereafter.
  • Decide when to stop medication using a pragmatic approach (for example, it may be possible to stop anti-emetic medication at around 12-16 weeks, by which time symptoms have usually improved) in conjunction with clinical judgement (for example severity of symptoms, response to treatment in previous pregnancies, preference of the woman).

Basis for recommendation

When to consider drug treatment
  • These recommendations are extrapolated from the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016], the Society of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists of Canada guideline: The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [SOGC, 2016], an American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) practice bulletin on Nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [ACOG, 2015] and published expert opinion [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011].
    • It is generally accepted that all drugs should be avoided if possible during the first trimester and should be taken only when the benefits of medication outweigh potential risks [BNF 73, 2017].
    • The RCOG guideline The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum advises treating women with mild nausea and vomiting of pregnancy with antiemetics in the community [RCOG, 2016].
    • The ACOG practice bulletin [ACOG, 2015], SOGC clinical guideline [SOGC, 2016], published expert opinion in a review article [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011], and feedback from CKS expert reviewers [RCOG, Personal Communication, 2013] recommend early treatment with anti-emetics. It is thought that early treatment with an anti-emetic can help avoid hyperemesis [ACOG, 2015; SOGC, 2016] which can lead to maternal metabolic disturbances, and may affect the fetus [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011]. 
Choice of anti-emetic
  • The choice of antiemetic drugs is based on the RCOG guideline The management of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016], and a review article from UK Medicines Information [UKMi, 2014].
    • The RCOG guideline recommends antihistamines (H1 receptor antagonists) (for example cyclizine and promethazine) and phenothiazines (prochlorperazine) as first-line choices when required for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016].
    • The RCOG also recommends the use of an antiemetic from a different class if the first-line choice is not effective [RCOG, 2016]. Second-line choices are metoclopramide (because of the risk of extrapyramidal effects [EMA, 2013]), and ondansetron (because of the lack of data in pregnancy) [Schaefer, 2015; RCOG, 2016]. 
  • In terms of safety in pregnancy:
    • Cyclizine and promethazine — these drugs may be preferred because they have been extensively used in pregnancy [UKTIS, 2015b] and they are considered to be an effective and safe treatment for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy [Schaefer, 2015]. There is a large amount of data available from a meta-analysis and a large prospective series on the safety of antihistamines in pregnancy. This does not indicate an increased rate of major congenital malformations for antihistamines in general (although data are limited to one small observational study for cyclizine) [UKTIS, 2015b]. No association between exposure to promethazine in pregnancy and congenital malformations, preterm delivery, low birth weight, or adverse neurodevelopmental effects has been reported in studies. No data on risk of spontaneous miscarriage were found [UKTIS, 2015c].
    • Metoclopramide — one large case-control study and a small cohort study found no increase in congenital malformations with metoclopramide use during pregnancy. There is some evidence of an increased incidence of premature delivery in women exposed to metoclopramide in pregnancy [UKTIS, 2015b]. 
    • Prochlorperazine — a meta-analysis of exposure to a number of phenothiazines, and a case-control study of prochlorperazine found no evidence of an increased risk of congenital malformations [UKTIS, 2015b]. 
    • Ondansetron — data are limited, but there is some evidence from a cohort study to suggest an association between ondansetron exposure in the first trimester of pregnancy and cardiac defects (a possible two-fold increase in risk), and conflicting evidence from three observational studies, of which a case-control study found a possible association with ondansetron and cleft palate, and two cohort studies reported no evidence to support this. Data were insufficient to evaluate the effect of ondansetron on other outcomes such as spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, premature delivery, and neurodevelopmental problems [UKTIS, 2015b; UKTIS, 2015d].
Treatment review
  • Expert opinion from reviewers of this CKS topic suggests women who have nausea and vomiting in pregnancy should be reviewed 24 hours after treatment with an anti-emetic has commenced, and once a week thereafter [RCOG, Personal Communication, 2013]. 
Duration of treatment
  • CKS found no evidence regarding duration of treatment. Expert opinion from reviewers of this CKS topic states that there is no evidence to recommend a short time period of treatment and therapy should be stepped up until it is effective, then continued until symptoms resolve [RCOG, Personal Communication, 2013]. However, note that the maximum duration of treatment with metoclopramide or ondansetron is 5 days [UKMi, 2014]. CKS has therefore recommended a pragmatic approach with the use of clinical judgement as it is recognized that circumstances will vary depending on the individual.
Treatments not recommended
  • Pyridoxine — The RCOG does not recommend pyridoxine for treating nausea and vomiting in pregnancy on the grounds that there is a lack of consistent evidence of effectiveness [RCOG, 2016]. Although there is no evidence that pyridoxine is associated with an increased risk of congenital malformations or spontaneous abortion [UKTIS, 2016], high doses of pyridoxine (greater than 50 mg daily) are known to cause peripheral neuropathy [Dalton and Dalton, 1987]. High doses of pyridoxine (up to 80 mg daily) are required to treat nausea and vomiting in pregnancy [Schaefer, 2015], and the Committee on Toxicity of Chemicals in Food in the UK recommends a maximum daily dose of 10 mg [DH, 1997]. Therefore CKS has not recommended the use of pyridoxine for nausea and vomiting.
  • Herbal treatments, homeopathy, hypnosis, hypnotherapy, psychotherapy — there is either no published evidence or limited evidence that these treatments are effective and safe to use in pregnancy.

When should I admit to hospital or seek specialist advice?

  • Consider admitting to hospital if the woman has one of:
    • Continued nausea and vomiting and is unable to keep down liquids or oral antiemetics.
    • Continued nausea and vomiting with ketonuria and/or weight loss (greater than 5% of body weight), despite treatment with oral antiemetics.
    • A confirmed or suspected comorbidity (for example she is unable to tolerate oral antibiotics for a urinary tract infection).
  • Have a lower threshold for admitting to hospital or seeking specialist advice if the woman has a co-existing condition (for example diabetes) which may be adversely affected by nausea and vomiting. 
  • Admission or specialist advice may also be required if there is suspicion of an alternative diagnosis or complication requiring specialist management. 

Basis for recommendation

When to consider admission to hospital
  • This recommendation is based on the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) guideline The management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy and hyperemesis gravidarum [RCOG, 2016] and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) practice bulletin on Nausea and vomiting of pregnancy [ACOG, 2015].
    • Admission is necessary for women who are dehydrated or unable to tolerate liquids because intravenous hydration is required in this situation [ACOG, 2015]. In addition, if there are signs of volume depletion or more than 5% weight loss, the woman should undergo investigations to exclude other causes of nausea and vomiting and determine the degree of systemic upset [BMJ Best Practice, 2016].
Co-existing conditions
  • This recommendation is based on expert opinion in a review article, which recommends that women with co-existing illness or comorbidities should be managed as inpatients [Jarvis and Nelson-Piercy, 2011].
Medical complications
  • These recommendations are pragmatic and are based on what CKS considers to be good clinical practice.
