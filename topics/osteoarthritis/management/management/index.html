Osteoarthritis: Scenario: Management

Last revised in June 2018

Covers the management of osteoarthritis of any synovial joint, with specific detail on osteoarthritis of the hand, hip, and knee.

Scenario: Management

From age 45 years onwards.

How should I initially manage a person withosteoarthritis?

If a person has a diagnosis of osteoarthritis, offer an individualisedmanagement plan, based on the person's expectations and goals:

  • Provide information onsources of adviceand support, such as:
    • Arthritis Research UK (website available at www.arthritisresearchuk.org)is a national charity which provides information and support for people with arthritis, includinginformation leaflets on Osteoarthritis, Hip pain,and Osteoarthritis of the kneewhich coverdifferent joint involvement, self-management strategies, medical and surgical treatments.
    • Arthritis and Musculoskeletal Alliance (ARMA, website available at arma.uk.net) is a national action network aiming to improve standards of care for people with arthritis and other musculoskeletal disorders by encouraging co-operation between charities and professional organisations.
    • The NHSinformation leaflet Osteoarthritis.
    • The patient information leafletOsteoarthritis available on the www.patient.info website.
    • The British Society for Surgery of the Hand patient leaflet Arthritis at the base of the thumbwhich recommends avoiding repetitive thumb movements, prolonged gripping, and heavy objects;and using as large a grip as possible.
  • Advise on self-care management strategies for symptom relief:
    • Recommend weight loss, if the person is overweight or obese.
      • See the CKS topic on Obesity for more information.
    • Advise on local muscle strengthening exercises and aerobic fitness training, which may also protect joints.
      • For hand involvement, this may include advice toavoid repetitive grasp, pinch, and twisting motions.
    • Advise on additional options such as appropriatefootwear, local heat or cold packs, and/ortranscutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS).
    • Advise that there is no evidence for the use ofover-the-counter glucosamine, chondroitin, or topical rubefacients (which may contain nicotinate compounds, salicylate compounds, essential oils and camphor).
    • Advise that there is no evidence for the use of acupuncture.
  • Offer support to help with the psychosocial impact of the disease, such as:
    • Psychological support if there is associated stress, anxiety, or depression. See the CKS topics onGeneralized anxiety disorderandDepression for more information.
    • Carer assessment and Adult Social Care referral, if needed.
    • Occupational health assessment, to consider alteration of work tasks, working hours, and workplace modifications, if needed.
  • Advise on the use of simple analgesiafor symptom relief.
    • Initial drug treatmentsinclude paracetamol and topical nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs, particularly for hand or knee involvement) if needed and there are no contraindications.
      • Advise on the use of regular rather than as-needed analgesia, if pain remains uncontrolled.
      • Advise people using topical ibuprofen gel not to smoke or go near naked flames as there is a risk of severe burns fabric (clothing, bedding, dressings etc) that has been in contact with gel burns more easily and is a serious fire hazard. Washing clothing and bedding may reduce product build-up but not totally remove it.
      • See the CKS topiconAnalgesia - mild-to-moderate painand the section on Topical NSAIDs inPrescribing informationfor more information.
  • If treatment with paracetamol and/or topical NSAIDs isineffective, consider the use of:
    • Oral NSAIDs such as ibuprofen, if needed and there are no contraindications.
      • Ensure that any topical NSAID is stopped if oral NSAIDs are used.
      • See the CKS topic onNSAIDs - prescribing issues for detailed prescribing information.
    • Opioids such as codeine, if needed and there are no contraindications.
      • See the CKS topic onAnalgesia - mild-to-moderate pain for detailed prescribing information on weak opioids.
    • Topical capsaicin, particularly for hand or knee involvement, depending on local prescribing guidelines.
      • See the section on Topical capsaicin in Prescribing information for more information.
  • Arrange to review the person regularly, the time interval depending on clinical judgement, to reinforce self-care advice, assess the response to treatment, andthe need for onward referral.

Basis for recommendation

The recommendations on management of osteoarthritis are largely based on the National Institute of Health and Care Excellence (NICE) clinical guideline Osteoarthritis: care and management[NICE, 2014], the European League Against Rheumatism (EULAR) expert consensus documentsEULAR recommendations for the health professional's approach to pain management in inflammatory arthritis and osteoarthritis [Geenen, 2018] and EULAR recommendations for the non-pharmacological core management of hip and knee osteoarthritis[Fernandes et al, 2013], the Osteoarthritis Research Society International (OARSI) publication OARSI guidelines for the non-surgical management of knee osteoarthritis[McAlindon, 2014], various Cochrane systematic reviews, the Arthritis Research UK publication Osteoarthritis in General Practice. Data and perspectives[Arthritis Research UK, 2013], and expert opinion inreview articles on osteoarthritis[Glyn-Jones, 2015], on hip osteoarthritis [Aresti, 2016], and on thumb pain [Dickson et al, 2015].

Providing information and advice
  • The recommendations on information and advice are largely based on the NICE guideline[NICE, 2014] and are supported by theEULAR expert consensus document on pain management[Geenen, 2018]and the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014].
    • NICE found good-quality evidencefrom observational and qualitative studies that many people with osteoarthritis would like more information about their condition, and provision of tailored information can support and encourage self-management of symptoms with associated costbenefits.
    • The OARSI guidelines found good-quality evidence showing moderate benefits of self-management for chronic musculoskeletal conditions.
Advice on weight loss
  • The recommendation on weight loss is based on limited evidence inthe NICE guideline [NICE, 2014],the EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018], and the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014].
    • The NICE guideline found thatinterventions reducing excessload on the knee in obese people,including weight loss, ledto improvement in functionproviding thatadequateweight loss was achieved. In contrast, the effect of weight loss on pain is inconsistent in the literature. NICE notes that all of the studies have been performed in people with osteoarthritis of the knee, so it is difficult to generalise the results to other joints, and there is reduced evidence for the functional benefits of weight loss for osteoarthritis of the hip.
    • The EULAR task force systematic literature review cited very low- to moderate-quality studies which found inconsistent results of no effects and positive effects of weight management on pain in osteoarthritis of the knee.
    • The OARSI guidelines found good-quality evidence supporting the benefitsof weight reduction on pain and functioning for overweight people with osteoarthritis of the knee.
Advice on exercise
  • The recommendations on exercise are largelybased on the NICEguideline [NICE, 2014], the EULAR expert consensus documenton pain management [Geenen, 2018], the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014], fourCochrane systematic reviews[Fransen, 2014; Fransen, 2015; Bartels, 2016; Osteras, 2017], and expert opinion inreview articles[Dickson et al, 2015; Glyn-Jones, 2015] , as increasing muscle strength reduces joint loading and associated pain.
    • The NICE guideline notes that the majority of the evidence on exercise is related to osteoarthritis of the knee, with good-quality evidence of the benefits of exercise compared with no exercise, which were usually seen at 18 months. It found more limited evidence for hip and hand involvement, and limited evidence thatexercise in water may be beneficial in the short-term.
    • The EULAR task force performed a systematic literature review which found that for general exercise, strength and resistance training, and aerobic exercise, the quality of studies was low to moderate, and effects on pain were mostly positivein osteoarthritis.
    • The OARSI guidelines evaluated good-quality evidence from meta-analyses and found small but clinically relevant short-term benefits of combination strength training and aerobic exercise for pain and function for osteoarthritis of the knee. In addition, it found good-quality evidence ofsmall-to-moderate short-termbenefits of water-based exercise for osteoarthritis of the hip and knee, particularly regarding joint function and quality of life.
    • In addition, a Cochrane systematic review found good-quality evidence ofshort-term benefit that is sustained for at least 26 months in terms of reduced pain, and moderate-quality evidence of an improvement in function for people with osteoarthritis of the knee. It noted a lack of blinding in the majority of studies[Fransen, 2015]. Similar findings of a positive effect on pain and function were found in a systematic review of exercise for people with osteoarthritis of the hip [Fransen, 2014]. A further Cochrane systematic review foundmoderate-quality evidence that aquatic exercise may have small, short-term effects on patient-reported pain, disability, and quality of life in people with osteoarthritis of the knee and hip [Bartels, 2016].
    • The recommendations on joint protection advice for hand involvement are based on the NICE guideline and expert opinion in a review article [Dickson et al, 2015], and these are supported by a Cochrane systematic review which found low-quality evidence of small beneficial effects of exercise on hand pain, function, and finger joint stiffness [Osteras, 2017].
Advice on footwear and orthotic devices
  • The recommendation on provision of suitable footwear and other orthotic devices is based on the NICEguideline [NICE, 2014], the EULAR task force systematic literature reviews on pain management[Geenen, 2018] and non-pharmacological management of hip and knee osteoarthritis [Fernandes et al, 2013].
    • The NICE guideline foundlimited data for the effectiveness of insoles (either wedged or neutral) in reducing symptoms of osteoarthritis of the knee. In the context of a lack of good-qualitytrial data and the low cost of the intervention, the Guideline Development Group (GDG) recommended consideration offootware with shock-absorbing properties.
    • The EULAR document on pain managementfound positive effects of orthotics on pain for orthopaedic shoes in osteoarthritis of the knee, splints in osteoarthritis of the hand, and sleeves and elastic bandages in osteoarthritis of the knee [Geenen, 2018].
    • The EULAR document on non-pharmacological managementspecifies that appropriate shoes should have thick, shock-absorbing soles, to provide support for the arches of the foot, tocontrol foot pronation, and provide adequate space for the toes [Fernandes et al, 2013].
Advice on additional local heat, cold, and transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) treatments
  • These recommendations are based on the NICEguideline[NICE, 2014]and the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014].
    • The NICE GDGfound very limited, poor-quality evidence on the use of the application of local heat and cold in the management of osteoarthritis joint pain.In the GDG's experience, however, these interventionsare widely used as part of self-management, are very low cost and are consideredsafe, therefore they are recommended as an additional treatment option.
    • NICE foundevidence that TENS is clinically beneficial for pain relief and reduction of stiffness in knee osteoarthritis, especially in the short term, however this was not shown in a community setting. It concluded that intermittentuse for exacerbations of symptoms may be ahelpful additional treatment.
    • The OARSI guidelines concluded that the use of TENS is of uncertain benefit in people with knee-only osteoarthritis, on the basis of limited evidence which produced inconclusive results.
Advice on treatments not recommended
  • The recommendation to avoid glucosamine,chondroitin, and topical rubefacients is largely based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014], and is supported by the OARSI consensus guidelines[McAlindon, 2014], a Cochrane systematic review [Derry, 2014],and expert opinion in a review article[Glyn-Jones, 2015].
    • The NICE guideline reviewed the evidence for glucosamine and chondroitin in isolation and in combination, and found a relative lack of dataand inconsistent effects on outcomes of pain and functional improvement.There was very-low to low-quality evidence thatin osteoarthritis of the knee, glucosamine preparationscompared withplacebo showed no clinically important difference for the outcome of pain. Similarly, the GDG noted that the evidence showed no clinically important difference between chondroitin and placebo inpain, stiffness, or joint function scores at3 months.
    • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines which found glucosamine and chondroitin were both 'not appropriate'for osteoarthritis of the knee when used for disease modification, andof 'uncertain' benefitwhen used for symptom relief.
    • Topical rubefacients are counter-irritantsof the skin that may have some pain-relieving effect in musculoskeletal disorders. The recommendation to avoid topical rubefacients is based on the NICE guideline and supported by a Cochrane systematic review which found no evidence to support theuse of salicylate-containing products for chronic musculoskeletal conditions.
  • The recommendation that there is no evidence of clinical effectiveness for the use of acupuncture is based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014],the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014], and a Cochrane systematic review [Manheimer, 2018].
    • NICE found no clinically important differencebetween acupuncture and sham acupuncture in studies of osteoarthritisof the knee for theoutcomes of pain reduction, functional improvement,and reduction of stiffness forshortand long-term follow-up. Similarly, no clinically important difference was found between acupuncture and sham acupuncture in osteoarthritisof the hip for the outcomes of pain reduction or functional improvement.
    • The OARSI guidelines concluded that good-qualityevidence on acupuncture for osteoarthritis of the knee was of 'uncertain' significance, as pooled analysisfound statistically significant benefit of acupuncture in sham-controlled trials, but this did not reach the threshold for clinical significance.
    • A Cochrane systematic review concluded that acupuncture probably has little or no effect in improving pain or function relative to sham acupuncturein people with osteoarthritis of the hip, and small sample sizes of the trials were noted.
Providing psychosocial support
  • These recommendations are based onthe EULAR expert consensus documenton pain management [Geenen, 2018], and are also pragmatic, based on what CKS considers to be good medical practice.
    • The EULAR task force systematic literature review citedvery low- to moderate-quality studies which found positive effects on pain from psychological interventions (such as cognitive behavioural therapy, mindfulness programmes, and stress management).
Advice on pain management
  • The recommendations on pain management are largely based on the NICEguideline [NICE, 2014], the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014],and the EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018].
    • The NICE guideline notes that most studies have focused on osteoarthritis of the knee (or single joint involvement), and studies are often of short duration using single therapies. It found a large evidence-base from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) for the efficacy of paracetamol compared with placebo in the treatment of osteoarthritis of the knee, with less evidence to support its use for other joint involvement. It also found paracetamol appears to be less effective than oralnonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), especially in people with moderate-to-severe pain.In addition, aNICE evidence reviewsuggests that paracetamol may havereduced efficacy inthe management of osteoarthritis than previously thought, and further evidence is awaited.
      • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines, which highlights increased potential risks (such as gastrointestinal adverse effects) with paracetamol for people with co-morbidities, particularly when used for extended durations,and classifies the use of paracetamolfor these peopleas of 'uncertain' benefit.
    • NICE reviewed severalstudies predominantlyof people with osteoarthritis of the kneewhich showed short-termbenefits lasting less than four weeks following the use oftopical NSAIDgels, creams, and ointments when compared withplacebo. It noted there are limited datacomparingthe efficacy and safety of topical with oral NSAIDs in people with osteoarthritis, and the reduction of adverse effects with topical NSAID use compared with oral use, when assessed in RCTs.No data were found comparing the risks and benefits of topical NSAID use with paracetamol.
      • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines, which recommend topical NSAIDsforpeoplewith knee-only osteoarthritis, andfound that, overall, these weresafer and better tolerated compared withoral NSAIDs. It found topicalNSAIDs were of uncertain benefit for people with multiple joint involvement.
    • NICE cited a large body of trial evidence supportingthe efficacy of oral NSAIDsin reducingpain and stiffness associated withosteoarthritis, with the majority of studies reflecting short-term useand involving osteoarthritis of the knee or hip. There was a lack ofstrong evidence to suggest a consistent benefit of oral NSAIDs over paracetamol or opioids, however, NICE noted thelack of well-designed comparator studies.
      • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines which found good-quality evidence that oral NSAIDs benefit people with osteoarthritis of the knee, if there are no associated relevant co-morbidities which increase the risk of adverse effects.
    • NICE found limited, poor-qualityevidence on the use of opioids for the management of osteoarthritis, but concluded that compared with placebo, opioid analgesics reduce pain in people with osteoarthritis of the knee. Ithighlighted there is little evidence to supportdose escalation of these agents, and there is a lack of trial datacomparingthe efficacy and safety of opioids with other symptomatic treatments for osteoarthritis. In addition, there arevery limited data comparing different formulations or routes of administration of opioids, and no specific recommendations could be made.
      • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines which designated oral opioid analgesia with an 'uncertain'recommendation forosteoarthritis of the knee, due to concerns about increased risks ofserious adverse effects compared with placebo.
    • Topical capsaicin is a counter-irritant of the skin that also affects neurotransmitters in sensory terminals, reducing the transmission of painful stimuli.NICE found limited evidencefrom small RCTs of people with osteoarthritis of the knee whichshowedsome positive effects from topical capsaicin compared with placebo, with short-term follow-up. The GDG were aware of the widespread use of this agent for self-management of osteoarthritis of the hand, andfelt that thestudy efficacy data for knee involvement couldbe extrapolated to the hand joint.
      • This is supported by the OARSI guidelines which recommended the use of capsaicin for people withknee-only osteoarthritis without co-morbidities, on the basis of good-quality evidence which found its use was superior to placebo for outcomes of pain reduction.
    • The EULAR publication notes that multiple biological and psychosocial factors may influence a person's perception and experience of pain, including pain severity, degree of joint damage, health beliefs, mood, avoidance behaviour, functional impairment,obesity, sleep quality, social support, and pacing (the balance of activities and rest throughthe day). Asystematic literature reviewhighlightedthat studies were heterogeneous with respect to intervention and comparator conditions.
Arranging regular review
  • The recommendation on arranging regular review is largely based on the NICE guideline[NICE, 2014] together with expert opinion in the Arthritis Research UK publication [Arthritis Research UK, 2013], and expert opinion in a review article[Glyn-Jones, 2015],as osteoarthritis symptoms may fluctuate and progress over time as may people's goals and expectations of treatment.
    • The NICE guideline also highlighted that information sharing should be an ongoing, integral part of the management plan, rather than a single event at the time of presentation.

When should I follow-up and refer a person with osteoarthritis?

If initial self-management strategies and/or drug treatments are ineffective or not tolerated, offer referral to the multidisciplinary team, depending on clinical judgement.

  • Consider referral to a physiotherapist or local musculoskeletal clinic for individualised physical treatment, which may include:
    • Advice on local muscle strengthening exercises,aerobic fitness training, and pacing of activities (balance of activities and rest, ideallyintegrating physical activity into the person's daily routine).
    • Additional manipulation and stretching, especiallyforhip involvement.
    • Provision of protective joint supports, splints, braces, gloves, and sleeves to reduce the load forjoint pain or instability.
    • Intra-articular corticosteroid injections for moderate-to-severe joint pain(if the appropriate expertise and training is not available in generalpractice).
  • Consider referral to an occupational therapist for advice on assistive devices for daily activities which reduce repetitive high loads to different joints.
    • For the hipand knee walking sticks and walking aids, long-handled reachers,sock aids to reduce bending, bath aids, chair and bed raisers, raised toilet seats, perch stools, half steps and grab rails, additional stair rails, and home adaptations to improve access and mobility.
    • For the hand enlarged grips for writing, using small non-slip mats for opening objects, electric can openers and peelers, tap turners.
  • Consider referral to a podiatrist for:
    • A biomechanical assessment of joint pain altered use because of pain in one joint may lead to increased mechanical stress and pain at other sites.
    • Advice on orthotic devices, such as appropriate footwear and insoles, if needed.
  • Consider referral to an orthopaedic surgeon,irrespective of the person's age, body mass index (BMI), or co-morbidities,before the person has severe functional limitation and/or pain if:
    • Symptoms of joint pain, stiffness, and functional impairment are refractoryto primary care managementand
    • There is a significantimpacton the person's quality of life.
    • There is uncertainty about the diagnosis or atypical symptoms.
    • There is a sudden worsening of symptoms.
      • Surgical treatment usually involvesjoint replacement or fusion.Arthroscopic lavage and debridement may be considered for people with knee osteoarthritis with a clear history of mechanical locking. Hand involvement may be treated by excision of the trapezium or joint fusion.
      • Complications of joint replacementmay include aseptic loosening, pain, dislocation, infection, and fracture.
  • Consider referral to a pain clinic if:
    • Pain is uncontrolled with optimal non-surgical and/or surgical management.
    • There is a suspected chronic pain syndrome which requires specialist management.
  • Consider referral to psychology or specialist mental health services if:
    • There is co-morbid anxiety or depression which is not responding to primary care management. See the CKS topics onGeneralized anxiety disorder andDepression for more information.

Basis for recommendation

Therecommendations on follow-up and referral are largely based on theNational Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) clinical guideline Osteoarthritis: care and management [NICE, 2014],the European League Against Rheumatism (EULAR) expert consensus documents EULAR recommendations for the use of imaging in the clinical management of peripheral joint osteoarthritis[Sakellariou, 2017] andEULAR recommendations for the health professional's approach to pain managment in inflammatory arthritis and osteoarthritis[Geenen, 2018], the Osteoarthritis Research Society International (OARSI) publication OARSI guidelines for the non-surgical management of knee osteoarthritis[McAlindon, 2014], various Cochrane systematic reviews, and expert opinion inreview articles on osteoarthritis[Glyn-Jones, 2015], on hip osteoarthritis [Aresti, 2016], and on thumb osteoarthritis and thumb pain[Anakwe, 2011; Dickson et al, 2015].

Arranging referral to physiotherapy
  • The recommendations onexercise, stretching, and pacingarebased onthe NICE guideline [NICE, 2014], theEULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018],and a Cochrane systematic review[Fransen, 2014].
    • The NICE guideline foundstrong evidence from a randomized controlled trial (RCT)for the benefit of manual therapy alone compared with exercise for osteoarthritis of the hip or knee, when undertaken by physiotherapists.
    • The EULAR task force performed a systematic literature review which found that for general exercise, strength and resistance training, and aerobic exercise, the quality of studies was low to moderate, and effects on pain were mostly positivein osteoarthritis.
    • The Cochrane systematicreview found that completion of a supervised physiotherapy programme reduces pain and improves physical function in people with mild to moderate pain from osteoarthritis of the hip. The benefits were found to last 36 months after treatment.
  • The recommendation to consider protective joint supports and other orthoticsis based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014],the EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018], the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014], a Cochrane systematic review [Duivenvoorden, 2015], and expert opinion in a review article [Dickson et al, 2015].
    • The NICE guideline found some evidence for the effectiveness of aids anddevices for osteoarthritis of the hand. It concluded that thumb splints (of any design) can help reduce painand improve hand function.It also found some evidence for the effectiveness ofassistive devices (such as braces) for osteoarthritis of the hip and knee.
    • The EULAR task force performed a systematic literature review which found positive effects of orthotics on pain forsplints in osteoarthritis of the hand, and sleeves and elastic bandages in osteoarthritis of the knee.
    • The OARSI guidelines evaluated the effectiveness of knee braces, knee sleeves, and foot orthoses in the management of osteoarthritis of the knee, and found moderate-quality evidence of benefit regarding pain, joint stiffness, and function.
    • In contrast, theCochrane systematic review found inconclusive evidence on the benefits of bracing and insoles for pain, stiffness, function, and quality of life for people with medial compartment osteoarthritis of the knee.
  • The recommendation to considerintra-articular corticosteroid injections is based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014], a Cochrane systematic review [Juni, 2015],and expert opinion inreview articles[Dickson et al, 2015; Glyn-Jones, 2015] [Aresti, 2016].
    • The NICE guideline notes that intra-articular corticosteroids can reduce the the volume of synovitis in osteoarthritis, however this does not necessarily have a direct effect on pain, and the impact of treatment is difficult to predict. It found consistentevidence of short-term benefit (of up to one week) of intra-articular corticosteroids for osteoarthritis of the knee with regards to pain reduction and global functioning, but no conclusive results were found in placebo-controlled studies of osteoarthritis of thehip and thumb. It concluded that this treatment may have a role in the management of acute flares of jointpain.
    • This approach is supported by the OARSI guidelines, which found good-quality evidence of significant short-term pain relief for osteoarthritis of the knee.
    • In contrast, a Cochrane systematic review concluded that there were uncertain benefits from intra-articular corticosteroids after 16 weeks due to the low quality of the available data, which showed considerable heterogeneity between trials with generally small sample sizes.
    • A review article on osteoarthritis of the hip cites evidence from RCTsthat corticosteroid injections provide short-term benefit for pain and function [Aresti, 2016].
    • Similarly, a review article states that injections to the base of the thumb may provide short-term benefit for people with moderate to severe pain [Dickson et al, 2015].
Arranging referral to occupational therapy
  • The recommendation to refer to occupational therapyis based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014],theEULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018], and the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014].
    • The NICE guideline assessed evidence from RCTsand non-controlled studies, and concluded that aids and devices are accepted by many people with osteoarthritis, who report high rates of satisfaction with their use.It also found some evidence for the effectiveness of walking aids and other assistive devices for osteoarthritis of the hip and knee. Walking aidscan significantly improve stride length and cadence (steps taken per minute).
    • The OARSI guidelines found limited, moderate-quality evidence to recommend walking aids such as a walking stick for people with knee-only osteoarthritis, with benefits such as decreased pain, improved function and quality of life observed in a single-blind RCT.
Arranging referral to podiatry
  • The recommendation on provision of footwear and insoles is based on the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014] and is extrapolated fromthe EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018].
    • The NICE guideline foundlimited data for the effectiveness of insoles (either wedged or neutral) in reducing symptoms of osteoarthritis of the knee. In the context of a lack of good-qualitytrial data and the low cost of the intervention, the Guideline Development Group(GDG) recommended consideration offootware with shock-absorbing properties.
    • The EULAR task force performed a systematic literature review which found positive effects of orthotics on pain for orthopaedic shoes in the management of osteoarthritis of the knee.
Arranging referral to orthopaedic surgery
  • The recommendation to consider early referral to orthopaedics is largely based on expert consensus of the GDG in the NICE guideline [NICE, 2014], together with theEULAR expert consensus document on imaging [Sakellariou, 2017], the OARSI consensus guidelines [McAlindon, 2014], and expert opinion in review articles[Glyn-Jones, 2015] [Aresti, 2016].
    • The NICE guideline notes that there is no evidence to restrictreferral for consideration of surgery based on the person's age, body mass index,or co-morbidities;and the elderly, overweight, or people with co-morbiditiesmay benefit more than the general population with osteoarthritis from joint surgery.
    • CKS notes that the NICE GDG do not recommend the use of osteoarthritis scoring tools to guide decisions on who to refer for consideration of surgery. These tools usually assess pain, functional impairment, and X-ray changes, andwere designed to measure population-based changes following surgery. Theyhave not been validated for the assessment of appropriateness of orthopaedic referral.
    • The OARSIguidelines recommend referral to an orthopaedic surgeon if non-surgical treatments are ineffective in the management of osteoarthritis of the knee.
    • Referral may allow specialist imaging such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), which may detect high-resolution early structural joint changes and can evaluate cartilage and soft tissues more accurately than plain X-ray (for example to identify synovial thickening or joint effusion), or ultrasound scanning which may detect soft tissue changes [Glyn-Jones, 2015; Sakellariou, 2017].
    • The information on hand surgery options is based on expert opinion in a review article [Anakwe, 2011].
    • The information on possible complications of joint replacement surgery are extrapolated from expert opinion in a review article [Aresti, 2016].
Arranging referral to a pain clinic
  • The recommendation to consider referral to a pain clinic is extrapolated from the EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018], on expert opinion in a review article [Aresti, 2016], and is also pragmatic, based on what CKS considers to be good medical practice.
Arranging referral to psychological services
  • The recommendation on provision of psychological support is based onthe EULAR expert consensus document on pain management [Geenen, 2018], and is also pragmatic, based on what CKS considers to be good medical practice.
    • Psychological and social interventions showed a uniform positive effect on pain for cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) on general osteoarthritis, and relaxation interventions in osteoarthritis of the hip and knee [Geenen, 2018].
