Scenario: Diarrhoea - prevention and advice for travellers

From age 1 month onwards.

How should I assess the risk of acquiring travellers' diarrhoea before travelling?

  • Ask which country the person is going to visit.
    • Low risk areas (fewer than 7% of travellers experience travellers' diarrhoea) include western Europe, USA, Canada, Japan, Australia, and New Zealand. 
    • Intermediate risk areas (8–20% of travellers experience travellers' diarrhoea) include southern Europe, Israel, South Africa, and some Caribbean and Pacific islands.
    • High risk areas (more than 20% of travellers experience travellers' diarrhoea) include Africa, Latin America, the Middle East, and most parts of Asia. 
  • For assessment of individual countries where travellers should practice strict food, water, and personal hygiene precautions, see the National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC) website (www.nathnac.org). 
  • Assess for other risk factors known to further increase the risk of travellers' diarrhoea such as: 
    • Local amenities and sanitation — in areas with low standards of hygiene and sanitation and poor control over the safety of food, drink, and drinking water, the risk of acquiring travellers' diarrhoea is high. 
    • How the person will be travelling — backpackers, campers, adventurers, and passengers on cruise ships are at increased risk.
    • When the person will be travelling — the peak incidence of travellers' diarrhoea is in the warmer months. Lower rates occur in winter.
    • What the person eats — for example risks are associated with food from buffets, restaurants, and street vendors, and certain types of food such as raw fish and seafood, salads, and meat and poultry that has been inadequately cooked. For more information, see Advice on food and drink. 
  • Review the person's susceptibility to traveller's diarrhoea and their risk of complications. For more information on which groups of people are considered to be at higher risk, see the section on Risk factors. 
  • Enquire about access to medical amenities — this is particularly important for those travelling to remote areas with few or no medical amenities (especially trekking, camping).
  • Discuss whether journeys to high-risk areas are essential and whether the person is suitably prepared (for example if backpacking through remote areas).

Basis for recommendation

Assessing risk of travellers' diarrhoea
  • The recommendation to perform a pre-travel assessment is based on advice from the World Health Organization (WHO) which suggests medical advisers base their recommendations on an assessment of risk for the individual person, considering features such as the likelihood of acquiring a disease and the potential consequences for the person. Examples include the destination, standards of accomodation and food hygiene, mode of transport, pretravel health status, and purpose of travel [WHO, 2012].  
Destination
  • The travel destination is regarded as an important determinant of risk for travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a]. Countries and places with generally poor standards of hygiene pose a greater risk. Although the risks are greater in developing countries, locations with poor hygiene may be present in any country [WHO, 2012].  
  • The examples of low, intermediate, and high-risk areas of travel are based on information from the National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC) [NaTHNaC, 2016]. 
Other risk factors associated with the destination of travel
  • The recommendation to assess for other risk factors is extrapolated from information published by the WHO [WHO, 2012] and a Canadian advisory committee statement on traveller's diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015], as well as expert opinion in review articles [Leder, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Barrett, 2016; Giddings et al, 2016].  
Susceptibility to travellers' diarrhoea and risk of complications
  • A number of guidelines and experts discuss people who are at more risk of developing travellers' diarrhoea or experiencing adverse consequences. CKS has extrapolated this recommendation from these sources [CATMAT, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Barrett, 2016; NaTHNaC, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. 
Access to medical amenities
  • Some travellers may have difficulty accessing reliable medical care [WHO, 2012].  Whether the person has access to appropriate medical care will influence the decision whether to provide the person with 'stand-by' treatment for travellers' diarrhoea [Giddings et al, 2016].  
Importance of journeys to high risk areas
  • This recommendation is pragmatic and based on what CKS considers to be good practice.

How should I manage someone at low or intermediate risk of travellers' diarrhoea?

  • For people at low or intermediate risk of travellers' diarrhoea:
    • Advise the person that most travellers' diarrhoea is caused by the consumption of contaminated food or water.
    • Reassure the person that no special precautions (other than basic hygiene measures) are required when travelling to locations with a high standard of hygiene.
    • Provide information on food hygiene and safe drinking water if the person is travelling to locations with low standards of hygiene and sanitation.
    • Offer advice regarding self-management and when to seek medical advice if the person develops diarrhoea during their trip.

Basis for recommendation

  • These recommendations reflect general advice issued to travellers by the UK National Travel Health Network and Centre [NaTHNaC, 2016] US guidelines and a Canadian advisory committee statement [CATMAT, 2015; Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017], information from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC, 2017a; CDC, 2017b], and expert opinion in review articles [Leder, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016].  
  • Although the risks of travellers' diarrhoea are greater in developing countries, all travellers should be reminded of basic hygiene measures because locations with poor hygiene may be present in any country [WHO, 2012]. 

How should I manage someone at high risk of travellers' diarrhoea?

  • For people at high risk:
    • Emphasize the importance of personal hygiene, as well as food hygiene and safe drinking water, to reduce the risk of travellers' diarrhoea.
      • For further information, see advice on food and drink.
    • Warn travellers about the risk of food- and water-borne infections and how to avoid potentially contaminated recreational water. Contaminated recreational water can be found in inadequately treated pools, water playgrounds, hot tubs, spas, and freshwater or sea water. Potential illness-causing contaminants include human faeces, animal waste, sewage, or wastewater runoff.
    • Do not routinely offer prophylactic treatment for prevention of travellers' diarrhoea, as it is not indicated for most travellers.
      • Antibiotic prophylaxis may be appropriate for certain high-risk travellers. For more information, see the section on prophylactic antibiotics.
      • The use of bismuth subsalicylate and probiotics for prophylaxis is not recommended.
      • Antidiarrhoeal drugs (such as loperamide) should not be taken prophylactically.
    • Consider whether 'stand by' antibiotic treatment (to use if affected) is appropriate. For more information, see the section on 'stand-by' antibiotics.
    • Inform the person that there are no universal vaccines to cover all the infections which may cause travellers' diarrhoea. However, travellers to risky areas must seek advice about appropriate vaccination against other intestinal infections such as cholera, hepatitis A, and typhoid. For further information on vaccines recommended for overseas travel and extended holidays, or for people working overseas, see the CKS topic on Immunizations - travel.
    • Offer advice regarding self-management and when to seek medical advice if the person develops diarrhoea during their trip.
      • For further information, see advice on managing travellers' diarrhoea.

Basis for recommendation

Personal and food hygiene, safe drinking water, and avoiding potentially contaminated recreational water.

  • These recommendations are based on information from the UK National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNac) [NaTHNaC, 2016]; the World Health Organization (WHO) [WHO, 2012]; US guidance and information issued by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC, 2017b; CDC, 2017a], the American College of Gastroenterology [Riddle, 2016], the Infectious Diseases Society of America [Shane, 2017]; and guidance from an advisory committee to the Public Health Agency of Canada [CATMAT, 2015]. 
Prophylactic treatments
  • Apart from the use of prophylactic antibiotics for certain at-risk groups, the WHO does not recommend any particular measures for the prophylaxis of travellers' diarrhoea [WHO, 2012]. 
  • Prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended for most travellers because they do not protect against nonbacterial pathogens, are associated with allergic and adverse reactions, and may contribute to drug resistance [NaTHNaC, 2016; CDC, 2017b; Riddle, 2017]. 
  • A number of organisations and experts suggest that bismuth subsalicylate may be considered as an option for the prevention of travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; Riddle, 2017; Taylor et al, 2017]. However, although bismuth subsalicylate is mentioned for prevention of travellers' diarrhoea in its summary of antimicrobial prescribing guidance, Public Health England states that ‘prophylaxis is rarely if ever indicated’ [NICE and PHE, 2018] and the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention note that bismuth subsalicylate is not commonly used as prophylaxis for travellers' diarrhoea [CDC, 2017a]. Bismuth subsalicylate is also off-label for the prophylaxis of travellers' diarrhoea [ABPI, 2015; MHRA, 2019]. CKS has not recommended the primary care intiation of bismuth subsalicylate for prophylaxis taking this into account, in addition to feedback from previous expert reviewers of this CKS topic and other factors limiting the usefulness of bismuth subsalicylate as a prophylactic treatment, for example: 
    • Compliance may be difficult as the treatment needs to be taken four times a day [CATMAT, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2016; Taylor et al, 2017]. 
    • Prophylaxis is recommended for up to 3 weeks (based on duration of trials) [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a] and the quantity required might be inconvenient [CDC, 2017a]. 
    • People may find the adverse effects unpleasant, particularly black tongue and stool, constipation, nausea, and tinnitus [CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017].  
    • It is not suitable for people with aspirin or salicylate allergy, renal insufficiency, gout, or those who are taking an anticoagulant such as warfarin, or pregnant or breastfeeding women [ABPI, 2015; Riddle, 2017; MHRA, 2019]. 
    • There is potential for drug interactions, including bismuth subsalicylate reducing absorption of doxycycline which may also be required for malaria prophylaxis [Giddings et al, 2016].  
    • Pepto-bismol® is not licensed for children younger than 16 years of age due to a possible association between salicylates and Reye's syndrome update [MHRA, 2019].  
  • Probiotics are not recommended for the prevention of travellers' diarrhoea because of insufficient evidence to support their use [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017]. A meta-analysis found a statistically significant effect of probiotics in preventing travellers' diarrhoea. However, the findings were limited by methodological issues including different types of probiotics included in the studies, and different routes of administration [Bae, 2018]. 
  • The WHO warns that antidiarrhoeal medicines such as loperamide are contraindicated for prophylactic use [WHO, 2012] and loperamide is not licensed for this purpose [ABPI, 2018a].
Considering 'stand-by' antibiotics
  • The use of 'stand-by' antibiotics for certain travellers is discussed in information from the UK National Travel Health Network and Centre (NathNac) [NaTHNaC, 2016], and the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC, 2017a], in addition to guidance from the American College of Gastroenterologists [Riddle, 2016], and an expert consensus panel [Riddle, 2017].  
Vaccinations
  • There is no universal vaccine to protect against the general syndrome of travellers' diarrhoea and no widely available vaccines for the most frequent causes of travellers' diarrhoea [NaTHNaC, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. However, vaccines are available for some causes of gastrointestinal upset while travelling (for example hepatitis A and typhoid vaccines may be given prior to travel; rotavirus vaccine is part of the routine immunisation schedule for children in the UK) [Barrett and Brown, 2018; PHE, 2018].  
Advice on self-management and seeking medical advice
  • This recommendation is extrapolated from advice in US guidelines on the prevention and treatment of travellers diarrhoea [Riddle, 2017] and expert opinion in review articles [Leder, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016]. 

What food and drink measures are recommended for preventing travellers' diarrhoea?

  • Advice on general hygiene
    • Hands should always be washed with soap and water before handling and consuming food (particularly after contact with raw meat and uncooked food), using the bathroom, or caring for an ill person.
    • If soap and water are not available, an alcohol-based hand sanitiser can be used as an alternative until it is possible to wash the hands. Note that hand sanitizer is not very effective when hands are greasy or visibly dirty and has limited effectiveness against Cryptosporidium and norovirus.
  • Advice on food and drink
    • Food must have been thoroughly cooked, and must remain steaming hot prior to serving.
      • Raw fish and seafood should be avoided, as well as shellfish, and any meat or poultry that has not been thoroughly cooked.
      • Risks are associated with food from buffets, restaurants, and street vendors. Food from these sources should be avoided, if it has neither been kept hot, nor refrigerated or kept on ice.
      • Cooked food that has been in contact with raw food, or contains raw or uncooked eggs (such as homemade mayonnaise), should be avoided.
    • If hygiene and sanitation are likely to be inadequate, avoid salads, uncooked vegetables, and unpasteurised fruit juices. Fruits and vegetables that can be peeled or shelled are suitable choices (fruits and vegetables with damaged skins should be avoided).
    • Ice should be avoided, unless it has been made from safe water.
    • Unpasteurized (raw) milk or cheese should be avoided.
    • Carbonated bottled beverages are usually safe to drink provided they are sealed.
  • Advice on drinking water
    • Tap water should be avoided for drinking, preparing food, making ice, cooking, and brushing teeth, unless there is reasonable certainty it is safe.
    • Bottled water is the safer choice for drinking water. The seal must not have been tampered with.
    • Water should be boiled (for at least 1 minute) if its safety for drinking is in doubt.
    • Other options include micropore filtering and the use of disinfectant preparations. These might be more relevant for people travelling to areas where there is little or no access to safe drinking water.
  • Information in the form of a fact sheet on Food and Water Hygiene is available on the Travel Health Pro website (travelhealthpro.org.uk). The World Health Organization also produces a Guide on safe food for travellers (available at www.who.int). 

Basis for recommendation

Advice on general hygiene
  • These recommendations are based on information issued by the World Health Organization (WHO) [WHO, 2010] and Canadian and US experts [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017b]. The evidence on handwashing and use of alcohol-based hand sanitizers is mixed, but theoretically these measures should be the most effective to prevent enteric infection from organisms that can cause illness at low inoculum doses [Riddle, 2016].  
Advice on food and drink
  • High-quality evidence that specific dietary measures can reduce the incidence of episodes of travellers' diarrhoea is lacking [Barrett, 2016; Riddle, 2016], but considering that the majority of cases are caused by ingestion of contaminated food and water, it is thought that discussing food and water hygiene measures reduces the risk of developing travellers' diarrhoea [Giddings et al, 2016]. Factors beyond the control of the traveller may also influence the chances of travellers' diarrhoea (such as poor restaurant hygiene) [Giddings et al, 2016].   
  • However, experts suggest advising common sense with food selection [Leder, 2015], and the recommendations are based on suggestions from the World Health Organization [WHO, 2012], Canadian and US organisations [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017b], and expert opinion [Leder, 2015; Barrett, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. 
Advice on drinking water
  • These recommendations reflect advice from Canadian and US experts [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017b].  

What should I advise about self-management of travellers' diarrhoea?

  • Advise the person: 
    • That most episodes of travellers' diarrhoea are short-lived and self-limiting, and last a few days. 
    • To consider purchasing sachets of oral rehydration salts before travelling as they may not be available at the destination. 
  • Advise that during an episode of diarrhoea:
    • It is important to prevent dehydration — particularly for young children, pregnant women, elderly people, and those with pre-existing illnesses.
      • For infants, breastfeeding should not be interrupted.
      • Hydration must be maintained, for example with boiled, treated, or sealed bottled water. Most otherwise healthy adults can stay hydrated by eating and drinking as normal.
      • Alcohol and other drinks and beverages with a diuretic effect (such as coffee and tea) and overly sweet drinks (in large quantities) should be avoided.
      • For more severe symptoms, or people prone to complications from dehydration, oral rehydration powders diluted into clean drinking water also help to correct electrolyte imbalances.
      • Immediate medical attention is required if children show signs of dehydration such as restlessness or irritability, great thirst, sunken eyes, and dry skin with reduced elasticity.
    • Symptomatic treatment with loperamide or bismuth subsalicylate (for example Pepto-Bismol®) may be considered for adults to relieve mild-to-moderate diarrhoea for a maximum of two days. 
      • Loperamide is usually considered the standard treatment when rapid control of symptoms is required — for example during travelling where toilet amenities are limited or unavailable. 
      • Bismuth subsalicylate is more suitable for mild diarrhoea; loperamide has a faster onset of action and is more effective than bismuth subsalicylate for controlling diarrhoea and cramping.
      • Bismuth subsalicylate is not suitable for people with aspirin allergy, renal insufficiency, gout, severe enteric disease or HIV (risk of bismuth absorption), or people who are taking an anticoagulant such as warfarin, or pregnant or breastfeeding women. Darkened tongue and stools are common adverse effects.
  • Advise the person not to use loperamide or bismuth subsalicylate (for example Pepto-Bismol®) if they have blood or mucous in the stool and/or high fever or severe abdominal pain. 
  • Neither loperamide nor bismuth subsalicylate (for example Pepto-Bismol®) are recommended for children to relieve diarrhoeal symptoms. 
    • Loperamide should not be used in children younger than 12 years of age because of concerns that it may cause intestinal obstruction. 
    • Bismuth subsalicylate must not be used in children younger than 16 years of age because of the possible association between salicylates and Reye's syndrome. 
  • Medical assistance must be sought if:
    • Stools are blood-stained, profuse and watery, or there is high or persistent fever or severe abdominal pain.
    • Severe diarrhoea occurs in infants or elderly people, or a child is unwell with travellers' diarrhoea and symptomatic treatment is needed.
    • The person becomes dehydrated — particularly in infants and children (restlessness or irritability, very thirsty, sunken eyes, and dry skin with reduced elasticity); or it is difficult to maintain adequate hydration.
    • Diarrhoea does not begin to improve within 24–36 hours despite self-treatment. 
    • There is a medical comorbidity (for example immunosuppression or gastrointestinal disorder).
  • Advice for travellers can be found on the following websites:
    • NHS: www.nhs.uk—Access to healthcare abroad.
    • The National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC): www.nathnac.net.
    • The 'Fit for travel' website provided by NHS Scotland: www.fitfortravel.scot.nhs.uk.
    • The World Health Organization: www.who.int.

Basis for recommendation

Advice on usual timecourse of travellers' diarrhoea and purchasing oral rehydration salts
  • The information on the usual short-lived and self-limiting nature of an episode of travellers diarrhoea is based on information from the World Health Organization (WHO) [WHO, 2012] and the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC, 2017a]. 
  • The advice to consider purchasing oral rehydration salts before travelling reflects expert opinion in review articles on advising travellers about the management of travellers' diarrhoea [Leder, 2015] and travellers' diarrhoea in children [Ashkenazi et al, 2016]. 
Measures to prevent dehydration
  • These recommendations are based on information issued by the WHO on international travel and health and safe food for travellers [WHO, 2010; WHO, 2012], UK advice for travellers from the National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC) [NaTHNaC, 2016], and US and Canadian recommendations on travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a].  
Symptomatic treatment for adults
  • Expert consensus guidance suggests that, given the concerns regarding potential adverse effects of antibiotics and antimicrobial resistance patterns, either increasing fluid intake or treatment with loperamide or bismuth subsalicylate may be sufficient for most people with mild travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; Riddle, 2017].  
  • CKS found limited evidence to support the use of bismuth subsalicylate and loperamide for travellers' diarrhoea in adults. Trials were generally of poor methodological quality. 
Loperamide
  • Loperamide is recommended as an option to consider for the symptomatic treatment of mild to moderate travellers' diarrhoea by Canadian and US experts [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017], expert opinion in travel information for healthcare professionals [NaTHNaC, 2016], the British National Formulary [BNF 76, 2018], and review articles [Steffen, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. Loperamide reduces frequency of bowel movements, taking 1–2 hours to reach its therapeutic effect, and appears to have antisecretory properties [CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017].  It has a well-established safety profile and is usually well-tolerated [Steffen, 2015; CDC, 2017a].  
    • The evidence supporting the efficacy of loperamide for travellers' diarrhoea is stronger than that for bismuth subsalicylate [Riddle, 2017] but is still limited. There is very limited evidence from two small studies that loperamide can reduce the frequency of unformed stools in the early stages (within the first 24 hours) compared with placebo [van Loon et al, 1989; Ericsson et al, 1990]. Other outcome measures (for example symptom reduction) are generally poorly reported. One small trial found no difference when loperamide was compared with loperamide plus oral rehydration salt solution [Caeiro et al, 1999].  
    • A systematic review found a small amount of data comparing loperamide with antibiotics and found no proof of antibiotics being more effective for the treatment of travellers' diarrhoea. The review authors therefore advised antibiotics only for severe illness and noted the value of loperamide for symptom control, particularly given increasing antimicrobial resistance [Laaveri et al, 2016].  A subsequent guideline found no randomized controlled trial (RCT) evidence comparing loperamide with azithromycin or fluoroquinolones [Riddle, 2017].  
    • There is weak evidence from two unblinded trials in students with travellers' diarrhoea that loperamide is more effective than bismuth subsalicylate at reducing the number of unformed stools in the initial stage of treatment (generally within the first 24 hours). However, the differences were small and the clinical significance is uncertain [Johnson et al, 1986; DuPont et al, 1990]. 
Bismuth subsalicylate
  • Bismuth subsalicylate is recommended as an option to consider for the symptomatic treatment of mild travellers' diarrhoea by a number of experts and organisations [CATMAT, 2015; NaTHNaC, 2016; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017]. It is well tolerated, with usually mild adverse effects in adults [Riddle, 2017], although it is slower to act than loperamide; may be less effective; cannot be used in a number of groups of people, and has the potential for interactions with a person's other medications [Moore, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017]. 
    • There is limited evidence from two small RCTs that, compared with placebo, bismuth subsalicylate can reduce the number of unformed stools and the time to the last unformed stool. However, in both cases the differences were small [DuPont et al, 1977; Steffen et al, 1988]. 
Recommended precautions for loperamide and bismuth subsalicylate (for example Pepto-Bismol®)
  • These are in line with information from NaTHNaC [NaTHNaC, 2016], the British National Formulary [BNF 76, 2018], and recommendations issued by the manufacturers of loperamide and bismuth subsalicylate [ABPI, 2015; ABPI, 2018a; MHRA, 2019]. 
  • CKS's recommendation not to use loperamide or bismuth subsalicylate beyond 2 days is consistent with information from the manufacturers of loperamide and Pepto-Bismol® [ABPI, 2018a; MHRA, 2019], and US guidelines covering the management of acute diarrhoea infections in adults [Riddle, 2016]. 
  • Loperamide should be avoided in people with fever or bloody diarrhoea, and in people with active inflammatory bowel disease [NaTHNaC, 2016; CDC, 2017a]; loperamide can inhibit peristalsis, resulting in complications such as ileus, megacolon, and toxic megacolon [ABPI, 2018a]. 
Symptomatic treatment for children is not recommended
  • The recommendation to avoid loperamide in children younger than 12 years of age is in line with guidance from the WHO (which advises against the use of antimotility drugs because they may cause intestinal obstruction) and travel advice from NaTHNaC. This advises that parents seek early advice for children in this group who become unwell with travellers' diarrhoea and need symptomatic treatment, emphasising that rehydration is the main treatment for young children [WHO, 2010; NaTHNaC, 2016]. Loperamide is only licensed for use in children older than 12 years of age [ABPI, 2018a] and there is limited data on its use in children less than 12 years old [ABPI, 2018b]. 
  • Bismuth salicylate (Pepto-Bismol®) is not licensed for use in children younger than 16 years of age because of the possible association between salicylates and Reye's syndrome [ABPI, 2015; MHRA, 2019]. It is also not recommended for children by Public Health England in recommendations on antimicrobial prescribing for travellers diarrhoea [NICE and PHE, 2018], and some experts also advise against its use in this group [Moore, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016].  
When to seek medical assistance
  • The recommendations on when to seek medical assistance are in line with guidance issued by the WHO [WHO, 2010], the American College of Gastroenterology and an expert panel [Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017], information published by NaTHNaC [NaTHNaC, 2016], and expert opinion [Leder, 2015; Moore, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018].  

When should I consider prescribing a prophylactic antibiotic for travellers' diarrhoea?

  • Do not routinely prescribe prophylactic antibiotic treatments for travellers' diarrhoea.
  • The decision to prescribe prophylactic antibiotic treatments depends on an assessment of:
    • The risk of the person developing travellers' diarrhoea (see Assessing risk (before travelling)).
    • The potential harm to the person if they were to develop travellers' diarrhoea.
    • The benefits and risks of antibiotic treatment. 
    • Access to medical assistance while travelling.
  • Consider prophylactic antibiotic treatment only for people who are at high risk of travellers' diarrhoea and its consequences — seek specialist advice before prescribing. The National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC) provides a telephone advice line for health professionals advising travellers with complex itineraries or specialist health needs. For more information, see the NaTHNaC website (www.nathnac.net). Groups who may benefit from prophylactic treatment include those who:
    • Have increased susceptibility to infection or are immunocompromised.
    • Have a high risk of complications if they were to develop travellers' diarrhoea, for example:
      • People with chronic gastrointestinal disease (such as Crohn's disease, or ulcerative colitis). 
      • People with an ileostomy or colostomy.
      • People with other diseases (such as malignancy, type 1 diabetes mellitus, renal disease, stroke, congestive heart failure, or sickle cell disease) in whom a diarrhoeal illness might severely impact on their health.
    • Have a history of significant long-term morbidity after an enteric infection (for example reactive arthritis).
    • Are undertaking critical trips in which a short bout of diarrhoea could severely impact the purpose of the trip (for example athlete, politician, professional musician, lecturer).
    • May not have access to adequate medical treatment if they experience complicated travellers' diarrhoea.

Basis for recommendation

Avoiding routine prophylactic treatment
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis has been shown in studies to give up to 90% protection against developing travellers' diarrhoea, although effectiveness depends on the choice of antibiotic and the area of travel [Giddings et al, 2016]. 
  • However, the World Health Organization (WHO) warns that the use of prophylactic antibiotics is controversial [WHO, 2012] (with some experts not recommending this [NIH Consensus Development Program, 1985]). More recent guidelines also emphasise the growing problem of antibiotic resistance associated with travellers' diarrhoea and self-treatment and its potential consequences for individual and population health [Riddle, 2017].  
  • The recommendations on avoidance of routine use of antibiotic prophylaxis and factors to consider when deciding whether prophylactic treatment may be an option are supported by UK, US, and Canadian experts [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017; NICE and PHE, 2018]. Reasons for not routinely prescribing antibiotic prophylaxis include [WHO, 2012; CATMAT, 2015; Leder, 2015; Riddle, 2016; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017]: 
    • For most people, travellers' diarrhoea is mild and self-limiting.
    • Antibiotic prophylaxis offers no protection against non-bacterial causes of travellers' diarrhoea (such as viruses and parasites).
    • Removal of normally protective microflora from the bowel with prophylactic antibiotics can increase the risk of infection with resistant bacteria.
    • Use of prophylactic antibiotics reduces the choices for treatment if required for travellers' diarrhoea.
    • Taking antibiotic prophylaxis may give the traveller a false sense of security, which might lead to neglect of food and water precautions.
    • The increasing prevalence of antibiotic resistance is of concern (including to fluoroquinolones).
    • No antibiotics are licensed in the UK for the prophylaxis of travellers' diarrhoea.
    • Antibiotic treatment can lead to adverse effects and routine use of antibiotics for prophylaxis could expose the person to risks greater than that of the illness.
When to consider prophylactic treatment

  • Some organisations and experts acknowledge that there is a role for the use of prophylactic antibiotics in certain high risk groups of travellers [WHO, 2012; Leder, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2016; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017; Barrett and Brown, 2018], with US guidelines discussing potential benefit for travellers at high risk of travellers' diarrhoea and susceptible to potentially serious health consequences [Riddle, 2016]. However, UK antimicrobial prescribing guidance issued by Public Health England states that prophylaxis is rarely, if ever indicated [NICE and PHE, 2018] and US guidelines on the prevention and treatment of travellers' diarrhoea advises self treatment of moderate to severe travellers' diarrhoea as the standard approach, with 'chemoprophylaxis the rare exception' [Riddle, 2017]. 

    • There is a lack of evidence to support the effectiveness of antibiotic prophylaxis for people travelling to countries with low or intermediate risk of travellers' diarrhoea. One double-blind trial in 127 participants found antibiotic treatment (norfloxacin) to be effective in reducing the number of Swedish travellers developing diarrhoea in high-risk countries (Africa, Asia, or Latin America, p = 0.0004) but not for those travelling to low-risk countries (Mediterranean Europe or the Canary Islands) [Wiström et al, 1987].

  • The groups of travellers for whom antibiotic prophylaxis may be considered are in line with recommendations issued by the WHO [WHO, 2012], along with guidelines from the US and an advisory committee statement from Canada [CATMAT, 2015; Riddle, 2016; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017], and expert opinion [DuPont, 2008; DuPont et al, 2009; Leder, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Barrett, 2016; Giddings et al, 2016; DuPont and Steffen, 2017; Taylor et al, 2017; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. 

Seeking specialist advice if prophylactic antibiotic treatment is being considered
  • Planning prophylactic treatment is complex due to geographic variation and the large number of potential pathogens, particularly if the person has a comorbidity (for example requiring immunosuppressant medication) [Zaidi and Wine, 2015], and the prophylactic treatment options discussed by experts all have their limitations. For example: 
    • Fluoroquinolones have evidence to support their role as prophylactic treatment for travellers' diarrhoea, but their use is limited by emerging resistance, particularly to Campylobacter in Nepal and Thailand and types of E. coli in India, which may mean their efficacy is now less than suggested by historic trials [CATMAT, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017]. Their safety profile may be considered to be unacceptable for a prophylactic indication, due to serious adverse effects such as cartilage damage, tendon rupture, and C. difficile-associated diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; Riddle, 2016]. In addition, use of fluoroquinolones for prophylaxis limits therapeutic options if travellers' diarrhoea develops [Taylor et al, 2017].  
    • Rifaximin is a non-absorbable antibiotic for which there is evidence of efficacy (although it does not prevent all cases of travellers' diarrhoea), with a favourable safety profile [Riddle, 2016; Riddle, 2017; Taylor et al, 2017]. It is not associated with antmicrobial resistance in travellers and does not appear to modify the gut microflora [CATMAT, 2015; Taylor et al, 2017], but Campylobacter is not susceptible, meaning it only offers moderate protection in South and South East Asia [Riddle, 2017].  
    • Azithromycin is lacking in evidence to support its use as a prophylactic agent for travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; Taylor et al, 2017] and is useful as a therapeutic option for travellers' diarrhoea, particularly in South and South East Asia [Leder, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2017; Taylor et al, 2017; Barrett and Brown, 2018] and in cases of dysentery [Riddle, 2017].  
  • Public Health England antimicrobial prescribing guidance does not offer an antibiotic option for prophylaxis of travellers' diarrhoea [NICE and PHE, 2018]. Taking this into account, along with the uncertainty regarding choice of prophylactic antibiotic treatment, its disadvantages, and the mixed opinion on which groups of people are likely to benefit, CKS has recommended seeking specialist advice if considering prescribing antibiotic prophylaxis for the prevention of travellers' diarrhoea.

When should I prescribe a 'stand-by' antibiotic for treatment of travellers' diarrhoea?

  • Do not routinely prescribe 'stand-by' (to be used if affected) treatments for travellers' diarrhoea.
  • Consider a 'stand-by' (to be used if affected) antibiotic for people who are:
    • Travelling to high-risk locations (especially where access to medical assistance is poor or not available, or there is doubt about the safety and purity of antibiotics available at their destination). 
    • At high risk of severe illness.
  • Seek specialist advice if considering prescribing a 'stand-by' antibiotic. 
    • The National Travel Health Network and Centre (NaTHNaC) provides a telephone advice line for health professionals advising travellers with complex itineraries or specialist health needs. For more information, see the NaTHNaC website (www.nathnac.net).

Basis for recommendation

Not routinely offering a 'stand-by' antibiotic
  • Most cases of travellers' diarrhoea are mild and self-limiting and will resolve without treatment [CATMAT, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2017].  
  • Risks of providing 'stand-by' antibiotics have also been highlighted, such as increasing antimicrobial resistance [Laaveri et al, 2016; Taylor et al, 2017; Barrett and Brown, 2018; Riddle et al, 2018]. The use of antibiotics for travellers' diarrhoea has been associated with acquiring extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) enterobacteriacae organisms, which may be harmful to certain groups of people, for example those who are immunocompromised or prone to urinary tract infections [Kantele et al, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Kantele et al, 2016; CDC, 2017a]. Use of antibiotics may also influence the traveller's microbiota, potentially leading to infection with Clostridium difficile [CDC, 2017a]. 
  • Some experts recommend that most, if not all, travellers should have an antibiotic for self treatment [Riddle, 2017]. However, others have questioned the routine use of empirical antibiotics [Taylor et al, 2017] and an advisory committee advises limited use of antibiotics for the treatment of travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015]. 
When to consider prescribing a 'stand-by' antibiotic
  • The recommendations on when to consider providing a ‘stand-by’ antibiotic for an adult are based on Public Health England antimicrobial prescribing guidance [NICE and PHE, 2018] and expert opinion in review articles [Giddings et al, 2016; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. In making a decision about prescribing 'stand-by' treatment, US experts advise that the advantages of antibiotic treatment should be considered alongside concerns about the traveller and antibiotics [CDC, 2017a]. 
  • There is evidence that antibiotics reduce the duration of illness to about one and a half days [Steffen, 2015; Riddle, 2017], have the advantages of reducing the stress and inconvenience of changing travel plans, and avoid the need for a traveller to seek care at their destination from local providers which may be ineffective or unsafe. There is also the possibility that early antibiotic treatment may reduce the risk of chronic sequelae such as irritable bowel syndrome [Riddle, 2017].
Seeking specialist advice if considering a 'stand-by' antibiotic
  • CKS recommends seeking specialist advice if considering prescribing a 'stand-by' antibiotic for travellers' diarrhoea because of the complexities surrounding the appropriate choice and the need to take into account a number of factors including efficacy, destination (and associated likely pathogens and resistance patterns), potential for adverse effects, and acceptability of the treatment regimen [Steffen, 2015; Giddings et al, 2016; Riddle, 2017]. Possible options for 'stand-by' antibiotic treatment (on specialist advice) include:
    • Azithromycin — although it is not licensed for the treatment of travellers' diarrhoea [ABPI, 2018c], azithromycin is particularly useful if there is severe travellers' diarrhoea, dysentery, high fever, travel to south and south East Asia, and if rifaximin is used as prophylaxis [Leder, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017; Barrett and Brown, 2018]. Azithromycin is well-tolerated by most people, and has a more convenient dosing schedule compared with quinolones [Leder, 2015; Riddle, 2017]. 
    • Fluoroquinolones — these are effective in much of the developing world, but their potential use is limited due to increasing fluoroquinolone resistance in Campylobacter, Shigella, and Salmonella species, particularly in South and South East Asia, where a significant proportion of travellers' diarrhoea is due to Campylobacter [Leder, 2015; CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017; Taylor et al, 2017]. Fluoroquinolones also may be less effective in cases of dysentery as these may be caused by a fluoroquinolone-resistant Campylobacter or Shigella species [Riddle, 2017]. There have also been concerns about the adverse effect profile of fluoroquinolones, including tendon rupture, C. difficile infection, and prolongation of the QT interval [CDC, 2017a; Riddle, 2017]. 
    • Rifaximin — this antibiotic is licensed for the treatment of travellers' diarrhoea that is not associated with fever, bloody diarrhoea, blood or leucocytes in the stools [ABPI, 2017]. Although there is evidence that rifaximin is effective for treating travellers' diarrhoea compared with placebo and has similar efficacy to fluoroquinolones in non-invasive travellers' diarrhoea [CATMAT, 2015; Steffen, 2015; Riddle, 2017], it is not effective for treating diarrhoea caused by invasive enteric pathogens such as Salmonella, Shigella, or Campylobacter [Steffen, 2015; ABPI, 2017; Tribble, 2017].
