Scenario: Antenatal care - uncomplicated pregnancy

From age 18 years to 40 years (Female).

Antenatal appointments

  • A woman with an uncomplicated pregnancy is usually managed in the community by a midwife. GPs, Obstetricians and specialist teams become involved if additional care is needed.
  • The time of the first contact will vary. This CKS topic provides a schedule of the antenatal appointments required if a woman presents to her GP prior to 10 weeks of pregnancy.
  • In general, after the first contact:
    • Ten appointments are sufficient for a nulliparous woman (who has never given birth to a viable infant).
    • Seven appointments are sufficient for a parous woman (who has borne at least one viable offspring — usually after 24 weeks).
  • Management at each appointment varies depending on the stage of the pregnancy.

How should I manage a woman at first contact?

  • Identify women who:
    • May need additional care. Refer to a specialist as appropriate.
    • Are taking any medication (including over-the-counter drugs). If necessary, arrange for an immediate medication review.
      • If you are unsure about the safety of a medication during pregnancy, contact the UK Teratology Information Service on 0344 892 0909 for a patient-specific risk assessment.
  • Assess and document the risk factors for venous thromboembolism including:
    • History of thromboembolism.
    • Thrombophilia (such as Factor V Leiden deficiency, Antiphospholipid syndrome, Prothrombin 20210, Protein C, protein S or antithrombin deficiencies).
    • Other medical conditions (such as heart disease, sickle cell disease, systemic lupus erythematosus, obesity, anaemia, diabetes mellitus, or hypertension).
    • Smoking. 
      • Referral to secondary care may be required for women at high risk.
  • Advise the woman to take folic acid (400 micrograms per day) throughout the first 12 weeks of pregnancy (if she is not already doing so).
  • Give lifestyle advice regarding:
    • Diet.
    • Food hygiene, including how to reduce the risk of a food-acquired infection.
    • Smoking cessation.
    • The implications of alcohol consumption and recreational drug use.
  • Provide information on sources of support.
  • Discuss all antenatal screening, including screening for haemoglobinopathies, the anomaly scan and screening for Down's syndrome, as well as the purpose, risks, benefits, and limitations of the screening tests.
    • The NHS Fetal Anomaly Screening Programme has published a leaflet entitled Screening Tests For You and Your Baby, with detailed information on the risks and benefits of antenatal screening.

How should I manage a woman at the booking appointment?

  • If not carried out at first contact, identify women who may need additional care (such as those with pre-existing medical conditions and women at increased risk of pregnancy complications) and refer to an appropriate specialist.
  • Measure and record the woman's:
    • Height, weight, and body mass index. 
      • There is no need to measure height and weight at subsequent antenatal appointments unless there are concerns regarding the mother's nutrition.
    • Blood pressure and proteinuria.
  • Identify women with risk factors for pre-eclampsia.
    • If present, consider arranging additional blood pressure measurements to those at routine antenatal appointments.
      • For detailed information on how to manage women who are at high risk of pre-eclampsia or who have hypertension with or without proteinuria, see the CKS topic on Hypertension in pregnancy.
  • Discuss all antenatal screening tests and ultrasound scans. 
    • Discuss the purpose, risks, benefits, and limitations of these tests, so that the woman can make an informed choice regarding whether to have them.
    • The NHS Fetal Anomaly Screening Programme has published a leaflet entitled Screening Tests For You and Your Baby, with detailed information on the risks and benefits of antenatal screening.
  • Offer ultrasound scans:
    • Dating scan  —  normally between 10 and 13 weeks.
    • Fetal anomaly scan — normally between 18 and 20 weeks.
  • Offer fetal screening for Down's Syndrome.
    • The 'combined test' (nuchal translucency, serum beta-human chorionic gonadotrophin, serum pregnancy-associated plasma protein-A) should be offered to screen for Down's syndrome between 11 weeks 0 days and 13 weeks 6 days. 
    • For women who book later in pregnancy the most clinically and cost-effective serum screening test (triple or quadruple test) should be offered between 15 weeks 0 days and 20 weeks 0 days.
      • The triple test measures plasma levels of beta-human chorionic gonadotrophin, unconjugated estriol, and alpha-fetoprotein.
      • The quadruple test measures beta-human chorionic gonadotrophin, unconjugated estriol, alpha-fetoprotein, and inhibin A.
    • In all three of these tests, the results from these measurements, along with additional information about the mother (for example age, weight, weeks of pregnancy, family origin, and smoking details) are used to calculate the risk of the baby having Down's syndrome.
  • Offer blood tests to check blood group.
  • Offer maternal screening for anaemia, red cell alloantibodies (such as anti-D and less commonly anti-c and anti-Kell), and haemoglobinopathies (such as sickle cell disease and thalassaemias). 
    • Testing for red cell alloantibodies and haemoglobinopathies should be offered as early as possible in pregnancy (ideally by 10 weeks).
    • The type of haemoglobinopathy screening depends on the local prevalence of the disorder. The NHS Sickle Cell and Thalassaemia Screening Programme advises that in high-prevalence areas (more than 1.5 infants born with sickle cell disease per 10,000 births):
      • All pregnant women are offered screening for sickle cell disorders, thalassaemia, and other haemoglobin variants.
      • All partners of women who are identified as carriers of significant traits are offered screening.
      • The NHS Family Origin Questionnaire (FOQ) is used to determine the need for the partner to be screened.
    • In low-prevalence areas (1.5 infants or fewer born with sickle cell disease per 10,000 births):
      • All pregnant women are offered screening for thalassaemia using routine erythrocyte indices.
      • A FOQ is used to assess the risk of either the woman or her partner being a carrier for sickle cell, thalassaemia, and other haemoglobin variants.
      • If a woman is defined as high risk, she is offered additional screening.
      • The partners of all identified carrier mothers (regardless of family origin) are offered screening for sickle cell, other haemoglobin variants, and thalassaemia.
    • For more information, see the CKS topic on Sickle cell disease.
  • Offer maternal screening for:
    • Hepatitis B virus and HIV infection — to decrease the risk of mother-to-child transmission via early identification and intervention. For more information, see the CKS topics on Hepatitis B, and HIV infection and AIDS. 
    • Asymptomatic bacteriuria — to reduce the risk of pyelonephritis due to early intervention. For more information on how to manage symptomatic bacteriuria in pregnant women, see the CKS topic on Urinary tract infection (lower) - women.
    • Syphilis — if left untreated, this may lead to miscarriage, stillbirth, preterm birth, and other complications.
    • Chlamydia screening should not be offered as part of routine antenatal care. 
      • However, if the woman is younger than 25 years of age, inform her about the high prevalence of chlamydia infection in this age group and give details of her local National Chlamydia Screening Programme (www.chlamydiascreening.nhs.uk). For more information, see the CKS topic on Chlamydia - uncomplicated genital.
  • Offer influenza vaccination for all women pregnant during the influenza season (October to January). 
    • Outside of these dates, use clinical judgement to assess whether the woman should be immunized. For more information see the CKS topic on Immunizations - seasonal influenza.
  • Arrange testing for gestational diabetes for women with any of the following risk factors:
    • Body mass index above 30 kg/m2.
    • Previous macrosomic infant weighing 4.5 kg or above. 
    • Previous gestational diabetes.
    • Family history of diabetes (first-degree relative with diabetes).
    • Family origin with a high prevalence of diabetes such as:
      • South Asian (specifically women whose country of family origin is India, Pakistan, or Bangladesh).
      • Black Caribbean.
      • Middle Eastern (specifically women whose country of family origin is Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates, Iraq, Jordan, Syria, Oman, Qatar, Kuwait, Lebanon, or Egypt).
  • Advise the woman to take a vitamin D supplement (10 micrograms of vitamin D per day). This is particularly important for women:
    • With darker skin, such as those of African, Afro–Caribbean or South Asian family origin.
    • Who have limited exposure to sunlight, such as those who are housebound or confined indoors for long periods.
    • Who cover their skin for cultural reasons.
  • Give lifestyle advice including advice on nutrition and diet, and food hygiene. 
  • Give written advice on the different stages of the baby's development.
    • The Department of Health has published The Pregnancy Book, which discusses the different stages of the baby's development and most other aspects of pregnancy.
  • Give information on:
    • Antenatal classes available locally, including those run by the National Childbirth Trust.
    • Breastfeeding, including workshops available locally.
    • Maternity benefits.
    • Exercise, including pelvic floor exercises.
    • The Healthy Start Programme.
    • Options for the place of birth. For more information, see the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline on Intrapartum Care.
  • Give the woman her hand-held maternity records. The woman should take these records to each subsequent antenatal appointment (including secondary care) and any visit to a healthcare professional.
  • Discuss mental health issues. For more information, see the NICE guideline on Antenatal and Postnatal Mental Health and the CKS topic on Depression - antenatal and postnatal.

How should I manage a woman at her 16 week appointment?

  • Review and record the results of any screening tests offered at the booking appointment.
    • If the woman's haemoglobin level is below 11 g/100 mL, investigate for a possible underlying cause and treat as appropriate. For more information, see the CKS topics on Anaemia - iron deficiency, and Anaemia - B12 and folate deficiency.
    • If the woman has tested positive for an atypical red cell alloantibody (such as anti-c or anti-Kell), refer to a haematologist for advice and management.
  • Screen for pre-eclampsia by measuring blood pressure and testing for proteinuria.
    • For more information, see the CKS topic on Hypertension in pregnancy.
  • Warn the woman about the symptoms of pre-eclampsia and advise her to seek immediate advice if she develops any symptoms (including during the postpartum period).
  • Offer vaccination against pertussis (whooping cough) to provide protection to the newborn infant before routine immunization can be started at 8 weeks of age.
    • For operational reasons, it may be more practical to offer vaccination on or after the foetal anomaly scan at around 18- 20 weeks.
    • Use the combined vaccination against pertussis, tetanus, diphtheria, and polio (Boostrix-IPV®). Further information on the whooping cough vaccination programme for pregnant women is available from Public Health England. 
  • Offer vaccination against influenza if the woman has not already been vaccinated in the current influenza season, .
    • Influenza vaccination is usually carried out between October and January. Outside of these dates clinical judgement should be used to assess whether the woman should be immunized. For more information, see the CKS topic on Immunizations - seasonal influenza.
  • Discuss the fetal anomaly ultrasound scan (18–20 weeks). If the woman wishes to have this, provide specific information about the scan.

How should I manage a nulliparous woman at her 25 week appointment?

  • Screen for hypertension and possible pre-eclampsia by measuring blood pressure and testing for proteinuria.
    • For more information, see the CKS topic on Hypertension in pregnancy.
  • Warn the woman about the symptoms of pre-eclampsia and advise her to seek immediate advice if she develops any symptoms (including during the postpartum period).
  • Offer vaccination against influenza if the woman has not already been vaccinated in the current influenza season.
    • Influenza vaccination is usually carried out between October and January. Outside of these dates clinical judgement should be used to assess whether the woman should be immunized. For more information, see the CKS topic on Immunizations - seasonal influenza.
  • Measure and plot symphysis–fundal height (to identify small- or large-for-gestational-age infants).
    • Symphysis–fundal height is the distance from the lowest part of the uterus (near the pubic bone) to the highest part of the uterus, measured with a non-elastic tape measure.
    • Detailed information on how to measure and record fetal growth using the symphysis–fundal height is given on the website of the Perinatal Institute, along with examples of fetal growth charts.
    • Refer for further investigation (ultrasound biometry, amniotic fluid assessment, and/or Doppler flow) if the first fundal height measurement:
      • Is below the 10th percentile on the customized chart.
      • Is above the 90th percentile or there are other clinical concerns (for example polyhydramnios).

How should I manage a woman at 28 weeks?

  • Screen for hypertension and possible pre-eclampsia by measuring blood pressure and testing for proteinuria. For more information, see the CKS topic on Hypertension in pregnancy.
  • Warn the woman about the symptoms of pre-eclampsia and advise her to seek immediate advice if she develops any symptoms (including during the postpartum period).
    • Offer vaccination against influenza if the woman has not already been vaccinated in the current influenza season. 
      • Influenza vaccination is usually carried out between October and January. Outside of these dates clinical judgement should be used to assess whether the woman should be immunized. For more information, see the CKS topic on Immunizations - seasonal influenza.
    • Offer a second screening for anaemia and atypical red cell alloantibodies.
      • If the woman's haemoglobin level is below 10.5 g/100 mL, investigate for an underlying cause and treat if appropriate. For more information, see the CKS topics on Anaemia - iron deficiency and Anaemia - B12 and folate deficiency.
      • If the woman is rhesus D negative, offer the first anti-D treatment.
      • If the woman tests positive for an atypical red cell alloantibody (such as anti-c or anti-Kell), refer to a haematologist for advice and management.
    • Measure and plot symphysis–fundal height (to identify small- or large-for-gestational-age infants).
      • Symphysis–fundal height is the distance from the lowest part of the uterus (near the pubic bone) to the highest part of the uterus, measured with a non-elastic tape measure.
      • Detailed information on how to measure and record fetal growth using the symphysis–fundal height is given on the website of the Perinatal Institute, along with examples of fetal growth charts.
      • Refer for further investigation (ultrasound biometry, amniotic fluid assessment, and/or Doppler flow) if:
        • This is the first fundal height measurement, and it is below the 10th percentile on the customized chart.
        • This is the first fundal height measurement, and it is above the 90th percentile or there are other clinical concerns (for example polyhydramnios).
        • On the basis of consecutive measurements, growth is static (flat), or there is concern about growth being slow because it does not follow the slope of the curves on the chart.
        • On the basis of consecutive measurements, there is concern about excessive growth because of the steepness of the curve.

    How should I manage a woman at 36 weeks?

      • Breastfeeding techniques and measures to help improve the likelihood of successful breastfeeding. For more information, see the UNICEF Baby Friendly Initiative.

        • For more information, see the Guide to Newborn Blood Spot Screening produced by the Department of Health.

    How should I manage a woman with an uncomplicated pregnancy after 41 weeks?

    • Screen for hypertension and possible pre-eclampsia by measuring blood pressure and testing for proteinuria. For more information, see the CKS topic on Hypertension in pregnancy.
    • Warn the woman about the symptoms of pre-eclampsia and advise her to seek immediate advice if she develops any symptoms (including during the postpartum period).
    • Offer a membrane sweep to all women at the 41-week antenatal visit.
      • Offer additional membrane sweeps if labour does not start spontaneously.
    • Offer induction between 41 and 42 weeks, depending on the woman's preferences.
      • For more information, see the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidance on Inducing labour.

    Pre-eclampsia

    • Risk factors for pre-eclampsia include:
      • Age 40 years or older.
      • Nulliparity.
      • Pregnancy interval of more than 10 years.
      • Family history of pre-eclampsia.
      • Previous history of pre-eclampsia.
      • Body mass index 30 kg/m2 or above.
      • Pre-existing vascular disease, such as hypertension.
      • Pre-existing renal disease.
      • Multiple pregnancy.
    • Symptoms of pre-eclampsia include:
      • Severe headaches (increasing frequency and unrelieved by regular analgesics).
      • Vision problems, such as blurred vision, flashing lights, double vision, or floating spots.
      • Persistent new epigastric pain or pain in the right upper quadrant.
      • Vomiting.
      • Breathlessness.
      • Sudden swelling of the face, hands, or feet.

    [National Collaborating Centre for Women's and Children's Health, 2010b]

    Basis for recommendation

    The information on managing pregnant women at each antenatal appointment is largely based on expert opinion in the National Institute of Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [National Collaborating Centre for Women's and Children's Health, 2010a]. Further information is derived from a Department of Health (DoH) publication The Pregnancy Book [DH, 2016a].

    • Risk factors for venous thromboembolism
      • The information on risk factors for venous thromboembolism during pregnancy is based on expert opinion in a scientific review article [James, 2009].
    • Measuring height and weight
      • NICE states that it is important to document maternal height and weight once during the pregnancy (usually at the booking appointment), but it is not necessary to measure the woman's height and weight at each subsequent appointment, unless there are concerns regarding her nutrition. 
    • Anaemia
      • It is important to document and treat anaemia as low levels of haemoglobin are associated with an increased risk of poor fetal outcome.
      • In normal pregnancy, maternal plasma volume increases by 50% and red cell mass increases by 20%; therefore, the haemoglobin concentration decreases. In the UK, the normal range of haemoglobin is 11 g/100 mL or greater in pregnant women up to 12 weeks of gestation, and 10.5 g/100 mL or greater at 28 to 30 weeks of gestation. 
    • Blood group and rhesus status
      • Identifying the woman's blood group is recommended in case transfusion is required in pregnancy or at delivery.
      • Screening for rhesus D status at booking and at at the 28 week appointment is recommended because rhesus D-negative women should be offered appropriate antenatal and postnatal immunoprophylaxis, with the aim of preventing rhesus D alloimmunization in subsequent pregnancies.
    • Vaccination against pertussis (whooping cough)
      • The purpose of immunizing a pregnant woman against pertussis is to boost her antibody levels in pregnancy, so that a high level of pertussis-specific antibodies is passed to her baby across the placenta.
      • The optimal time for vaccination was previously thought to be during weeks 28 to 32 (inclusive) of pregnancy. However, recent studies have shown that reasonable levels of pertussis antibodies are present in neonates through transplacental transfer from mothers vaccinated earlier in pregnancy. As a result, the Joint Committee on Vaccination and Immunisation (JCVI) has recommended that women should be offered pertussis-containing vaccine between gestational weeks 16 and 32 to maximise the likelihood that the baby will be protected from birth.
      • Offering the vaccine from week 16 of pregnancy gives pregnant women greater opportunity to take up the offer of vaccination and will offer some protection to infants born prematurely who may be particularly vulnerable to complications from pertussis [PHE, 2016].
    • Influenza immunization
      • There is evidence that women may be at increased risk of complications (such as pneumonia if they contract influenza infection during pregnancy, and infection may be associated with preterm birth and smaller infant size at birth. Vaccination in pregnancy can also protect the neonate due to transplacental transfer of antibodies [PHE, 2015a].
    • Fetal anomaly screening
      • The information on fetal anomaly screening is based on expert opinion in the NHS guideline on the Fetal anomaly screening programme [NHS Screening Programmes, 2010] and a guideline from the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) on Temination of pregnancy for fetal abnormality [RCOG, 2010].
    • Symphysis–fundal height
      • The recommendation to use symphysis-fundal height to identify large- or small-for-gestational-age babies is based on expert opinion from the NICE guideline development group. NICE states that although they did not find good-quality evidence to support the use of symphysis–fundal height measurement, they still felt that it was appropriate to recommend that symphysis–fundal height should be measured and plotted at each antenatal appointment to detect small- or large-for-gestational-age infants.
      • The description of how to measure symphysis-fundal height is based on published expert opinion from the NHS Perinatal Institute [NHS Perinatal Institute, 2009].
      • The recommendations regarding when to refer if the symphysis–fundal height is outside the normal range are based on expert opinion from the Perinatal Institute [NHS Perinatal Institute, 2010].

    What lifestyle advice should I offer to a woman with an uncomplicated pregnancy?

    What dietary advice should I offer?

    • Advise the woman to eat a variety of foods, including:
      • Fruit and vegetables (at least 5 portions each day).
      • Carbohydrates (for example bread, pasta, rice, and potatoes).
      • Protein, such as lean meat, fish, beans, and lentils.
      • Foods rich in fibre (such as wholegrain breads, fruits, and vegetables).
      • Dairy foods, such as milk, yoghurt, and cheese.
    • Advise the woman to eat foods rich in:
      • Folic acid such as fortified breakfast cereal and yeast extract.
      • Folate such as peas, beans and orange juice.
      • Vitamin D such as oily fish, eggs, fortified breakfast cereals, and margarine.
    • Give women who qualify for the Healthy Start Scheme an application form. See the section on The healthy start scheme for more information.
    • Advise the woman that peanuts may be eaten during pregnancy.
      • Previous advice from the Department of Health advised that pregnant women may wish to avoid eating peanuts during pregnancy. This advice has now changed, because there is no clear evidence that peanuts affect the chance of the unborn baby developing a peanut allergy.
    • Advise the woman to avoid foods that may put her or her fetus at risk, including:
      • Foods that may contain Listeria such as soft mould-ripened cheeses (Camembert, Brie, and blue-veined cheese), unpasteurized milk or cheese, and paté (including vegetable paté).
        • Listeria infection in pregnancy can lead to miscarriage, stillbirth, or severe illness in the newborn baby.
      • Foods that may contain Salmonella such as uncooked or undercooked ready-prepared meals, uncooked or cured meat (including salami), and raw shellfish such as oysters.
        • Salmonella infection in pregnancy is unlikely to harm the baby but can cause serious illness in the mother.
      • Liver and liver products as these may contain high levels of vitamin A. 
        • Ingesting too much vitamin A in pregnancy can increase the risk of birth defects in the baby.
      • Fish containing relatively high levels of methylmercury, such as shark, swordfish, and marlin. 
        • Exposure to high levels of methylmercury in the womb can affect the nervous system of the fetus, potentially increasing the risk of learning or behavioural problems.
    • In addition to the above, the Food Standards Agency advises that pregnant women should limit their consumption of:
      • Tuna to no more than four medium-sized cans or two fresh tuna steaks per week — may contain high levels of mercury.
      • Caffeine to 200 mg a day — high levels of caffeine have been associated with low birth weight in the baby. Caffeine is present in coffee, tea, chocolate and colas. The amount of caffeine in food and drink varies, but as a guide:
        • One mug of instant coffee contains 100 mg of caffeine.
        • One mug of filter coffee contains 140 mg of caffeine.
        • One mug of tea contains 75 mg of caffeine.
        • One can of cola contains up to 40 mg of caffeine.
        • One can of 'energy' drink contains up to 80 mg of caffeine.
        • One bar of plain chocolate contains up to 50 mg of caffeine — the caffeine content of milk chocolate is about half that of plain chocolate.
    • For more detailed information, see The Pregnancy Book, published by the Department of Health.

    What advice can I give regarding nutritional supplements?

    • Advise the woman:
      • To take folic acid (400 micrograms daily) before conception and throughout the first 12 weeks of pregnancy. This is to reduce the risk of neural tube defects in the baby.
      • To take vitamin D (10 micrograms daily) throughout pregnancy. All pregnant and breastfeeding women are at risk of vitamin D deficiency but women at particular risk include:
        • Young women and teenagers.
        • Women of South Asian, African, Caribbean, or Middle Eastern family origin.
        • Women who have limited exposure to sunlight, such as women who are predominantly housebound or who usually remain covered when outdoors.
        • Women who eat a diet particularly low in vitamin D, such as women who consume no oily fish, eggs, cheese, or vitamin D-fortified margarine or breakfast cereal.
        • Women with a pre-pregnancy body mass index above 30 kg/m2.
      • To avoid vitamin A supplementation — an intake greater than 700 micrograms daily may be teratogenic.
      • How to obtain her vitamins if she is eligible for the Healthy Start Scheme. 
        • Eligible women can obtain vitamin tablets by taking their voucher to a local distribution point.
        • Healthy Start vitamin tablets contain: vitamin C 70 mg per tablet; vitamin D 10 micrograms per tablet; folic acid 400 micrograms per tablet.
    • Do not offer iron supplementation unless the mother has iron deficiency anaemia. For more information, see the CKS topic on Anaemia - iron deficiency.

    What advice can I give regarding exercise during pregnancy?

    • That she may continue or start moderate exercise during pregnancy. 
    • There is no evidence that a moderate course of exercise during pregnancy is associated with adverse outcomes.
    • To avoid sports that may cause abdominal trauma (for example vigorous racquet sports), falls, or excessive joint stress.
    • Scuba diving should be avoided as it has been associated with an increased risk of birth defects and fetal decompression disease.

    What advice can I give regarding use of medicines and complementary therapies during pregnancy?

    • Advise the woman to check with her GP or pharmacist before taking any:
      • Over-the-counter medicines — few medicines have been established as being safe to use in pregnancy, and the potential risks and benefits of use for both mother and baby should therefore be weighed up. 
        • Paracetamol is generally considered to be safe to use during pregnancy.
    • Complementary therapies (such as herbal remedies) — few complementary therapies have been established as being safe during pregnancy. 
      • Use of products bought via the internet should be avoided as these might not have undergone safety testing and in some cases, can contain harmful substances (such as lead).

    What advice can I give regarding alcohol, smoking, and recreational drugs during pregnancy?

    • Advise the woman to abstain from alcohol consumption during pregnancy as there are no proven safe levels of consumption for the fetus. See the CKS topic on Alcohol - problem drinking for more information, if needed.
      • Studies have shown that heavy drinking during pregnancy, usually measured as repeatedly consuming more than around five units of alcohol (2 large glasses of wine) per day, carries the greatest risk of a baby being affected with fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD), which incorporates structural birth defects as well as problems with learning and behaviour.
      • Studies of pregnant women who drank lower levels of alcohol, or who ‘binge drank’ (occasionally drank more than four to five units of alcohol in a day) have produced mixed results, with some studies showing no clear links with FASD-type problems in the baby and others showing an increased risk of problems with thinking, learning, and behaviour.
    • Offer women who smoke cessation advice and encouragement to stop.
      • Inform the woman that smoking increases the risk of miscarriage, pre-term birth, low birth weight, and stillbirth. Later in life, the child's physical growth and academic attainment may be reduced.
      • Encourage the woman women to use local NHS Stop Smoking Services or to contact the NHS pregnancy smoking helpline (0300 123 1044).
      • Consider offering nicotine replacement therapy; particularly for women who do not wish to accept the offer of help from the NHS Stop Smoking Service.
      • If the woman is unable to quit smoking during pregnancy, encourage her to reduce the amount she smokes.
      • For more detailed information, see Pregnant or breastfeeding in the CKS topic on Smoking cessation.
    • Advise women who use recreational drugs to abstain during pregnancy as the effects on the fetus are uncertain. 
      • Offer help and support to stop drug use, and refer to an appropriate support service if necessary.

    What advice can I give regarding sexual intercourse during pregnancy?

    • Reassure the woman that intercourse is thought to be safe during pregnancy.

    What advice can I give on how to avoid toxoplasmosis?

    • Toxoplasmosis is an infection with the parasite Toxoplasma gondii. 
    • If acquired during pregnancy, toxoplasmosis can cause spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, and fetal anomalies.
    • The toxoplasmosis parasite can be acquired by:
      • Eating undercooked or uncooked meat (such as salami).
      • Eating unwashed fruit or vegetables which may be contaminated by cat faeces.
      • Accidental ingestion after handling cat litter faeces.
      • Contact with lambs or sheep.
      • Mother-to-child transmission. This occurs when primary infection occurs during pregnancy (congenital toxoplasmosis) but is rare in immunocompetent mothers who have had toxoplasmosis earlier in life.
    • To reduce the risk of toxoplasmosis infection, advise the woman to:
      • Wash her hands before handling food.
      • Thoroughly wash all fruit and vegetables, including ready-prepared salads, before eating.
      • Thoroughly cook raw meats and ready-prepared chilled meals.
      • Wear gloves and thoroughly wash hands after handling soil and gardening.
      • Avoid cat faeces in cat litter or in soil.
      • Avoid lambing or milking ewes and contact with newborn lambs.

    What advice can I give regarding travel during pregnancy?

    • Air travel
      • Advise the woman that flying is not harmful for her or the baby, but some airlines will not let pregnant women fly towards the end of the pregnancy.
      • Long-haul air travel (journey time longer than 5 hours) is associated with an increased risk of venous thromboembolism. It is not clear whether there is an additional risk in pregnancy. Advise the woman to:
        • Drink plenty of water to stay hydrated.
        • Do the recommended calf exercises.
        • Consider buying a pair of support stockings over-the-counter, as this will reduce leg swelling. For more information, see the CKS topics on Deep vein thrombosis and Compression stockings.
    • Car travel 
      • Advise the woman to always wear her seatbelt with the diagonal strap across her body between her breasts and with the lap belt over her upper thighs. The straps should lie above and below the bump, and not over it.
    • Travelling abroad  
      • Advise the woman that if she is travelling abroad, she should ensure that she has:
        • Access to medical help if required.
        • Had any necessary immunizations.
        • Adequate travel insurance.
        • A European Health Insurance Card (formerly known as E111), which entitles her to some free treatment while in the European Economic Area.
    • Malaria
      • Strongly advise the woman against travelling to areas where malaria is endemic because pregnant women:
        • Are more likely to be bitten by mosquitoes.
        • Have a poorer immune response to malaria.
        • Are at greater risk of complications that can affect both mother and baby.
      • Advise a woman travelling to a malarial area to take strict bite avoidance precautions including use of DEET as an insect repellent, and to use appropriate chemoprophylaxis, as advised by a doctor. For more information, see the CKS topic on Malaria prophylaxis and the UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS) bump patient information leaflet on malaria.
    • Zika Virus
      • Zika virus is a mosquito-borne illness. Zika virus infection in pregnancy (particularly in the first trimester) has been linked to an increased risk of microcephaly (abnormally small brain and skull) and associated problems in the baby. Infection with Zika virus causes mild flu-like symptoms, a raised temperature, conjunctivitis, joint pain in the hands and feet, and a rash. However, the majority (around 3 in 4) infected people remain asymptomatic.
      • Advise women planning to travel to an area where Zika virus is circulating to consider avoiding travel until after pregnancy (Public Health England provide for up-to-date information on affected areas).
      • Advise women who cannot avoid travel to such an area to take strict precautions to avoid mosquito bites including the use of DEET as an insect repellent.
    • Women who have travelled to a Zika virus-affected area during a current pregnancy should be:
      • Tested for the infection (using blood sampling) if they are currently symptomatic. If there is any uncertainty, liaise with the local infectious diseases team.
      • Referred to a fetal medicine specialist if they previously experienced symptoms consistent with Zika virus infection or if infection is confirmed.
      • Consider referral for monthly ultrasound scans to check fetal head circumference even in the absence of reported Zika virus symptoms as the majority of infected people are asymptomatic.
    • Zika virus can be transmitted via semen. A pregnant women whose partner has travelled to a Zika-affected area should use condoms for:
      • 28 days following the man's return if he showed no symptoms of Zika virus
      • 6 months if he was symptomatic or infection was confirmed.
    • The UK Teratology Information Service has a bump patient information leaflet on Zika virus infection in pregnancy.

    What is the Healthy Start Scheme?

    • The Healthy Start Scheme is a government scheme in the UK that aims to improve the health of pregnant women and families with children aged under 4 years who have a low income or who are on benefits.
    • The Healthy Start application form must be supported by a health professional (midwife, health visitor, registered nurse, or doctor).
    • Women who are more than 10 weeks pregnant qualify for Healthy Start vouchers and vitamins if:
      • They are aged under 18 years when they apply. They do not need to be in receipt of benefits or tax credits.
      • They are aged 18 years and over and are in receipt of certain benefits or tax credits. For current criteria see the Healthy Start website.
    • Women who qualify for the scheme receive through the post:
      • A weekly voucher that may be spent in participating local shops, greengrocers, and supermarkets as payment or part payment for plain cow's milk and plain, fresh, or frozen fruit and vegetables.
      • Vitamin coupons every 8 weeks that may be exchanged for Healthy Start once daily vitamin tablets.

    What advice should I give about working and maternity benefits?

    • Advise that most women can continue working during pregnancy and that employers are required to assess risks which might be posed to the health and safety of pregnant women. If a significant risk is identified, steps to avoid the risk should be taken, such as:
      • Use of preventative or protective behaviours.
      • Altering working conditions or hours.
      • Arranging alternative work.
    • Examples of occupations where it is not advisable for the woman to continue working include occupations that require scuba diving and working with lead.
      • For more detailed information and advice, see the Health and Safety Executive website.
    • Advise the woman about her maternity rights and benefits. Pregnant women are:
      • Entitled to 26 weeks of ordinary maternity leave and 26 weeks of additional maternity leave, making 1 year in total (if they work for an employer).
        • Maternity leave may be taken no matter how long the woman has been with an employer, how many hours she works, or how much she is paid.
      • Entitled to the right to take time off work for antenatal care.
      • Entitled to free prescriptions and dental treatment during pregnancy and for 1 year after the birth.
      • Legally required to cease working after 33 weeks unless their GP or midwife informs their employer that they may continue work.
      • Not legally permitted to return to employment in the 2 weeks following childbirth (or 4 weeks if they work in a factory).
    • For the most up-to-date detailed information on maternity rights and benefits, including information on maternity pay, see the Working Families website.

    The information on lifestyle advice for pregnant women is largely based on expert opinion in the National Institute of Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline Antenatal care for uncomplicated pregnancies [National Collaborating Centre for Women's and Children's Health, 2008]. Further information is derived from a Department of Health (DoH) publication The Pregnancy Book [DH, 2016a].

    Alcohol, smoking, and recreational drugs
    • The information on the fetal effects of alcohol exposure, smoking, and recreational drug use is based on guidance from the UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS) [UKTIS, 2010a; UKTIS, 2011a; UKTIS, 2009; UKTIS, 2011b; UKTIS, 2010b].
    • In 2016, the government changed its recommendations to advise that pregnant women completely abstain from alcohol consumption [DH, 2016b].
    Zika virus
    • The information on the possible consequences of Zika virus infection in pregnancy and how to manage pregnant women with possible or confirmed exposure is based on a Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists interim clinical guideline Zika virus infection and pregnancy [RCOG, 2016].

    When are ultrasound scans offered and for what purpose?

    • During the course of an uncomplicated pregnancy, at least two ultrasound scans are usually offered:
      • A dating scan (10–13 weeks). The purpose of this scan is to:
        • Determine gestational age.
        • Detect multiple pregnancies.
        • Confirm viability.
        • Detect any gross fetal abnormalities (such as anencephaly).
        • Provide a component of screening for Down's syndrome (if requested) via measurement of nuchal translucency. 
      • A fetal anomaly scan (18–20 weeks). The purpose of this is to detect major structural anomalies, locate the placenta, and if an abnormality is detected, allow:
        • Reproductive choice (termination of pregnancy).
        • Parents to prepare for any treatment, disability, or palliative care.
        • Management of the birth in a specialist centre.
        • Intrauterine therapy such as laser therapy for a fetal tumour, or placement of a pleural or bladder shunt.
    • Additional ultrasound scans may be offered if there are concerns with the progress of the pregnancy (for example fetal growth restriction, or estimation of maximum amniotic pool depth after 42 weeks).
    • Do not routinely offer ultrasound scans:
      • For estimation of fetal size for suspected large-for-gestational-age unborn babies.
      • After 24 weeks.

    What advice can I give regarding the risks and limitations of a fetal anomaly scan?

    • Advise the parents that:
      • There are no known risks of a ultrasound scan; it is thought to be safe for the mother and baby.
      • At the fetal anomaly scan, the sonographer looks specifically for conditions in the baby that are associated with mortality (for example anencephaly), morbidity (for example serious heart defects) or require immediate postnatal support (for example spina bifida).
      • A fetal anomaly scan is not perfect, does not have a 100% detection rate, and inevitably some conditions will be missed or misidentified. 
      • In a small number of cases, babies are born with abnormalities that were not detected by the scan.
        • For more information, see Antenatal scan detection rates.
      • If a screening scan identifies a potential fetal abnormality, the woman will be referred to a fetal medicine specialist for a second opinion to confirm the diagnosis.
