Scenario: Jet lag

From age 16 years onwards.

What advice should I give people who experience sleep disturbance due to jet lag?

If a person is experiencing troublesome sleep disturbance and other complications associated with jet lag:

  • Advise on sources of information and support, such as:
    • The NHS patient leaflet Jet lag.
    • The patient information leaflet Jet lag available on the www.patient.info website.
    • The World Health Organization (WHO, website available at www.who.int) leaflet on Jet lag.
    • Diabetes UK (website available at www.diabetes.org.uk) which has an information section on Travel with advice on time zones and the implications for taking medication such as insulin.
  • For short travel stays (of two days or less), advise:
    • To consider continuing activities such as sleeping and eating on ‘home time’, to minimize disruption to the normal sleep/wake cycle.
  • For longer travel stays:
    • Maximize pre-flight sleep before travelling, to prevent the accumulation of 'sleep debt'.
    • Consider shifting bedtimes in the days before travel to be more closely in line with sleep times at the destination.
    • Adopt the destination time schedule from when boarding the flight.
  • During the flight:
    • Eat light meals, maintain hydration, and limit alcohol consumption.

    • Sleep as much as possible during the hours of darkness that will be experienced in the destination time zone, for example using eye masks and ear plugs. 

  • At the destination:
    • Try to get as much sleep as normal during a 24-hour period, with a minimum block of four hours during the local night-time period in the new time zone. 
    • Take naps in response to sleepiness.
    • Try to get timely exposure to light and darkness (scheduled sleep), and consider the use of sunglasses to help this.

      • For eastward travel over 3-8 time zones, aim for morning exposure to bright light and evening avoidance of bright light.

      • For westward travel over 3-8 time zones, aim for evening exposure to bright light and morning avoidance of bright light.

    • Take a daylight walk to help the 'internal clock' adjust.
  • Advise on measures to promote sleep including:
    • Creating a comfortable cool, dark, quiet sleeping environment, for example using blackout blinds, eye masks, ear plugs, or white noise generators.
    • Avoiding eating a heavy meal shortly before going to sleep, and limit night eating.
    • Encourage regular physical activity and exercise, but some people may have poorer sleep after exercise within four hours of planned sleep time. Other people do not seem affected by exercise even 1 hour before sleep onset.
    • Avoid the use of caffeine, nicotine, and alcohol at least three hours before planned sleep time.
    • Try to relax before going to bed, for example by having a bath, reading, or gentle stretching.
  • Advise the person to consider the use of caffeinated drinks to counteract sleepiness in the new time zone. 
  • Advise the person to minimize any stressors prior to, during, and after travel that may contribute to travel fatigue, such as changes in ambient temperature, humidity, and food quality, where possible.
  • To manage sleep disturbance at the destination:
    • Do not routinely recommend the use of short-acting hypnotic drugs to promote sleep, but they may be considered for short-term use depending on clinical judgement. See the CKS topic on Insomnia for more information.
    • Do not recommend the use of melatonin to promote sleep.

Basis for recommendation

The recommendations on the management of jet lag symptoms are largely based on the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) publication Practice parameters for the clinical evaluation and treatment of circadian rhythm sleep disorders [Morgenthaler, 2015], two Cochrane systematic reviews Melatonin for the prevention and treatment of jet lag [Herxheimer, 2002] and Caffeine for the prevention of injuries and errors in shift workers [Ker et al, 2010], a systematic review of non-pharmacological interventions for jet lag [Bin, 2019], a randomized controlled trial of light treatment for jet lag [Thompson, 2013], and expert opinion in review articles on jet lag [Weingarten, 2013; Atkinson, 2014], and pharmacological interventions for jet lag [Arendt, 2018].

Maintaining 'home time' for short stay travel
  • The recommendation to consider maintaining 'home time' for short stay travel is based on the AASM publication, which states that keeping home-based (rather than destination) sleep hours may reduce sleepiness and jet lag symptoms. It notes, however, that this regime may be associated with increased sleep disturbance and recovery time following the return flight, and may be difficult to maintain in practice [Morgenthaler, 2015].
    • It identified one crossover study that found after a 9-hour westward flight, the participants who kept their home-based sleep hours had less sleepiness and symptoms of jet lag compared with the group that adopted the destination time. However, one-third of the study population reported they would prefer to adopt the destination sleep times in order to participate fully in local activities.
  • This approach is supported by expert opinion in a review article that for short stopovers, the aim is to preserve sleep and alertness without necessarily modifying the circadian system [Arendt, 2018].
Advice for longer stay travel
  • The recommendation to maximize sleep prior to travel and minimize residual 'sleep debt' is based on the fact that sleep deprivation during travel may contribute to jet lag symptoms [Bin, 2019].
  • The recommendation to shift pre-flight bedtimes to be in line with those at the destination are based on limited evidence in review articles that sleep and light treatment on a shifting schedule pre-flight can help rapid adaptation [Arendt, 2018]. A review of non-pharmacological interventions for jet lag notes that shifting sleep schedules prior to travel is a common strategy, but found no evidence for impact on jet lag symptoms [Bin, 2019].
  • The AASM publication did not find any studies where adjusting sleep schedule was the only intervention to improve jet lag symptoms [Morgenthaler, 2015].
  • Expert opinion in a review article also recommends encouraging napping and attempting to follow the destination sleep schedule prior to travel [Weingarten, 2013].
Advice on in-flight travel strategies
  • The recommendations to maintain hydration and avoid alcohol during travel are based on expert opinion in a review article, as alcohol can increase urine output and dehydration and can impair the quality of sleep [Weingarten, 2013].
Advice on strategies at the destination
  • A review article of 13 studies (n = 392) investigating various non-pharmacological interventions for jet lag including light exposure, sleep scheduling, physical activity, and meal timing and diet changes, found no significant impact of jet lag outcomes for nine studies, mixed findings in three studies, and one observational study reported a benefit of dietary changes prior to travel (but this was not randomized and was subject to confounding factors). It concluded that there is limited evidence for the effectiveness of interventions for jet lag due to the low- to moderate-quality of included studies, with a high risk of selection bias, small number of studies, and lack of blinding [Bin, 2019].
    • Nine studies examined the effect of light exposure on jet lag symptoms. It theorized that appropriately-timed, scheduled bright light and darkness can facilitate the shift of circadian rhythms and sleep/wake cycles to a new time zone, with subsequent improvement in alertness. Only two very small studies showed a beneficial impact of light on sleep efficiency and psychomotor performance, and they were underpowered to detect statistically significant results. Additional limitations may have been inefficient timing of light exposure, lack of control over ambient light (which can counteract the light intervention), and large individual variability in circadian phase. The authors propose that appropriate light exposure and avoidance before and during the flight as well as at the destination could reduce the degree of circadian misalignment and the time required for adaptation to a new time zone.
    • Two observational studies found little evidence that physical activity before or after travel improves jet lag symptoms, but the authors highlighted possible confounding factors between the self-selected exercise and control groups, which make the results difficult to interpret.
    • It found no strong evidence that meal timing or diet changes have an impact on jet lag symptoms, but suggested it may be an area that would benefit from further study.
  • These limited findings on the impact of supplementary light treatment are supported by a small RCT (n = 22) of professional athletes which found no clinically relevant effects of light treatment for jeg lag symptoms following eastwards travel from the USA to Europe. The study also noted large differences in the perception of jet lag symptoms between study participants [Thompson, 2013].
  • The specific recommendations on scheduled light and dark exposure for east- and westwards travel and sunglasses use are based on expert opinion in a review article [Weingarten, 2013]. This is supported by another review article which recommends avoidance of light at critical times, for example by using light-blocking glasses [Arendt, 2018].
Advice on measures to promote sleep
  • The recommendations on strategies to promote sleep at the destination are largely extrapolated from studies on the effect of shift work on sleep. This approach is also supported by the expert opinion of previous external reviewers of this CKS topic.
    • The recommendations on creating an optimal sleeping environment are based on expert opinion in review articles [Wright Jr et al, 2013; McKenna, 2018].
    • The recommendations on meal timing are based on expert opinion in review articles [Kecklund, 2016; McKenna, 2018].
    • The recommendations on physical activity are based on expert opinion in review articles that regular exercise can improve sleep quantity and quality, but it may affect sleep if performed too close to anticipated sleep time [Wright Jr et al, 2013; Kecklund, 2016; Richter, 2016; McKenna, 2018].
    • The recommendations on avoiding stimulants such as caffeine and nicotine, and alcohol close to anticipated sleep time is based on expert opinion in review articles. Caffeine in particular has a peak onset of action 20–45 minutes after consumption, and its effects last 3–5 hours [Kecklund, 2016; McKenna, 2018].
    • The recommendation on a regular routine and relaxation techniques prior to anticipated sleep time is based on expert opinion in a review article [McKenna, 2018].
Advice on the use of caffeine
  • The recommendation to consider the use of caffeine to counteract sleepiness associated with jet lag is extrapolated from a Cochrane systematic review of 12 RCTs on caffeine use in shift workers which also studied the effects of caffeine in people with jet lag [Ker et al, 2010].
    • It found caffeine improved several aspects of cognitive performance, such as orientation, attention, and reasoning during jet lag, when compared with placebo.
  • This strategy is supported by the AASM publication which analyzed two small double-blind trials of moderate quality that investigated the use of slow-release caffeine compared with placebo or melatonin to counteract sleepiness due to jet lag. It found caffeine is an option to treat excessive daytime sleepiness but noted it may disrupt night-time sleep. Information was lacking on the effects of caffeine on other daytime symptoms of jet lag [Morgenthaler, 2015]. 
  • Expert opinion in review articles also suggests considering caffeine use early in the day to help counteract daytime sleepiness [Weingarten, 2013]. Caffeine improves alertness and performance, such as reaction time, vigilance, and reasoning, and is generally used to maintain daytime alertness in the new time zone [Arendt, 2018].
Managing stressors contributing to travel fatigue
  • The recommendation to reduce environmental and individual stressors before, during, and after travel to help reduce travel fatigue associated with jet lag is based on expert opinion in a systematic review of non-pharmacological interventions for jet lag [Bin, 2019].
  • In addition, expert opinion in a review article states that eating at the conventional time at the destination may help adaptation to the new time zone [Arendt, 2018].
Considering short-term hypnotic drug treatment
  • The recommendation to consider the short-term use of a hypnotic drug for sleep disturbance associated with jet lag is based on limited evidence of benefit and potential risks of treatment.
    • The ASSM publication recommends the short-term use of a hypnotic drug for the treatment of jet lag-induced sleep disturbance and insomnia based on an evaluation of nine studies (including three high-quality RCTs), but highlights the risks of adverse effects such as global amnesia, and states that the effects on daytime symptoms of jet lag disorder are unproven and have not been adequately studied [Morgenthaler, 2015].
    • Expert opinion in a review article suggests that hypnotics may provide short-term alleviation of sleep problems and may speed up adaptation to the destination time zone, but drug treatment does not address the underlying lack of synchrony between the 'internal clock' and local time cues. They may also have a role in short stopovers when adaptation to the new time zone is not wanted. The review highlights potential problems with adverse effects and risks of dependency [Arendt, 2018].
Melatonin drug treatment not recommended
  • Sleep-promoting drugs such as melatonin are licensed in the UK for short-term management of jet lag disorder [BNF 76, 2018], however CKS has not recommended their use in primary care as there is limited and conflicting evidence of benefit.
    • A Cochrane systematic review of nine randomized trials assessed the effects of melatonin on various subjective jet lag symptoms compared with placebo. In eight trials melatonin reduced subjective ratings of symptoms such as fatigue compared with placebo, if taken close to the target bedtime at the destination. It suggested that the benefit is likely to be greater the more time zones are crossed and for eastwards travel. It suggested the short-term occasional use of melatonin for the prevention and treatment of jet lag, however it highlighted the need for the correct timing of the medication, as if taken early in the day, it may cause sleepiness and delay adaptation to the new time zone, and it had an uncertain adverse effect profile [Herxheimer, 2002].
    • The AASM states that melatonin given following travel improves the duration and quality of sleep based on both subjective and objective measures, however the most effective dose of melatonin for jet lag disorder is unclear [Morgenthaler, 2015].
    • Melatonin has potential hypnotic and phase-shifting effects, however expert opinion in a review article states there are insufficient trial data to recommend its use, with some studies showing improved sleep and alertness, but others finding little effect [Arendt, 2018].
    • An additional review article pooled data from six studies which found a reduced effect size for melatonin, suggesting that melatonin may be of limited benefit in reducing jet lag symptoms [Atkinson, 2014].
